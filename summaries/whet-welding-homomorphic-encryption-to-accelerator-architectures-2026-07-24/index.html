WHET: Welding Homomorphic Encryption to Accelerator Architectures

arXiv:2606.11541 [cs.CR] · June 2026 · full view ↗

Overview and contributions

WHET ("Welding Homomorphic Encryption to accelerator architectures") argues that FHE cryptographic/algorithmic design and FHE hardware design have progressed in isolation, and that co-designing them yields large gains. The target scheme is CKKS, the leading approximate-arithmetic FHE scheme for machine learning. The authors identify that conventional constructions produce excessive working sets at the highest ciphertext levels, forcing state-of-the-art ASIC accelerators to spend nearly half their chip area on hundreds of MiB of on-chip memory, which they then still underutilize.

The central bottleneck is the coefficient-to-slot transformation (CtS), a step of bootstrapping that runs at the top levels where data objects are largest and accounts for 60.3% of bootstrapping time on a conventional accelerator configuration. WHET introduces three memory-centric algorithmic techniques plus two lightweight architectural refinements:

  • Fine-grained CtS (fg-CtS) - aggressively decompose the CtS matrix into many levels (up to 7) and abandon the baby-step giant-step (BSGS) algorithm, shrinking the per-transform working set.
  • Plaintext compression - exploit a proven repetition structure in CtS/StC plaintexts to compress them by up to $8192\times$.
  • Intermediate ModRaise - run the first CtS level at a small intermediate modulus $Q_{\mathrm{intmd}}$, saving one level and shrinking non-compressible plaintexts.
  • KeyMult buffer - a dedicated on-chip buffer for the frequently-accessed key-switching accumulators.
  • EWE instruction extensions - fused element-wise instructions that relieve data-dependency stalls.

Evaluated on SHARP8+ (an enhanced SHARP baseline), WHET achieves a 2.01× mean speedup and 2.29× mean energy-delay-product (EDP) improvement while using less on-chip memory and chip area, and delivers 1.38-8.74× higher performance per area than prior ASIC FHE accelerators. It reports the first ever sub-millisecond CKKS full-slot bootstrapping (915 microseconds for $2^{15}$ complex slots) and encrypted CNN inference of 33.1-239 ms, versus 301-1447 s on a single-threaded CPU.

Publication: arXiv:2606.11541v1 [cs.CR], 10 June 2026. Authors: Jongmin Kim, Hyesung Ji, Wonseok Choi, Hyunah Yu, Jung Ho Ahn (Seoul National University). The first two authors contributed equally.

CKKS and FHE background

CKKS operates over the polynomial ring $\mathcal{R}_Q = \mathbb{Z}_Q[X]/(X^N+1)$, with degree $N$ (typically $2^{16}$) and a huge modulus $Q$ that can reach $2^{1747}$ under 128-bit security. A client encodes a complex vector $\mathbf{u}\in\mathbb{C}^{N/2}$ into a plaintext polynomial $u=\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$ and encrypts it into a ciphertext $[\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle]$, which is a pair of polynomials $(a,b)$.

The server-side homomorphic operations on $[\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle]=(a,b)$ and $[\langle\mathbf{v}\rangle]=(a',b')$ are:

  • HAdd: $[\langle\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\rangle] \leftarrow (a+a',\, b+b')$
  • PMult (plaintext multiply, $p=\langle\mathbf{p}\rangle$): $[\langle\mathbf{p}\odot\mathbf{u}\rangle] \leftarrow (p\cdot a,\, p\cdot b)$
  • HMult: $[\langle\mathbf{u}\odot\mathbf{v}\rangle] \leftarrow (a\cdot b' + a'\cdot b,\, b\cdot b') + \mathrm{KS}(a\cdot a',\, \mathrm{evk}_\odot)$
  • HRot (rotate by $R$): $[\langle\mathbf{u}\lll R\rangle] \leftarrow (0,\, b(X^{5^R})) + \mathrm{KS}(a(X^{5^R}),\, \mathrm{evk}_R)$

Key-switching (KS) dominates CKKS computation, involving dozens of polynomial operations and requiring client-provided evaluation keys (evks), each comprising $2\beta$ polynomials (typically $\beta=4$; $\beta$ is the decomposition number, "dnum"). HMult needs $\mathrm{evk}_\odot$; each distinct rotation distance $R$ needs its own $\mathrm{evk}_R$.

RNS representation. $Q$ is handled via the residue number system: $L$ small prime sub-moduli $Q_0,\dots,Q_{L-1}$ (each $<2^{32}$) with $Q=\prod_{i=0}^{L-1} Q_i$. A polynomial $a\in\mathcal{R}_Q$ becomes an $L\times N$ matrix whose $i$-th row (a limb) is $a$ reduced modulo $Q_i$; additions and multiplications run per limb on hardware-friendly small integers.

The four polynomial operation types are: NTT (number-theoretic transform, $O(N\log N)$ FFT that turns negacyclic convolution into element-wise multiply and dominates over half of all cost), BConv (base conversion, an $L'\times L$ matrix-matrix multiply that changes the modulus), Automorphism (the coefficient permutation $a(X)\mapsto a(X^{5^R})$), and element-wise operations.

Bootstrapping (Boot) is what makes HE "fully" homomorphic. Leveled CKKS starts at a large modulus $Q_{\mathrm{top}}\simeq 2^{1400}$ and consumes levels down to $Q_{\mathrm{bot}}\simeq 2^{50}$; Boot restores the modulus/level so computation can continue. Its four sequential steps are:

(1) ModRaise  : raise modulus Q_bot -> Q_top (injects Q_bot multiples)
(2) CtS       : coefficient-to-slot, an encrypted (N/2 x N/2) linear transform
(3) EvalMod   : evaluate an approximate mod-Q_bot function (mostly HMults)
(4) StC       : slot-to-coefficient, another linear transform at lower levels

The effective level $L_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the number of usable levels remaining after Boot; a higher $L_{\mathrm{eff}}$ reduces how often Boot must run.

The working-set problem in CtS

CtS at the highest levels is the most demanding step of Boot (60.3% of Boot time on SHARP8+). The working set grows superlinearly with level, driven mainly by evk size, which reaches up to 60 MiB at the top level, while a top-level ciphertext reaches 24 MiB (Figure 1b). This forces architects to provision hundreds of MiB of on-chip memory: ARK dedicates 54.8% of its area to 588 MiB, SHARP 48.9% to ~200 MiB, and FAST 43.7% to 281 MiB. The resulting designs are memory-dominated and inflexible.

Because higher levels are reserved for CtS, EvalMod, and StC, and NTT accounts for over half the KS cost (Figure 1a), the accelerator's compute engines sit idle waiting on memory. On the ~200 MiB SHARP8+ baseline during Boot, the NTT units reach only 25.4% utilization while HBM is at 92.6% - a clear memory-bandwidth bottleneck.

Conventional CtS optimizations (baseline)

A linear transform $[\langle\mathbf{M}\cdot\mathbf{u}\rangle]$ with an unencrypted matrix $\mathbf{M}$ is evaluated via its diagonals. Extracting diagonals $\langle\mathbf{d}_0\rangle,\langle\mathbf{d}_1\rangle,\dots$ and using the identity (Equation 1):

$$\mathbf{M}\cdot\mathbf{u} \;=\; \mathbf{d}_0\odot\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{d}_1\odot(\mathbf{u}\lll 1) + \mathbf{d}_2\odot(\mathbf{u}\lll 2) + \mathbf{d}_3\odot(\mathbf{u}\lll 3)$$

For a matrix with $\#\mathrm{diag}$ non-zero diagonals this needs $(\#\mathrm{diag}-1)$ HRots and $\#\mathrm{diag}$ PMults. Three optimizations refine this:

  • Minimum key-switching (Min-KS). Instead of a separate $\mathrm{evk}_R$ for each distance, obtain $[\langle\mathbf{u}\lll 2\rangle]$ by rotating $[\langle\mathbf{u}\lll 1\rangle]$ again by 1, so the whole transform uses only $\mathrm{evk}_1$. Min-KS is essential for accelerators: without it (as BTS showed) off-chip evk traffic dominates runtime.
  • Baby-step giant-step (BSGS). Split Equation 1 into a baby step and giant step using precomputed $\mathbf{d}'_2=(\mathbf{d}_2\ggg 2)$, $\mathbf{d}'_3=(\mathbf{d}_3\ggg 2)$ (Equation 2): 
    BS: v  <- (u << 1)
GS: M.u <- (d0 (.) u + d1 (.) v) + (d'2 (.) u + d'3 (.) v) << 2
    The baby step makes $\sqrt{\#\mathrm{diag}}$ "baby" ciphertexts; the giant step accumulates $\sqrt{\#\mathrm{diag}}$ partial results. BSGS reduces the key-switch count $\#\mathrm{KS}$ from $(\#\mathrm{diag}-1)$ to $2(\sqrt{\#\mathrm{diag}}-1)$, a big win for large $\#\mathrm{diag}$.
  • CtS/StC matrix decomposition. The dense $\tfrac{N}{2}\times\tfrac{N}{2}$ transform matrix factors into $D_{\mathrm{tr}}$ sparse matrices, each with $O(\log_{D_{\mathrm{tr}}} N)$ diagonals (Figure 2). Since $\#\mathrm{KS}=O(\sqrt{\#\mathrm{diag}})$ and plaintext traffic is $O(\#\mathrm{diag})$, larger $D_{\mathrm{tr}}$ is desirable - but each factor consumes one level, so conventionally $D_{\mathrm{tr}}$ stayed at 2-4.

Capacity-aware BSGS. With $\#\mathrm{baby}=O(\sqrt{\#\mathrm{diag}})$ baby ciphertexts, efficient BSGS requires the working set (Equation 3) to fit on-chip:

$$\mathrm{WS}_{\mathrm{BSGS}} = (\#\mathrm{baby}+1)\cdot\mathrm{Size}_{\mathrm{ciphertext}} + \mathrm{Size}_{\mathrm{evk}} + \mathrm{WS}_{\mathrm{KS}}$$

SHARP restricts $\#\mathrm{baby}\le 4$ for ~200 MiB, giving $\mathrm{WS}_{\mathrm{BSGS}}\le 180\,\mathrm{MiB}+\mathrm{WS}_{\mathrm{KS}}$. The problem: this large working set leaves no room for software prefetching, so off-chip access latency is exposed, throttling hardware utilization.

WHET algorithm 1: fine-grained CtS (fg-CtS)

fg-CtS deliberately breaks the conventions above: it sets the CtS decomposition number aggressively high (up to $D_{\mathrm{CtS}}=7$) and eliminates BSGS, returning to the basic diagonal method of Equation 1. Fine decomposition limits each CtS level to at most eight non-zero diagonals, so even without BSGS each level is cheap.

The apparent downsides are a higher total KS count and two extra consumed levels versus the 4-level baseline. But in memory-bound systems, removing BSGS is equivalent to setting $\#\mathrm{baby}=1$ in Equation 3, which sharply cuts the per-transform working set. The freed on-chip memory enables aggressive software prefetching of the next CtS level, eliminating 70% of the off-chip-bandwidth NTTU idle cycles. fg-CtS also needs only one evk per level and fewer diagonal plaintexts, cutting off-chip accesses to those objects by 46.8% (from $1008+448$ MiB to $451+324$ MiB per the paper's Table 2).

The extra level consumption lowers $L_{\mathrm{eff}}$ (from 12-13 to 9-10 in the optimized parameter set), but the authors note state-of-the-art FHE-ML studies trend toward low $L_{\mathrm{eff}}$ ($\le 10$, down to 2), and $L_{\mathrm{eff}}$ can be raised by increasing $\beta$ if needed. Their parameters keep $N=2^{16}$, $Q_{\mathrm{top}}\simeq 2^{1374}$ (47 primes), $P\simeq 2^{372}$ (12 primes), $\beta=4$, and all sub-moduli $<2^{31}$ (following Cheddar), maintaining 128-bit security per the Lattice Estimator.

WHET algorithm 2: plaintext compression

Unlike ciphertexts and evks (which must look random), CtS/StC plaintexts are unencrypted and structured, so they can be compressed almost for free. The key observation, formalized as Theorem 1, is that repetition in the encoded vector produces repetition in the NTT-domain limbs.

Theorem 1. Suppose $\mathbf{u}\in\mathbb{C}^{N/2}$ satisfies $\mathbf{u}[j]=\mathbf{u}[j+S]$ for all $0\le j<\tfrac{N}{2}-S$, where $S\mid\tfrac{N}{2}$. Let $u=\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle\in\mathcal{R}_Q$ be its CKKS encoding, and $\mathbf{t}_{Q_i}=\mathrm{NTT}(u_{Q_i})\in\mathbb{Z}^{N}_{Q_i}$ each limb's NTT. Then for all $0\le j

In words: if the original vector has $S$ distinct values repeated $\tfrac{N}{2S}$ times, the encoded polynomial's limbs have $2S$ values repeated $\tfrac{N}{2S}$ times, enabling $\tfrac{N}{2S}\times$ compression. Because the decomposed CtS/StC matrices have this diagonal-repetition structure (Figure 2, rightmost matrix), most CtS/StC plaintexts are compressible - up to $8192\times$ with WHET's parameters, shrinking total plaintext off-chip traffic to just 23.5 MiB.

WPC independently observed the same property for ML weight plaintexts; WHET extends it to CtS/StC, where the level-dependent sizes make the memory impact much larger. An accelerator-aware wrinkle: storing limbs in bit-reversed order for efficient automorphisms (as in ARK) changes the repetition pattern (e.g. $[A,A,C,C,B,B,D,D]$) but preserves compressibility with appropriate hardware support.

WHET algorithm 3: intermediate ModRaise

Intermediate ModRaise redesigns conventional ModRaise to complement fg-CtS. It executes the first CtS level at a separate small intermediate modulus $Q_{\mathrm{intmd}}$ (e.g. $2^{140}$) instead of $Q_{\mathrm{top}}$ (e.g. $2^{1400}$), which (i) effectively reclaims one level consumed by fg-CtS, and (ii) shrinks the first-level CtS plaintexts - which are not compressible - by reducing their modulus, directly cutting their off-chip traffic.

Cryptographic adjustments: before Boot, WHET switches to a ternary sparse secret (Hamming weight 48) drawn from a subring of degree $N'=2^{13}$, preserving 128-bit security; the subring structure lets the first CtS level skip KS entirely (valid when $\#\mathrm{ptxt}\le N/N'$). It extends the OverModRaise idea, but rather than raising above $Q_{\mathrm{top}}$ it uses the dedicated $Q_{\mathrm{intmd}}$.

Correctness condition (Equation 4). If the first CtS level uses $\#\mathrm{ptxt}$ plaintexts with coefficients bounded by $B_{\mathrm{ptxt}}$, correctness holds when

$$Q_{\mathrm{intmd}} \;\ge\; \#\mathrm{ptxt}\cdot B_{\mathrm{ptxt}}\cdot Q_{\mathrm{bot}}\cdot N.$$

The proof (Appendix C) bounds coefficient growth: since $a,b\in\mathcal{R}_{Q_{\mathrm{bot}}}$ have coefficients bounded by $Q_{\mathrm{bot}}/2$, and rotations only permute coefficients, each output coefficient of $\sum_i p_i\cdot a(X^{5^{R_i}})$ is bounded by $\#\mathrm{ptxt}\cdot B_{\mathrm{ptxt}}\cdot \tfrac{Q_{\mathrm{bot}}}{2}\cdot N$; if $Q_{\mathrm{intmd}}$ exceeds the stated bound no overflow occurs and the modulo-$Q_{\mathrm{intmd}}$ result matches the modulo-$Q_{\mathrm{top}}$ result.

Architecture: SHARP8+ baseline and bottleneck analysis

WHET builds on a SHARP-like vector-cluster architecture (Figure 4). Each of 8 clusters has $\sqrt{N}=256$ vector lanes and functional units for NTT (NTTU, a four-step FFT datapath), BConv (BConvU, an output-stationary $2\times 6$ MAD systolic array per lane), automorphism (AutoU), and element-wise operations (EWE). The memory system has a 180 MiB main scratchpad shared by all FUs, an 18 MiB BConv buffer, a 6 MiB constant scratchpad, and two HBM3 stacks giving 1 TB/s.

SHARP8+ is an enhanced baseline reflecting recent advances: 32-bit words (vs SHARP's 36-bit) aligned with BitPacker, Cheddar's 25-30 prime system, and 8 clusters (vs SHARP's 4) with CraterLake-style equal job distribution and fixed-wire NoCs.

NTTU idle-cycle analysis (Figure 5) finds four causes: (1) off-chip memory bandwidth stalls, (2) data-dependency stalls (waiting on FU outputs), (3) main-scratchpad bandwidth stalls (ports occupied by EWE/AutoU), and (4) NTT/INTT transition bubbles. On SHARP8+, off-chip bandwidth stalls dominate; WHET's algorithmic techniques (Section 4) eliminate 70% of them, after which data dependencies and on-chip bandwidth become the new bottlenecks - motivating the two architectural refinements below.

Architecture: KeyMult buffer and EWE extensions

KeyMult buffer. Element-wise operations dominate on-chip bandwidth, and the heaviest is KeyMult - multiplying a polynomial $d$ with an evk in every KS. It is defined (Equation 5) as:

$$\mathrm{KeyMult}:\;(a,b)\leftarrow\Big(\textstyle\sum_{i=0}^{\beta-1} d\cdot\mathrm{evk}[0][i],\;\; \sum_{i=0}^{\beta-1} d\cdot\mathrm{evk}[1][i]\Big)$$

An evk has $2\beta$ polynomials; the $\mathrm{evk}[0][i]$ halves are deterministic random polynomials regenerated on-chip by a PRNG. KeyMult runs iteratively over $\beta$ steps in the EWE, accumulating into $(a,b)$. Because $(a,b)$ is accessed so often, WHET adds a dedicated 32 MiB KeyMult buffer providing extra FU ports and boosting aggregate on-chip bandwidth. To offset its cost, the main scratchpad shrinks from 180 MiB to 128 MiB - feasible precisely because fg-CtS's smaller working set (Equation 3 with $\#\mathrm{baby}=1$) now fits in 128 MiB + 32 MiB with room for prefetching. This removes most main-scratchpad bandwidth stalls but exposes data-dependency stalls.

EWE instruction extensions. Each EWE has four modular MAD (MMAD) units, but four independent MMADs would need 12 operand loads + 4 stores per lane per cycle while the main scratchpad supplies only 7. WHET adds two fused instructions that share operands, using the KeyMult buffer's extra bandwidth:

(1) d_res  <- (p . a + a')
    (a_res, b_res) <- (d_res . evk[0][i],
                       d_res . evk[1][i] + (p . b + b'))
(2) a_res  <- (C . a - a') . C'      for constants C and C'

Instruction (1) fuses PMult + HAdd + KeyMult across all four MMADs (heavily used for Equation 1 in fg-CtS); instruction (2) implements Cheddar's operation-fusion. Together they eliminate 23% of NTTU data-dependency stalls for only 0.4 mm^2 of added area.

Supporting the algorithms. The 6 MiB constant scratchpad is reused to hold compressed plaintexts, with four SRAM groups per HBM channel (32 lanes/channel), supporting $8\times$-to-$32\times$ compression at full HBM bandwidth. Intermediate ModRaise needs an RNS reconstruction over 2-3 limbs, done by extending the BConvU with reconstruction logic.

Area. Thanks to the smaller on-chip memory and minimal overheads, WHET's total die area is 190.2 mm^2, slightly below SHARP8+'s 193.1 mm^2. WHET's total SRAM is 128 (main) + 32 (KeyMult) + 18 (BConv) + 6 (constant) = 184 MiB, smaller than UFC (274), FAST (281), HAWK (212).

Evaluation setup and workloads

The authors built a cycle-granularity simulator/scheduler (8-cycle minimum granularity) using decoupled data orchestration (a la CraterLake), Belady's MIN replacement for off-chip/on-chip scheduling, and software prefetching with static stalls inserted when prefetch fails. Energy and area come from RTL synthesized in the ASAP7 7 nm predictive PDK; most logic at 1 GHz (NoCs and small buffers at 2 GHz), SRAM modeled with a heavily modified FinCACTI, two HBM3 stacks at 512 GB/s each.

Four standard workloads: Boot ($2^{15}$ complex slots), HELR (logistic-regression training, 32 iterations, batch 1024 on 14x14 images), Sorting (bitonic network over $2^{14}$ reals), and ResNet-20 (CIFAR-10 CNN inference). Baseline/optimized parameters give $L_{\mathrm{eff}}$ = 12 / 9 for Boot; HELR uses a much smaller 28x28 CtS matrix (4-level fg-CtS).

Geometric-mean improvements: $1.84\times$ delay, $1.25\times$ energy, $2.30\times$ EDP, and $2.33\times$ energy-delay-area product (EDAP). Ablation (paper Figure 7). Applied incrementally on SHARP8+, the algorithmic + parameter optimizations give $1.30$-$2.27\times$ speedups and $1.09$-$1.29\times$ energy reductions: fg-CtS alone gives $1.65\times$ faster ModRaise+CtS and $1.12\times$ faster per-$L_{\mathrm{eff}}$ Boot; plaintext compression gives $1.29$-$1.73\times$ EDP; intermediate ModRaise speeds ModRaise $3.81\times$ (8.2% overall Boot gain). Sensitivity (paper Figure 8). Doubling clusters, HBM bandwidth, or scratchpad from WHET's chosen (8, 1024 GB/s, 128 MiB) yields only $1.20\times$/$1.16\times$/$1.09\times$ gains, while halving them costs $1.67\times$/$1.65\times$/$1.99\times$ - confirming the configuration is balanced.

Broader CNN evaluation and limitations

Using the Orion FHE-ML framework (SiLU activations, Min-KS), WHET was evaluated on a wider CNN set at 128 MiB and 180 MiB scratchpad (paper Table 1). Representative results (SHARP8+ vs WHET-128MiB vs WHET-180MiB vs CPU-1-thread):

Model (dataset)          SHARP8+   WHET-128  WHET-180   CPU(Orion)  Plain
VGG-16 (CIFAR-10)         92.6ms    94.9ms    76.6ms     397 s      11.9ms
ResNet-20 (CIFAR-10)      50.8ms    33.1ms    28.8ms     301 s       2.30ms
MobileNet (Tiny ImageNet) 159 ms    98.5ms    87.0ms     892 s       3.12ms
ResNet-18 (Tiny ImageNet) 304 ms    239 ms    216 ms    1447 s      45.6ms

The default 128 MiB WHET gives $1.27$-$1.61\times$ speedups and $1.17$-$1.27\times$ energy reductions over SHARP8+. VGG-16 is the exception: Orion's implementation uses extremely large linear transforms that make BSGS unavoidable and require hundreds of baby ciphertexts, so 128 MiB becomes a bottleneck; expanding to 180 MiB restores a $1.24\times$ speedup. Overall WHET narrows the encrypted-vs-plaintext CNN gap from Orion's >30,000$\times$ (CPU) to 4.74-31.6$\times$, making real-time encrypted inference practical.

Limitations. Two HBM stacks cap capacity at ~128 GiB even with HBM4, whereas large-input CNNs (e.g. ImageNet) can materialize hundreds of GiBs of plaintexts; the authors suggest FHE-CNN implementations that reduce plaintext count. Scaling WHET requires increasing compute and bandwidth together (scale-up: more clusters + several-TB/s HBM; scale-out: multi-chip approaches like CiFHER, Cinnamon, Cerium, CROSS).

Related work spans GPU FHE (TensorFHE, WarpDrive, NEO, Cheddar), FPGA (HEAX, FAB, Poseidon, Hydra, EFFACT), PIM/NDP (Anaheim, FHENDI, CIPHERMATCH), and ASICs (F1, BTS, CraterLake, ARK, SHARP, UFC, Trinity, UniFHE, FAST, HAWK, and Intel's taped-out HERACLES). WHET's distinction is co-designing cryptographic/algorithmic constructions with the accelerator's memory system rather than treating them separately.

Algorithms

The following consolidates the paper's core procedures as self-contained pseudocode.

Algorithm 1 - Diagonal linear transform (basis of fg-CtS).

Input : ciphertext [], plaintext diagonals .. of matrix M
        (k = #diag), evk_1 for Min-KS
Output: []
1.  acc  <- PMult(, [])          # d_0 (.) u
2.  ct   <- []
3.  for r = 1 to k-1:
4.      ct  <- HRot(ct, 1)  using evk_1   # Min-KS: rotate previous by 1
5.      acc <- HAdd(acc, PMult(, ct))
6.  return acc
# fg-CtS: apply this per decomposed CtS level (D_CtS up to 7), each level
# holding at most 8 non-zero diagonals; NO baby-step giant-step is used.

Algorithm 2 - Baby-step giant-step (BSGS) linear transform (baseline, avoided by fg-CtS).

Input : [], diagonals; n1 = ceil(sqrt(#diag)) baby, n2 giant steps
Output: []
1.  baby[0] <- []
2.  for i = 1 to n1-1:  baby[i] <- HRot(baby[i-1], 1)   # Min-KS baby step
3.  acc <- 0
4.  for g = 0 to n2-1:                                  # giant step
5.      part <- 0
6.      for i = 0 to n1-1:
7.          part <- HAdd(part, PMult(rotated_diag(g,i), baby[i]))
8.      acc <- HAdd(acc, HRot(part, g * n1))            # Min-KS giant rot
9.  return acc
# #KS reduced from (#diag - 1) to 2(sqrt(#diag) - 1), but working set grows
# with n1 baby ciphertexts (Eq. 3), which is what WHET removes.

Algorithm 3 - CKKS bootstrapping with WHET's intermediate ModRaise.

Input : level-0 ciphertext [] in R^2_{Q_bot}, sparse subring secret
        (Hamming weight 48, subring degree N' = 2^13)
Output: refreshed ciphertext at high level
1.  reinterpret (a,b) in R_{Q_intmd}          # no arithmetic; choose Q_intmd
                                              #   >= #ptxt * B_ptxt * Q_bot * N
2.  # first CtS level at Q_intmd, KS skipped via subring (if #ptxt <= N/N'):
    [] <- ( sum_i p_i * a(X^{5^{R_i}}),  sum_i p_i * b(X^{5^{R_i}}) )
3.  raise modulus  Q_intmd -> Q_top
4.  run remaining fg-CtS levels (Algorithm 1 per level)   # coeff -> slot
5.  EvalMod: evaluate approximate mod-Q_bot (mostly HMults)
6.  StC: slot-to-coefficient linear transform at lower levels
7.  return refreshed ciphertext

Algorithm 4 - Plaintext compression / decompression (Theorem 1).

Compress(plaintext limbs t_{Q_i}, repetition S):
1.  # by Theorem 1, NTT-domain limbs repeat every 2S entries
2.  store only the first 2S values of each limb        # ratio = N/(2S), up to 8192x
Decompress on-chip:
3.  broadcast the 2S stored values to fill length N per limb
# Bit-reversed limb storage (for ARK-style automorphisms) changes the pattern
# to e.g. [A,A,C,C,B,B,D,D] but remains compressible with matching HW support.

Algorithm 5 - KeyMult with EWE fused instruction (Eq. 5 and extension 1).

Input : polynomial d, evk = evk[0][0..beta-1], evk[1][0..beta-1]
Output: (a, b) accumulators (kept in the 32 MiB KeyMult buffer)
1.  a <- 0 ; b <- 0
2.  for i = 0 to beta-1:                       # beta = 4
3.      a <- a + d * evk[0][i]                  # evk[0][i] from on-chip PRNG
4.      b <- b + d * evk[1][i]
5.  return (a, b)
# Fused EWE instruction (used for Eq. 1 in fg-CtS), all 4 MMADs at once:
