Homomorphic Encryption from Learning with Errors: Conceptually-Simpler, Asymptotically-Faster, Attribute-Based (GSW)

PDF, IACR ePrint 2013/340 (Gentry, Sahai, Waters; CRYPTO 2013) · full view

Motivation: a natural multiplication for LWE-based FHE

In earlier LWE-based FHE schemes (BV11b, BGV12, and Brakerski's Bra12), a ciphertext $c$ and secret key $s$ are both $n$-dimensional vectors whose dot product encodes the message: $\langle c, s\rangle \approx \mu$, equal to the message $\mu$ up to a small error removed by rounding. Homomorphic addition is easy, but multiplication is the hard part. It relies on the tensor-product identity $\langle u_1 \otimes u_2,\; v_1 \otimes v_2\rangle = \langle u_1, v_1\rangle \cdot \langle u_2, v_2\rangle$, so if $\langle c_1, s\rangle \approx \mu_1$ and $\langle c_2, s\rangle \approx \mu_2$ then $\langle c_1 \otimes c_2,\; s \otimes s\rangle \approx \mu_1 \cdot \mu_2$. Multiplying ciphertexts thus tensors them: the product $c_1 \otimes c_2$ encrypts $\mu_1\mu_2$ but under the longer key $s \otimes s$ of dimension $\Theta(n^2)$. Because tensoring blows up the ciphertext, the evaluator must relinearize: multiply the long ciphertext by a special $n \times \Theta(n^2)$ relinearization matrix to compress it back to a normal $n$-dimensional ciphertext under a normal key $s'$.

Relinearization is expensive. Each relinearization matrix has size $\Omega(n^3)$, and the public key must hold $L$ of them to support multiplicative depth $L$; relinearizing costs $\Omega(n^3)$ operations per multiply, where each operation is polynomial in $L$. These relinearization matrices form the user-specific evaluation key the evaluator must obtain. Relinearization was the central insight that enabled LWE-FHE, but it is unnatural, hard to explain intuitively, and costly. The authors ask: can homomorphic add and multiply just be $c_1 + c_2$ and $c_1 \cdot c_2$ over some ring, with the product having the same form (not a long ciphertext), and with no evaluation key at all? Their answer is yes.

Main results

  • Conceptually simpler FHE from LWE. The entire scheme is described in the paper's introduction; homomorphic add and multiply are (almost) just matrix add and multiply.
  • Asymptotically faster. Relinearization and its $\Omega(n^3)$ matrices are eliminated. Ciphertexts are matrices multiplied naturally, so sub-cubic matrix multiplication applies (Strassen at $n^{2.807}$, Williams at $n^{2.3727}$). Space complexity drops from quasi-cubic to quasi-quadratic.
  • No evaluation key. The evaluator needs only basic public parameters such as $n$, $m$, $\ell$, not any user-specific key.
  • Identity-based FHE (IBFHE). Because there is no evaluation key, the authors give a compiler turning any suitable LWE-based IBE (GPV08, ABB10a/b, CHKP10) into an IBFHE, solving a long-standing open problem.
  • Attribute-based FHE (ABFHE). The same compiler, with minor changes, compiles the Gorbunov-Vaikuntanathan-Wee (GVW13) ABE-for-circuits into an ABFHE.

The scheme keeps the usual LWE-FHE advantages: optional bootstrapping (BGV12), security from quasi-polynomial LWE factors when bootstrapping is used, no modulus switching (Bra12), and security based directly on classical $\mathrm{GapSVP}$. The authors caution that the asymptotic speedup is mainly evidence of conceptual novelty rather than a practical win: LWE-FHE remains much slower than RLWE-FHE, an RLWE version of this scheme is worse than the best RLWE schemes by log factors, and sub-cubic matrix multiplication may not beat cubic by much in practice. The concrete practical gain is space (quasi-cubic to quasi-quadratic), not time.

The approximate-eigenvector method

The core idea: the secret key is an approximate eigenvector of the ciphertext matrix, and the message is the eigenvalue. For modulus $q$ and dimension $N$ (fixed later), a ciphertext $C$ is an $N \times N$ matrix over $\mathbb{Z}_q$ with small entries (much smaller than $q$), and the secret key $v$ is an $N$-dimensional vector over $\mathbb{Z}_q$ with at least one big coefficient $v_i$. The message $\mu$ is restricted to a small integer. We say $C$ encrypts $\mu$ when $C \cdot v = \mu \cdot v + e$ for a small error vector $e$. To decrypt, extract row $C_i$, compute $x = \langle C_i, v\rangle = \mu \cdot v_i + e_i$, and round: $\mu = \lfloor x / v_i \rceil$.

The homomorphic operations now follow from linear algebra. Suppose $C_1 \cdot v = \mu_1 \cdot v + e_1$ and $C_2 \cdot v = \mu_2 \cdot v + e_2$. For addition, $C_+ = C_1 + C_2$ gives $$C_+ \cdot v = (\mu_1 + \mu_2)\cdot v + (e_1 + e_2).$$ For multiplication, $C_\times = C_1 \cdot C_2$ gives $$C_\times \cdot v = C_1\cdot(\mu_2 v + e_2) = \mu_2(\mu_1 v + e_1) + C_1 e_2 = \mu_1 \mu_2 \cdot v + \mu_2 e_1 + C_1 e_2 = \mu_1 \mu_2 \cdot v + \text{small}.$$ So the matrix sum encrypts the message sum (Equation 1) and the matrix product encrypts the message product (Equation 2), provided the final error stays small (which it does because $\mu_2$, $C_1$, $e_1$, $e_2$ are all small). Interestingly $C_2 \cdot C_1$ also encrypts $\mu_1\mu_2$, even though matrix multiplication is non-commutative: in the error-free picture where $C_i \cdot v = \mu_i \cdot v$ exactly, if $C_1$ and $C_2$ share eigenvector $v$ with eigenvalues $\mu_1, \mu_2$, then both $C_1 C_2$ and $C_2 C_1$ have eigenvector $v$ with eigenvalue $\mu_1 \mu_2$. Introducing the error $e$ (necessary to base security on LWE) makes $v$ only an approximate eigenvector, which is precisely why certain terms must be kept small.

Noise growth without flattening: somewhat-homomorphic

Call $C$ a $B$-bounded ciphertext if $\mu$ and the coefficients of $C$ and $e$ all have magnitude at most $B$. Then $C_+$ is $2B$-bounded but $C_\times$ is $(N+1)B^2$-bounded. So the error grows worse than $B^{2^L}$, doubly exponentially in multiplicative depth $L$. Phrased by degree instead of depth: evaluating a multivariate polynomial $P(x_1,\dots,x_t)$ of total degree $d$ on $B$-bounded inputs gives a $|P|(N+1)^{d-1}B^{d}$-bounded ciphertext, where $|P|$ is the $\ell_1$-norm of $P$'s coefficient vector. Taking $q$ to comfortably exceed this bound, one can roughly evaluate polynomials of degree $\log_{NB} q$. Since $q/B$ must be at most sub-exponential in $N$ for security, the scheme so far evaluates only polynomial degree, equivalently logarithmic depth. This is a somewhat-homomorphic encryption (SWHE) scheme: by far the most homomorphic LWE-based scheme using only natural operations, but not yet FHE.

The gadget and flatten technique: BitDecomp, Powersof2, Flatten, G

To obtain leveled FHE without bootstrapping or relinearization, the error must grow only singly-exponentially. The fix is flattening, built on simple bit-decomposition transformations from BV11b/BGV12/Bra12 that change a vector without affecting its dot products. Let $a, b$ be $k$-dimensional vectors over $\mathbb{Z}_q$, let $\ell = \lfloor \log_2 q\rfloor + 1$, and $N = k \cdot \ell$.

BitDecomp(a) = (a_{1,0},...,a_{1,ell-1}, ..., a_{k,0},...,a_{k,ell-1})
   where a_{i,j} is the j-th bit of a_i (least significant bit first),
   an N-dimensional 0/1 vector.

BitDecomp^{-1}(a') = ( sum_j 2^j . a_{1,j}, ..., sum_j 2^j . a_{k,j} )
   the inverse of BitDecomp, but well-defined even when the
   input a' is NOT a 0/1 vector.

Flatten(a') = BitDecomp( BitDecomp^{-1}(a') )
   an N-dimensional vector with 0/1 coefficients.

Powersof2(b) = (b_1, 2 b_1, ..., 2^{ell-1} b_1, ...,
                b_k, 2 b_k, ..., 2^{ell-1} b_k)
   an N-dimensional vector.

For a matrix A, BitDecomp(A) / BitDecomp^{-1}(A) / Flatten(A)
   apply the operation to each row of A separately.

The key dot-product identities (which hold without knowing $b$) are $\langle \mathrm{BitDecomp}(a),\, \mathrm{Powersof2}(b)\rangle = \langle a, b\rangle$, and for any $N$-dimensional $a'$, $$\langle a',\, \mathrm{Powersof2}(b)\rangle = \langle \mathrm{BitDecomp}^{-1}(a'),\, b\rangle = \langle \mathrm{Flatten}(a'),\, \mathrm{Powersof2}(b)\rangle.$$ Equation 3 shows that $\mathrm{Flatten}$ makes the coefficients of a vector or matrix small (0/1) without affecting its product with $\mathrm{Powersof2}(b)$, and without knowing $b$. The scheme sets the secret key to $v = \mathrm{Powersof2}(s)$ for a secret vector $s$; since its coefficients climb by powers of $2$ across $\lfloor \log_2 q\rfloor$ steps, $v$ has a big coefficient suitable for decryption. Then for any $N \times N$ matrix $C$ we get the crucial identity $\mathrm{Flatten}(C) \cdot v = C \cdot v$. The matrix $G = \mathrm{BitDecomp}^{-1}(I_N)$ is (the transpose of) the Micciancio-Peikert primitive (gadget) matrix.

Flattening yields leveled FHE

Call $C$ a $B$-strongly-bounded ciphertext if $\mu$ and all coefficients of $C$ have magnitude at most $1$, while the coefficients of $e$ have magnitude at most $B$. Evaluating a NAND gate as $C_3 \leftarrow I_N - C_1 \cdot C_2$ keeps the message in $\{0,1\}$ and produces error of magnitude at most $(N+1)B$; but the coefficients of $C_3$ are not small. Applying $\mathrm{Flatten}$ fixes exactly this: because $\mathrm{Flatten}(C) \cdot v = C \cdot v$, setting $C_{\mathrm{NAND}} = \mathrm{Flatten}(C_3)$ gives a ciphertext with $0/1$ coefficients that is still strongly bounded. Iterating a depth-$L$ NAND circuit keeps the error at most $(N+1)^L \cdot B$, only singly-exponential in $L$. With $q/B$ sub-exponential in $N$, the scheme can evaluate circuits of polynomial depth rather than merely polynomial degree: a leveled FHE scheme, with no relinearization and under a single fixed approximate-eigenvector secret key.

Key generation, encryption, and reduction to LWE

To base security on LWE, key generation produces an LWE instance: for parameters $q$, $n$, $m = O(n \log q)$, an LWE instance is an $m \times (n+1)$ matrix $A$ such that there is an $(n+1)$-dimensional vector $s$ with first coefficient $1$ where $e = A \cdot s$ is a small error vector. The approximate eigenvector is $v = \mathrm{Powersof2}(s)$, of dimension $N = (n+1) \cdot \ell$ with $\ell = \lfloor \log_2 q\rfloor + 1$. To encrypt $\mu \in \mathbb{Z}_q$, sample a uniform $0/1$ matrix $R \in \{0,1\}^{N \times m}$ and set $$C = \mathrm{Flatten}\big(\mu \cdot I_N + \mathrm{BitDecomp}(R \cdot A)\big) \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{N \times N}.$$ Since $\mathrm{Flatten}$ does not affect the product with $v$, the ciphertext of Equation 4 satisfies $$C \cdot v = \mu \cdot v + \mathrm{BitDecomp}(R \cdot A)\cdot v = \mu \cdot v + R \cdot A \cdot s = \mu \cdot v + \text{small},$$ since $A \cdot s = e$ is small (Equation 5).

Security. Let $C' = \mathrm{BitDecomp}^{-1}(C)$. Because $C$ is $\mathrm{Flatten}$'d, $C = \mathrm{BitDecomp}(C')$, so $C$ reveals nothing more than $C'$. And $C' = \mathrm{BitDecomp}^{-1}(\mu \cdot I_N) + R \cdot A = \mu \cdot G + R \cdot A$, where $G = \mathrm{BitDecomp}^{-1}(I_N)$ is the Micciancio-Peikert primitive matrix and the rows of $R \cdot A$ are Regev encryptions of $0$. By the leftover hash lemma with $m = O(n \log q)$ (concretely it suffices to take $m \gt 2n \log q$), the joint distribution $(A, R\cdot A)$ is statistically/computationally indistinguishable from uniform, so $C$ is independent of $\mu$. Security thus reduces directly to LWE (Lemma 1, implicit in Regev05). Security can also be based on classical $\mathrm{GapSVP}$ via a classical reduction (Pei09, BLP+13); unlike previous FHE schemes, the fresh-ciphertext error bound $B$ need not be small, so $q$ may even be exponential as long as $q/B$ is only sub-exponential.

The formal scheme: Setup, key generation, Enc, Dec, MPDec

$\mathrm{KeyGen}$ is split into $\mathrm{Setup}$, $\mathrm{SecretKeyGen}$, $\mathrm{PublicKeyGen}$. Two decryption routines are provided: $\mathrm{Dec}$ recovers a small message $\mu$ (e.g. in $\{0,1\}$), and $\mathrm{MPDec}$ is the Micciancio-Peikert procedure recovering any $\mu \in \mathbb{Z}_q$.

Setup(1^lambda, 1^L):
   Choose modulus q of kappa = kappa(lambda,L) bits, lattice
   dimension n = n(lambda,L), and error distribution chi for
   at least 2^lambda security against known attacks. Also choose
   m = m(lambda,L) = O(n log q). params = (n, q, chi, m).
   ell = floor(log q) + 1,   N = (n+1) . ell.

SecretKeyGen(params):
   Sample t <- Z_q^n.
   Output sk = s <- (1, -t_1, ..., -t_n) in Z_q^{n+1}.
   Let v = Powersof2(s).

PublicKeyGen(params, sk):
   Generate B <- Z_q^{m x n} uniformly,  e <- chi^m.
   Set b = B . t + e.
   Set A = [ b | columns of B ]   (an m x (n+1) matrix).
   Output pk = A.    (Remark: A . s = e.)

Enc(params, pk, mu):    for mu in Z_q
   Sample R in {0,1}^{N x m} uniformly.
   Output  C = Flatten( mu . I_N + BitDecomp(R . A) ) in Z_q^{N x N}.

Dec(params, sk, C):     (small message, e.g. {0,1})
   The first ell coefficients of v are 1, 2, ..., 2^{ell-1}.
   Among them pick v_i = 2^i in (q/4, q/2].
   Let C_i be row i of C. Compute x_i = <C_i, v>.
   Output mu' = round( x_i / v_i ).

MPDec(params, sk, C):   (any mu in Z_q; here q a power of 2)
   q = 2^{ell-1}; the first ell-1 coeffs of v are 1,2,...,2^{ell-2}.
   If C.v = mu.v + small, the first ell-1 coeffs of C.v are
   mu.g + small with g = (1,2,...,2^{ell-2}).
   Recover LSB(mu) from  mu . 2^{ell-2} + small;
   recover the next bit from (mu - LSB(mu)) . 2^{ell-3} + small;
   etc.  (See MP12 for the general q case.)

Correctness: if $C$ is well-formed then $C \cdot v = \mu \cdot v + R \cdot A \cdot s = \mu \cdot v + R \cdot e$. $\mathrm{Dec}$ uses only coefficient $i$, namely $x_i = \mu \cdot v_i + \langle R_i, e\rangle$, whose error has magnitude at most $\lVert e\rVert_1$. In general if $x_i = \mu \cdot v_i + e'$ with $|e'| \le q/8$ and $v_i \in (q/4, q/2]$, then $x_i/v_i$ differs from $\mu$ by less than $(q/8)/v_i \lt 1/2$, so rounding recovers $\mu$. $\mathrm{Dec}$ is simply a $\mathrm{BitDecomp}$'d version of Regev decryption applied to a single row; the extra rows of $C$ matter only for the homomorphic operations.

Homomorphic operations: MultConst, Add, Mult, NAND

All four operations end with $\mathrm{Flatten}$ so the output is strongly bounded. The earlier operations $\mathrm{BitDecomp}$, $\mathrm{BitDecomp}^{-1}$, $\mathrm{Flatten}$, $\mathrm{Powersof2}$ underlie the analysis; $\mathrm{BitDecomp}$ could alternatively decompose with respect to bases other than $2$, or by the Chinese Remainder Theorem.

MultConst(C, alpha):    multiply ciphertext C by known alpha in Z_q
   M_alpha <- Flatten(alpha . I_N)
   output Flatten(M_alpha . C)

Add(C1, C2):
   output Flatten(C1 + C2)

Mult(C1, C2):
   output Flatten(C1 . C2)

NAND(C1, C2):   for mu1, mu2 in {0,1}
   output Flatten(I_N - C1 . C2)

$\mathrm{MultConst}$. $\mathrm{MultConst}(C,\alpha)\cdot v = M_\alpha \cdot C \cdot v = M_\alpha(\mu v + e) = \alpha \mu \cdot v + M_\alpha e$, so the error grows by a factor of at most $N$ regardless of which $\alpha \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ is used. Multiplying-by-constant via repeated $\mathrm{Add}$ would instead multiply the error by at least $\alpha$. This enables native homomorphic FFT over $\mathbb{Z}_q$ without $q$-dependent error growth, unlike GHS12a/b where error growth depended on the field size.

$\mathrm{Add}$. Correctness is immediate; message addition is over the full base ring $\mathbb{Z}_q$.

$\mathrm{Mult}$. $\mathrm{Mult}(C_1,C_2)\cdot v = C_1 C_2 v = \mu_1\mu_2 \cdot v + \mu_2 e_1 + C_1 e_2$. $C_1$ contributes a factor of at most $N$ (its entries are in $\{0,1\}$), but the $\mu_2 e_1$ term means the message magnitude affects error growth, so the message space must be kept small (e.g. via Boolean NAND circuits).

$\mathrm{NAND}$. For $\mu_1, \mu_2 \in \{0,1\}$, $\mathrm{NAND}(C_1,C_2)\cdot v = (I_N - C_1 C_2)\cdot v = (1 - \mu_1\mu_2)\cdot v - \mu_2 e_1 - C_1 e_2$. This keeps the message in $\{0,1\}$ and increases the error by a factor of at most $N+1$.

Circuits. A Boolean circuit converted to NAND gates, evaluated at NAND-depth $L$ over fresh ciphertexts with error bound $B$, yields error at most $(N+1)^L \cdot B$. For arithmetic circuits over $\mathbb{Z}_q$ where all inputs are bounded by $T$ and all intermediate values stay bounded by $T'$, the final error is at most $(N+T')^L \cdot B$; this gives somewhat-homomorphic evaluation of degree-$d$ polynomials when initial messages are bounded by roughly $q^{1/d}$.

Parameters and performance

If $C_1, C_2$ are $B$-bounded encryptions of bits in $\{0,1\}$, then $\mathrm{NAND}(C_1, C_2)$ is $(N+1)B$-bounded. As long as $q/B \gt 8(N+1)^L$, a depth-$L$ NAND circuit yields a $q/8$-bounded ciphertext that $\mathrm{Dec}$ decrypts correctly. To hold $2^\lambda$ security, $n$ (hence $N$) must grow linearly with $\log(q/B)$, so $q/B$ grows like $\exp(L \log L)$. With $\kappa = \log q = O(L \log n)$ and $N = O(n\kappa) = \tilde{O}(nL)$, each NAND is dominated by one $N \times N$ matrix multiply.

Theorem 3. For dimension $n$ and depth $L$, the scheme evaluates depth-$L$ NAND circuits with $\tilde{O}((nL)^{\omega})$ field operations per gate, where $\omega \lt 2.3727$ is the matrix-multiplication exponent. Previous LWE-FHE schemes need at least $\tilde{O}(n^3 L)$ field operations per gate; moreover their field operations multiply a small number by a general $\mathbb{Z}_q$ element (cost $\tilde{O}(\kappa) = \tilde{O}(L)$), giving real complexity $\tilde{O}(n^3 L^2)$ versus this scheme's $\tilde{O}((nL)^{\omega})$. A hidden benefit: this scheme's $0/1$ ciphertext entries multiply faster than general $\mathbb{Z}_q$ matrices; a hidden cost: $N = O(nL\log n)$ hides log factors. Typically $n$ dominates $L$, since for very deep circuits one would bootstrap to make per-gate cost independent of $L$. Bootstrapping works as in prior LWE schemes because $\mathrm{Dec}$ is essentially Regev decryption (evaluable in $O(\log n)$ depth), so $q/B$ can be quasi-polynomial in $n$ and security based on quasi-polynomial LWE factors.

No evaluation key, and the IBFHE / ABFHE compiler

The decisive structural property is that homomorphic evaluation needs only public parameters (such as $n$, $m$, $\ell$), never a user-specific evaluation key. This removes the obstacle that had kept identity-based FHE open: prior FHE always required an evaluation key that cannot be computed non-interactively from a user's identity, undermining IBE's whole appeal (non-interactivity). Earlier work achieved only weak IBFHE, where the evaluator still needs a user-specific evaluation key.

The compiler turns any LWE-based IBE with three properties into an IBFHE. Property 1 (ciphertext and key vectors): the decryption key $s_{\mathrm{ID}}$ and a ciphertext $c_{\mathrm{ID}}$ for identity $\mathrm{ID}$ are vectors in $\mathbb{Z}_q^{n'}$ with $s_{\mathrm{ID}}$'s first coefficient equal to $1$. Property 2 (small dot product): if $c_{\mathrm{ID}}$ encrypts $0$ then $\langle c_{\mathrm{ID}}, s_{\mathrm{ID}}\rangle$ is small. Property 3 (security): encryptions of $0$ are indistinguishable from uniform vectors over $\mathbb{Z}_q$ under LWE.

Theorem 4 (IBE -> IBFHE compiler):
   Use E's Setup and KeyGen; supplement E's MPK with the FHE
   parameters (such as m, ell).
   N = (n+1) . ell,   ell = floor(log q) + 1.

   To encrypt mu in {0,1} under ID:
     generate N encryptions of 0 via E.Enc;
     let C'_ID be the N x (n+1) matrix whose rows are these;
     output C_ID = Flatten( mu . I_N + BitDecomp(C'_ID) ).

   Decryption: v_ID = Powersof2(s_ID); run Dec(v_ID, C_ID).
     Correctness: C_ID . v_ID
       = mu . v_ID + C'_ID . s_ID
       = mu . v_ID + small      (small by Property 2).
   Homomorphic ops: as in Section 3.3.

Semantic security follows: any adversary breaking the IBFHE distinguishes $C'_{\mathrm{ID}}$ from uniform and hence breaks LWE (Property 3). All known LWE-based IBE schemes (GPV08, ABB10a/b, CHKP10) satisfy these properties. For ABFHE the authors re-interpret decryption in the GVW13 ABE: from $s_y$ with $R(x,y)=1$ one derives a sub-key $s_{x,y}$ satisfying a Property-2-like small-dot-product condition (if $c_x$ encrypts $0$ under $x$ then $\langle c_x, s_{x,y}\rangle$ is small), so the same compiler applies, yielding the first ABFHE in which ciphertexts under a common index are processed homomorphically with no evaluation key. Full IBE/ABE details are deferred to the full version of the paper.

Significance for FHEW and TFHE

This scheme, now universally called GSW, is the conceptual foundation of the FHEW and TFHE line of fast bootstrapping schemes. Two GSW features are decisive for them. First, the asymmetric noise growth of $\mathrm{Mult}$: since $C_1$'s entries are $0/1$, the $C_1 e_2$ term contributes only a factor-$N$ blowup, so a chain of GSW multiplications grows error roughly additively rather than multiplicatively. This makes GSW the natural vehicle for the homomorphic accumulator at the heart of FHEW/TFHE bootstrapping, where many controlled GSW products are chained. Second, the gadget machinery ($G$, $\mathrm{BitDecomp}$, $\mathrm{Flatten}$, equivalently the $G$ / $G^{-1}$ decomposition) is exactly the external-product structure that TFHE generalizes to its GGSW ciphertexts and the external product (a GGSW times a GLWE giving a GLWE), the operation driving the blind-rotation step of programmable bootstrapping. The approximate-eigenvector view (eigenvalue as message) and the absence of an evaluation key carry directly into how FHEW and TFHE structure their bootstrapping keys and their CMux / blind-rotation gates.
