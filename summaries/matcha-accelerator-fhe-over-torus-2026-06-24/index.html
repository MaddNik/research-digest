MATCHA: A Fast and Energy-Efficient Accelerator for FHE over the Torus

PDF (text-layer), arXiv:2202.08814 · full view

Problem and Motivation

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) lets a cloud server compute directly on encrypted data and return encrypted results without ever learning the plaintext, which addresses both data-breach risk and privacy legislation. Among FHE schemes, FHE over the Torus (TFHE) is uniquely attractive because it supports arbitrary Boolean logic gates (NAND, XOR, XNOR, AND, OR) with unlimited computation depth, enabled by an exceptionally fast bootstrapping step of roughly 13 ms on a CPU, versus hundreds of seconds for BGV, BFV, or CKKS and about 1 s for FHEW.

Despite fast bootstrapping, TFHE gates remain extremely slow in practice. Every gate triggers a bootstrapping, so a complex TFHE circuit is sluggish: a TFHE-based RISC-V CPU made of thousands of gates runs at only about 1.25 Hz. On state-of-the-art platforms each gate still takes more than 0.2 ms, and the only prior FPGA accelerator is slower than a GPU. There was no ASIC accelerator for TFHE. The authors note that the bottleneck is the bootstrapping, and within bootstrapping the costly part is the double-precision floating-point FFT and IFFT kernels, which hurt both speed and energy efficiency.

TFHE Background

TFHE operates over the torus T (real numbers modulo 1). A plaintext bit m is encrypted as a TLWE sample (a, b) where b = a·s + e + m/2 with secret key s and small Gaussian noise e. Decryption rounds 2(b - a·s) and is correct as long as the noise stays bounded (|e| < 1/4). TGSW is the matrix extension of TLWE; an external product maps a TGSW ciphertext and a TLWE ciphertext to a TLWE ciphertext of the product of their messages.

Each logic gate performs a ciphertext addition followed by a bootstrapping that resets the noise. The bootstrapping (Algorithm 1 in the paper) consists of initialization, rounding, a BlindRotate loop of external products, sample extraction, and key switching. The dominant step is the homomorphic decryption inside BlindRotate, which computes a product of many degree-N polynomials. Naive polynomial multiplication is O(N squared); FFT and IFFT reduce it to O(N log N). In practice TFHE rescales torus elements by 2^32 and maps them to 32-bit integers, so modular reduction is implicit in native 32-bit arithmetic. However, TFHE still uses 64-bit double-precision floating-point FFT/IFFT because 32-bit integer or single-precision kernels are not accurate enough for correct decryption. Crucially, in a TFHE gate the FFT-to-IFFT invocation ratio is 1:4 (IFFT is invoked far more), and FFT/IFFT consume about 80 percent of bootstrapping latency, while bootstrapping itself is about 99 percent of total gate latency.

Key Idea 1: Approximate Multiplication-less Integer FFT/IFFT

MATCHA's first technique replaces the expensive floating-point FFT/IFFT with approximate, multiplication-less integer FFT/IFFT that use only additions and binary shifts. The insight is that TFHE uniquely tolerates these approximation errors: because a bootstrapping runs at the end of every gate, the FFT/IFFT errors are rounded off together with the noise during the bootstrapping's decryption step and never accumulate. This trick does not transfer to BGV, BFV, or CKKS, whose NTT/INTT errors would accumulate without per-operation bootstrapping and cause decryption failures.

Two structural choices implement this. First, MATCHA adopts the depth-first iterative conjugate-pair FFT (CPFFT) instead of the breadth-first Cooley-Tukey or Gentleman-Sande flows. CPFFT needs only a single complex root of unity per radix-4 butterfly, lets two butterflies in a block share a twiddle factor (halving twiddle-buffer reads), and traverses sub-transforms depth-first to capture spatial locality, avoiding bit-reversal overhead that TFHE has few chances to amortize given its 1:4 FFT/IFFT ratio. Second, MATCHA uses a lifting-structure butterfly: each lifting step is an integer-to-integer transform with a rounding operation, and its floating-point lifting coefficient is quantized to a dyadic value alpha/2^beta so multiplication becomes a few bit-shifts and adds. For example, the coefficient 9/128 = 1/2^4 + 1/2^7 is realized as 4-bit and 7-bit shifters plus an adder. Perfect reconstruction is preserved under dyadic quantization.

Key Idea 2: Aggressive Bootstrapping Key Unrolling (BKU)

The BlindRotate computes external products sequentially, one per secret-key bit. Bootstrapping Key Unrolling (BKU) processes two key bits per external product, cutting the number of homomorphic additions from n to n/2 and reducing bootstrapping latency. Because the secret key bits are binary, the two-bit term can be rewritten using a small truth table into a sum of TGSW-scaled bootstrapping-key components, encrypted as a bootstrapping key bundle (BKB). BKU can be generalized to unroll m bits per external product (Equation 1 in the paper), with m between 2 and n.

The paper finds that on a CPU, increasing m beyond 2 actually slows bootstrapping. Unrolling by two times (m = 2) cuts latency by 49 percent, but larger m suffers from (1) too few CPU cores for the many terms (m = 4 yields 15 terms), (2) cache conflicts as the bootstrapping key size grows exponentially with m, and (3) no pipelining, since the BKB construction and the external product run sequentially in the existing implementation.

MATCHA Architecture and Pipelined Datapath

MATCHA resolves the CPU limitations of aggressive BKU with a hardware pipeline that splits each bootstrapping step into two stages: constructing the bootstrapping key bundle (handled by a TGSW cluster) and the external product (handled by an EP core). A TGSW cluster has a TGSW adder tree and multiple TGSW scale units, each computing one BKB term; the EP core then computes ACC <- BKB external-product ACC. The two stages run in a pipeline so that in each time step the EP core processes the BKB produced by the TGSW cluster in the previous step, and the workload of both stages can be balanced by tuning m. The TGSW cluster and EP core use separate register banks to avoid on-chip memory conflicts.

The full chip contains a polynomial unit, eight TGSW clusters, and eight EP cores, all connected to 32 scratchpad memory (SPM) banks through crossbars, plus a memory controller driving HBM2. The polynomial unit handles additions/subtractions, bootstrapping initialization, sample extraction, and key switching. Each TGSW cluster holds 16 32-bit integer multipliers and 16 adders with two register banks (TGSW scale accesses have strong spatial locality, allowing concurrent read of one bank and write of the other). Each EP core has one FFT core and four IFFT cores (matching the 1:4 ratio), four 32-bit multipliers and adders, and eight register banks to serve the irregular FFT/IFFT access pattern. Each FFT core has an address generation unit, a twiddle-factor buffer, two FIFOs, and 128 butterfly cores, each butterfly built from two 64-bit integer adders and two 64-bit binary shifters.

Implementation, Error, and Noise Analysis

MATCHA was implemented in RTL and synthesized in a 16 nm PTM process with industry tools, using CACTI for the SPM and register-file modeling. The simple structure lets the whole design run at 2 GHz. The on-chip network uses two 8x32 bit-sliced crossbars and one 8x8 crossbar. In total MATCHA occupies 36.96 square mm and consumes 39.98 W, with 640 GB/s HBM2 bandwidth. Per the area/power table, the eight EP cores plus TGSW clusters dominate at 30.8 W and 18.06 square mm, with the SPM (4 MB, 32 banks) at 3.52 W and the memory controller plus HBM2 PHY at 14.9 square mm.

On accuracy, with 64-bit dyadic-value-quantized twiddle factors (DVQTFs) the approximate FFT/IFFT error is about -141 dB, slightly worse than the -150 dB of true double-precision FFT/IFFT because only adds and shifts are used. As m increases, noise from external products and rounding falls linearly, but noise from the larger bootstrapping keys grows exponentially, so smaller m tolerates more FFT/IFFT error. Experiments over 10^8 TFHE gates show 38-bit DVQTFs cause no decryption failure for small m, but large m (e.g., m = 5) requires the full 64-bit DVQTFs. MATCHA therefore adopts 64-bit DVQTFs throughout.

Evaluation Setup and Results

Performance was modeled at cycle level with OpenCGRA, which compiles each TFHE operation into a data-flow graph and schedules it onto an architecture description of MATCHA. Baselines were an 8-core 3.7 GHz Xeon E-2288G running the TFHE library (CPU), a 5120-core Tesla V100 with 16 GB HBM2 running cuFHE (GPU), the TFHE Vector Engine (TVE) replicated eight times on a Stratix-10 GX2800 (FPGA), and that same design synthesized in 16 nm PTM as the ASIC baseline. BKU was enabled on CPU, GPU, and MATCHA, but fixed at m = 1 on FPGA and ASIC, which do not support BKU. Standard 110-bit security parameters from the TFHE paper were used: polynomial degree N = 1024, TLWE dimension k = 1, decomposition basis Bg = 1024 and length ell = 3. Only NAND results are reported, since AND/OR/NAND/XOR/XNOR have nearly identical bootstrapping-dominated latency (NOT has no bootstrapping).

Latency: a NAND gate costs 13.1 ms on CPU, dropping to 6.67 ms at m = 2 but no further (CPU core, cache, and pipelining limits). GPU takes 0.37 ms at m = 1, improving to 0.18 ms at m = 4. MATCHA reduces NAND latency by 13 percent over GPU at m = 3; it cannot exploit m = 4 efficiently because it has only 8 TGSW clusters. FPGA and ASIC each need more than 6.8 ms at m = 1 due to no pipelining or memory optimization for BKU.

Throughput and energy: because FPGA and ASIC support only m = 1, even CPU at m = 2 beats them on gate throughput. GPU and MATCHA lead by a wide margin, and at m = 3 MATCHA improves NAND throughput by 2.3x over GPU thanks to its pipelined aggressive-BKU architecture. On throughput per Watt, the FPGA (~40 W) and ASIC (~26 W) baselines improve over CPU by 2.4x and 8.3x at m = 1, but GPU's best efficiency (m = 4) is only about 58 percent of the ASIC's because the GPU draws more than 200 W. MATCHA, at just 39.98 W, improves NAND throughput per Watt by 6.3x over the ASIC baseline.

In summary, MATCHA accelerates TFHE gates without decryption errors by combining approximate multiplication-less integer FFT/IFFT with a pipelined datapath for aggressive bootstrapping key unrolling, delivering 2.3x higher gate throughput and 6.3x higher throughput per Watt than prior accelerators.
