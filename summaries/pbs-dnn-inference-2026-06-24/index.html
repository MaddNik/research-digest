Programmable Bootstrapping Enables Efficient Homomorphic Inference of Deep Neural Networks

PDF, IACR ePrint 2021/091 · full view · verified

Overview

This paper (Chillotti, Joye, Paillier; Zama) reports the first experiments showing that deep neural networks are within reach of fully homomorphic encryption (FHE). It builds on the TFHE scheme and its key enabling technique, the programmable bootstrapping (PBS), which simultaneously refreshes a ciphertext's noise and evaluates an arbitrary function of the underlying plaintext. Crucially, and in contrast to prior work, the framework evaluates the original trained model as is: it does not require re-designing operations, altering the network topology, or re-training the model.

The authors demonstrate homomorphic inference on MNIST with networks of depth 20, 50, and 100 (NN-20, NN-50, NN-100), reaching accuracies close to the in-the-clear baseline. The library is an extended TFHE variant written in Rust and released open source as Concrete (https://github.com/zama-ai/concrete).

Motivation: FHE and noise control

Machine learning often processes sensitive data (medical, biometric, communications), and regulations such as GDPR and CCPA require its protection. Traditional encryption protects data only at rest or in transit; the data must be decrypted before processing. FHE, posed as a challenge in 1978 and first realized by Gentry in 2009, instead lets a recipient compute directly on encrypted data.

All known FHE schemes produce noisy ciphertexts, and homomorphic operations increase that noise. Once the noise exceeds a threshold the ciphertext is no longer decryptable. A scheme limited to a predetermined noise budget is termed leveled. Bootstrapping generically refreshes ciphertexts by homomorphically evaluating the decryption circuit, turning a leveled scheme into a fully homomorphic one. A fast GSW-based bootstrapping by Ducas and Micciancio (sub-second) was refined into TFHE.

TFHE operates in two modes. The leveled mode supports linear combinations and a bounded number of external products, with noise always growing; it suits small-depth circuits. The bootstrapped mode finely controls noise and, being programmable, evaluates complex non-linear functions. Large-depth circuits such as deep neural networks require the bootstrapped mode.

Preliminaries: the torus and torus polynomials

The letter T in TFHE denotes the real torus $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$, the reals modulo 1. $(\mathbb{T}, +)$ is an abelian group, but $\mathbb{T}$ is not a ring since an internal product of torus elements is undefined. An external product between integers and torus elements is well defined: for $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $t \in \mathbb{T}$, $k \bullet t \in \mathbb{T}$, giving $\mathbb{T}$ a $\mathbb{Z}$-module structure.

Polynomials are defined over the torus. With $\Phi(X)$ the $M$-th cyclotomic polynomial of degree $N$, and $M$ a power of 2, one has $N = M/2$ and $\Phi(X) = X^N + 1$. Define $\mathbb{R}_N[X] := \mathbb{R}[X]/(X^N+1)$ and $\mathbb{Z}_N[X] := \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^N+1)$, and the $\mathbb{Z}_N[X]$-module $\mathbb{T}_N[X] := \mathbb{R}_N[X]/\mathbb{Z}_N[X] = \mathbb{T}[X]/(X^N+1)$. Elements of $\mathbb{T}_N[X]$ are polynomials modulo $X^N+1$ with torus coefficients; they can be added and externally multiplied by polynomials of $\mathbb{Z}_N[X]$.

Two distributions are used: the uniform distribution $\mathcal{U}$ and the normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. A real normal distribution induces a discretized version over $\mathbb{Z}$ via $Z = \lfloor X q \rceil$. The shorthand $s \xleftarrow{\$} S$ means $s \leftarrow \mathcal{U}(S)$.

Discretized TFHE: LWE and GLWE over the torus

The LWE assumption over the torus says that $r = \sum_{j=1}^{n} s_j \bullet a_j + e \in \mathbb{T}$ is indistinguishable from a uniform torus element, even given $(a_1,\dots,a_n)$. So $r$ masks a message $\mu \in \mathbb{T}$ to form a ciphertext $c = (a_1,\dots,a_n, r+\mu) \in \mathbb{T}^{n+1}$, where $s = (s_1,\dots,s_n) \in \mathbb{B}^n$ is the secret key and $e \leftarrow \chi = \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$.

The GLWE assumption generalizes this to polynomials: the encryption of $\mu \in \mathbb{T}_N[X]$ under key $\mathfrak{s} = (\mathfrak{s}_1,\dots,\mathfrak{s}_k) \in \mathbb{B}_N[X]^k$ is $\mathfrak{c} = (\mathfrak{a}_1,\dots,\mathfrak{a}_k, \mathfrak{b}+\mu)$ with $\mathfrak{b} = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \mathfrak{s}_j \bullet \mathfrak{a}_j + \mathfrak{e}$. LWE ciphertexts are the special case $(k,N) = (n,1)$, so the presentation stays in the general GLWE setting.

In a real implementation torus elements have finite precision $\Omega$ (typically 32 or 64 bits), so $\mathbb{T}$ is replaced by the submodule $\hat{\mathbb{T}} := q^{-1}\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{T}$ with $q = 2^\Omega$, and computations are done in $\hat{\mathbb{T}}_N[X]$. Any $\mu \in \hat{\mathbb{T}}_N[X]$ can be written $\mu = \overline{\mu} \bullet \tfrac{1}{q}$ for $\overline{\mu} \in \hat{\mathbb{Z}}_N[X]$ where $\hat{\mathbb{Z}}_N[X] := (\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})[X]/(X^N+1)$.

Encoding and decoding messages

A plaintext $\overline{\mu} \in \hat{\mathbb{Z}}_N[X]$ entering encryption corresponds to a cleartext $m$ in a finite message space $\mathcal{M}$ via $\mathsf{Encode}$ and $\mathsf{Decode}$, with $\mathsf{Decode}(\mathsf{Encode}(m)) = m$. Because noise is added in the least significant positions, only the upper bits of each coefficient carry the cleartext. Some leading bits must be set to 0 as padding: let $\varpi \ge 0$ be the number of padding bits and $\omega \ge 1$ the message bit-precision, with $\varpi + \omega \le \Omega$. The message modulus is $p = 2^{\varpi + \omega}$, and coefficients take the form $\overline{\mu}_i = 2^{\Omega - (\varpi+\omega)}(\nu_i \bmod 2^\omega)$. This bit layout is shown in Figure 1.

For $x = x_H \tfrac{q}{p} \pm x_L \in \mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z}$ with $0 \le x_L \le \tfrac{q}{2p}$, the function $\mathsf{Upper}$ returns the upper part: $$\mathsf{Upper}_{q,p}(x) = \frac{q}{p}\left\lfloor \frac{p\,\mathrm{lift}(x)}{q} \right\rceil \pmod{q}$$ where $\mathrm{lift}$ lifts $\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z}$ to $\mathbb{Z}$. It extends to polynomials coefficient-wise.

Encryption scheme and leveled operations

KeyGen, Encrypt, Decrypt. KeyGen fixes $(k,N)$, an error distribution $\chi = \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$, and samples $\mathfrak{s} \xleftarrow{\$} \mathbb{B}_N[X]^k$. Encryption of $\mu \in \mathcal{M}_N[X]$ is $$\mathfrak{c} \leftarrow \mathrm{GLWE}_{\mathfrak{s}}(\mu) := (\mathfrak{a}_1,\dots,\mathfrak{a}_k, \mathfrak{b}), \quad \mathfrak{b} = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \mathfrak{s}_j \mathfrak{a}_j + \mu + \mathfrak{e} \pmod{(q, X^N+1)}.$$ Decryption computes $\mu^* = \mathfrak{b} - \sum_{j=1}^{k} \mathfrak{s}_j \mathfrak{a}_j$ and outputs $\mathsf{Upper}_{q,p}(\mu^*)$. Correctness holds provided $\lVert \mathfrak{e} \rVert_\infty < \tfrac{q}{2p}$.

Addition. GLWE is additively homomorphic: $\mathfrak{c}_1 + \mathfrak{c}_2$ encrypts $\mu_1 + \mu_2$ as long as $\mathfrak{e}_1 + \mathfrak{e}_2$ stays small.

Scalar multiplication. For $K \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$, $K \cdot \mathfrak{c}$ encrypts $K \cdot \mu$; for $K < 0$, $K \cdot \mathfrak{c} = (-K)\cdot(-\mathfrak{c})$. More generally, for $\mathcal{K} \in \mathbb{Z}_N[X]$, $\mathcal{K} \cdot \mathfrak{c}$ encrypts $\mathcal{K} \cdot \mu$, provided $\mathcal{K} \cdot \mathfrak{e}$ stays small.

External product, gadget decomposition, and the CMux gate

GLWE has no native internal multiplication. The GSW-style matrix approach supplies one. With parameters $B = 2^\beta$ and $\ell$ such that $\ell\beta \le \Omega$, define the gadget vector $\mathbf{g} = (2^{\Omega-\beta}, 2^{\Omega-2\beta}, \dots, 2^{\Omega-\ell\beta})$. A GGSW encryption of $\mathcal{k} \in \mathbb{Z}_N[X]$ is $$\mathcal{G} \leftarrow \mathrm{GGSW}_{\mathfrak{s}}(\mathcal{k}) := \mathcal{Z} + \mathcal{k} \cdot \mathbf{G}^{\mathsf{T}},$$ where $\mathcal{Z}$ stacks $(k+1)\ell$ fresh $\mathrm{GLWE}_{\mathfrak{s}}(0)$ rows and $\mathbf{G}^{\mathsf{T}} = \mathbf{I}_{k+1} \otimes \mathbf{g}^{\mathsf{T}}$ is the gadget matrix. Any $d$ can be approximated by a signed radix-$B$ expansion $g^{-1}(d) = (d_1,\dots,d_\ell)$ with error bounded by $q/(2B^\ell) = 2^{\Omega-\beta\ell-1}$, extending coefficient-wise to $G^{-1}(\cdot)$ on vectors of polynomials.

The external product $\boxdot$ between a GGSW ciphertext $\mathcal{G}_1 \leftarrow \mathrm{GGSW}_{\mathfrak{s}}(\mathcal{k}_1)$ and a GLWE ciphertext $\mathfrak{c}_2 \leftarrow \mathrm{GLWE}(\mu_2)$ is $$\mathfrak{c}_3 = \mathcal{G}_1 \boxdot \mathfrak{c}_2 := G^{-1}(\mathfrak{c}_2)\,\mathcal{G}_1,$$ which is a GLWE encryption of $\mathcal{k}_1 \mu_2$ when the noise stays small.

From this comes the CMux gate, a selector over encrypted data driven by an encrypted bit $b$ (held in a GGSW ciphertext $\mathcal{G}$). Given GLWE encryptions $\mathfrak{c}_0, \mathfrak{c}_1$ of $\mathfrak{d}_0, \mathfrak{d}_1$, it returns an encryption of $\mathfrak{d}_b$: $$\mathfrak{c}' \leftarrow \mathrm{CMux}(\mathcal{G}, \mathfrak{c}_0, \mathfrak{c}_1) := \mathcal{G} \boxdot (\mathfrak{c}_1 - \mathfrak{c}_0) + \mathfrak{c}_0.$$ The CMux gate is central to the bootstrapping's blind rotation.

Programmable bootstrapping: blind rotation

The programmable bootstrapping resets noise to a fixed level while evaluating a function on the input ciphertext. Regular bootstrapping is the special case where that function is the identity.

Intuition. For an LWE ciphertext $c = (a_1,\dots,a_n,b)$ with $b = \sum_j s_j a_j + \mu^*$ and $\mu^* = \mu + e$, decryption (without rounding) gives $-\mu^* = -b + \sum_{j=1}^{n} s_j a_j \pmod{q}$. Placing this value at the exponent of $X$ yields the monomial $X^{-\mu^*}$. A test polynomial $\mathfrak{v} = v_0 + v_1 X + \cdots + v_{N-1}X^{N-1}$ is built so each coefficient encodes the noise-free value $v_i = \mathsf{Upper}_{q,p}(\tfrac{q}{2N} i \bmod q)$. Multiplying by $X^{-\mu^*}$ rotates the value $\mu$ into the constant coefficient, as shown in Figure 2.

Because GLWE polynomials live modulo $X^N+1$, $X$ has order $2N$, so $\mu^*$ (defined mod $q$) must be rescaled to mod $2N$: instead of $-\mu^*$ one uses $-\tilde{\mu}^* = -\tilde{b} + \sum_{j=1}^{n} s_j \tilde{a}_j \pmod{2N}$ with $\tilde{b} = \lfloor 2N(b \bmod q)/q \rceil$ and $\tilde{a}_j = \lfloor 2N(a_j \bmod q)/q \rceil$. The resulting rounding error is the drift; it depends on $n$ and $N$ and shrinks with smaller $n$ or larger $N$. Setting the most significant bit of $\tilde{\mu}^*$ to 0 (i.e. $\varpi \ge 1$) limits encodable values to $N$, so the test polynomial uses $v_i = \mathsf{Upper}_{q,p}(\tfrac{q}{2N} i \bmod q)$ for $0 \le i \le N-1$.

Implementation. The rotation is performed homomorphically (a blind rotation) through a sequence of CMux gates. The accumulator is initialized to a GLWE encryption of $X^{-\tilde{b}} \cdot \mathfrak{v}$ and updated using the bootstrapping keys $\mathrm{bsk}[i] \leftarrow \mathrm{GGSW}_{\mathfrak{s}'}(s_i)$ (GGSW encryptions of the secret-key bits under a key $\mathfrak{s}'$):

Algorithm 1: Blind rotation.
ACC <- (0, ..., 0, X^(-b~) . v)
for i = 1 to n do
    ACC <- CMux(bsk[i], ACC, X^(a~_i) . ACC)
end for
return ACC

At the end of the loop, ACC holds a GLWE encryption of $X^{-\tilde{\mu}^*} \cdot \mathfrak{v}$ under key $\mathfrak{s}'$. Each iteration computes $\mathrm{ACC} \leftarrow \mathrm{CMux}(\mathrm{bsk}[i], \mathrm{ACC}, X^{\tilde{a}_i} \cdot \mathrm{ACC})$.

Sample extraction and key switching

Sample extraction extracts the constant coefficient $\mu$ of the polynomial $\mathfrak{u} := X^{-\tilde{\mu}^*}\cdot\mathfrak{v}$ from the GLWE ciphertext as an LWE ciphertext of $\mu$. It is a simple rearrangement of coefficients, with no homomorphic computation:

Algorithm 2: Sample extraction.
Parse c'  <- GLWE_s'(u) as (a'_1, ..., a'_k, b'),
  with a'_j = a'_{j,0} + ... + a'_{j,N-1} X^{N-1},
       b'  = b'_0   + ... + b'_{N-1}   X^{N-1}.

Output the LWE ciphertext
  c' := ( a'_{1,0}, -a'_{1,N-1}, ..., -a'_{1,1},
          ...,
          a'_{k,0}, -a'_{k,N-1}, ..., -a'_{k,1},
          b'_0 )  in (Z/qZ)^(kN+1)

under key s' = (s'_1, ..., s'_kN) in B^(kN),
  where s'_{l+1+(j-1)N} := s'_{j,l}
        for 1 <= j <= k and 0 <= l <= N-1.

Key switching converts an LWE ciphertext from one key/parameter set to another, closing the bootstrapping loop. It needs key-switching keys $\mathrm{ksk}[i,j] \leftarrow \mathrm{LWE}_s(s'_i \cdot \tfrac{q}{B^j})$ for a gadget decomposition with $B := B_{KS}$ and $\ell := \ell_{KS}$:

Algorithm 3: Key switching.
Input:  c' <- LWE_s'(mu) = (a'_1, ..., a'_kN, b') in (Z/qZ)^(kN+1)
        under key s' = (s_1, ..., s_kN) in B^(kN).
Keys:   ksk[i,j] <- LWE_s( s'_i . q / B^j )
          for 1 <= i <= kN, 1 <= j <= l_KS.

Compute the gadget decomposition
  (a'_{i,1}, ..., a'_{i,l_KS}) = g^{-1}(a'_i)

Output:
  c'' := (0, ..., 0, b')
         - sum_{i=1}^{kN} sum_{j=1}^{l_KS} a'_{i,j} . ksk[i,j]
         in (Z/qZ)^(n+1)

This is an LWE encryption of mu under key s,
provided the resulting noise keeps small.

After sample extraction the output LWE ciphertext uses key $\mathfrak{s}'$ and format $(\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^{kN+1}$; key switching returns it to the original key $s$ and format $(\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^{n+1}$, so the bootstrapping can be iterated.

Look-up table evaluation (the programmable part)

The same blind rotation that refreshes a ciphertext can simultaneously evaluate an arbitrary function $f: \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{I}$, encoded as a look-up table inside the test polynomial. Given encoders $\mathsf{Encode}: \mathcal{D} \to \mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathsf{Encode}': \mathcal{I} \to \mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z}$ and matching decoders, the table entries are $$T[i] = \mathsf{Encode}' \circ f \circ \mathsf{Decode} \circ \mathsf{Upper}_{q,p}\!\left(\tfrac{q}{2N} i \bmod q\right), \quad 0 \le i \le N-1.$$ Programming the test polynomial as $\mathfrak{v} = v_0 + \cdots + v_{N-1}X^{N-1}$ with $v_i = T[i]$, and otherwise running the procedure unchanged, transforms an input ciphertext of $\mu$ (encoding $x \in \mathcal{D}$) into a low-noise ciphertext encoding $f(x)$, up to the drift. This is the programmable bootstrapping.

LUT evaluation via programmable bootstrapping.
Given f : D -> I, Encode/Decode (for D) and Encode'/Decode' (for I):

1. Build the look-up table:
   for i = 0 to N-1:
       T[i] = Encode' o f o Decode o Upper_{q,p}( (q / 2N) i  mod q )

2. Program the test polynomial:
   v = v_0 + v_1 X + ... + v_{N-1} X^{N-1},  with v_i = T[i]

3. Run Algorithm 1 (blind rotation) with this v,
   then sample extraction (Algorithm 2) and key switching (Algorithm 3).

Result: a low-noise ciphertext encoding f(x).

Remark. The padding requirement $\varpi \ge 1$ of regular bootstrapping can be lifted when the table is negacyclic, i.e. $T[i+N] \equiv -T[i] \pmod{2N}$; then $2N$ values can be programmed.

Application to neural networks

Neural-network layers split into two classes over encrypted data: those evaluable with leveled operations, and those needing one or more programmable bootstrappings (PBS).

Layers without PBS (leveled). The framework keeps weights, biases, and filters in the clear, so these are constant multiplications and additions:

  • Dense / linear layer: dot product of inputs with a clear weight matrix plus an optional bias; only the activation (next class) needs a PBS.
  • Convolution layer: convolution with clear filters plus optional biases; same as dense apart from activation.
  • Addition layer: component-wise additions, all leveled.
  • Flatten layer: reshapes inputs; merely rearranges ciphertexts, no homomorphic operation.
  • Global average pooling: reduces to the sum $\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i$; the division by $n$ is folded into the next PBS (e.g. by dividing the next layer's weights by $n$), so only ciphertext addition is needed.

Layers with PBS.

  • Activation layer (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh, ...): any activation is evaluated by a single PBS with the function encoded in the test polynomial's look-up table.
  • Max-pooling layer: using $\max(x,y) = y + \mathrm{ReLU}(x-y)$, the two-argument max costs one PBS (one ReLU plus additions/subtractions). For $k$ arguments, applying $\max(x_1,\dots,x_k) = \max(\max(x_1,\dots,x_{k-1}), x_k)$ costs $k-1$ PBS.

Leveled layers may also trigger a bootstrap on intermediate values whenever noise exceeds the threshold.

Experimental results and benchmarks

Experiments target the MNIST dataset (28x28 handwritten digits) with networks NN-20, NN-50, NN-100 (depths 20, 50, 100), each hidden layer holding at least 92 active neurons and mixing dense and convolution layers with activations. The word size is $\Omega = 64$ bits. Targeted security levels are 80 and 128 bits, with parameter sets I, II, III validated using the lwe-estimator. The sets support inference at a precision of 8 up to 12 bits. The cryptographic parameters $(k, N, \sigma)$ for GLWE and $(n, \sigma)$ for LWE are listed in Figure 3.

Three machines are used: a PC (2.6 GHz 6-core Intel Core i7), an AWS instance (3.00 GHz Intel Xeon Platinum 8275CL, 96 vCPUs), and AWS2 (the same with 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs). Figure 4 gives in-the-clear run-times and accuracy (measured with ONNX Runtime) as a baseline; Figure 5 gives the run-time and accuracy over encrypted data across parameter sets and machines.

Key takeaways: encrypted-data accuracy stays close to the clear baseline (e.g. NN-20 reaches 97.5 percent encrypted vs 97.5 percent clear; NN-50 up to 95.1 percent; NN-100 up to 91.1 percent at 80-bit security). For a fixed security level, a larger $N$ raises accuracy at the cost of more processing. GPU acceleration (AWS2) cuts run-times sharply (e.g. NN-20 set II: 30.04 s on PC vs 2.10 s on AWS2). All reported times are for a single independent inference and are not amortized over a batch.

Conclusion

The paper presents a general FHE framework for evaluating deep neural networks that scales efficiently with depth while preserving good accuracy, by combining flexible message encodings with programmable bootstrapping. The authors report new records for homomorphic inference of deep networks, achieved without re-training the model. They flag specialized hardware (potentially several orders of magnitude faster) and the extension to homomorphic training as future directions. The implementation, an extended TFHE variant in Rust, is released as the open-source Concrete library.
