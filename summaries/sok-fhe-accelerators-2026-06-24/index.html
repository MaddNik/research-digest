SoK: Fully Homomorphic Encryption Accelerators

arXiv preprint (arXiv:2212.01713v4, 31 Jan 2024) · December 2022 · full view ↗

Overview and scope

This systematization-of-knowledge (SoK) paper by Zhang, Cheng, Yang, Hu, Liu and Chen (iSINGLab @ HKUST / Clustar / Fuzhou University) is the first systematic study dedicated specifically to FHE accelerators. Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) lets arbitrary computation run directly over encrypted data without decryption, which makes it attractive for privacy-preserving cloud computing, machine learning, and database search. Its fundamental drawback is inefficiency: FHE-enabled computation is orders of magnitude slower than plaintext computation. Beyond algorithm-level optimizations (modern schemes such as BGV, BFV and CKKS support SIMD-style batching), the authors focus on the complementary direction of hardware acceleration.

The paper surveys 14 accelerators in chronological order, makes qualitative observations about their evolution, then provides a quantitative testbed comparison of the open-source ones plus reported numbers from the closed-source ones. It closes with future directions. The 14 accelerators are cuHE/cuFHE, nuFHE, HEAT, HEAX, HEXL, HEXL-FPGA, $100\times$, F1, CraterLake, BTS, ARK, Poseidon, FAB and TensorFHE. A reproducibility Docker image is provided. Prior SoKs cover FHE libraries and compilers, but not accelerators.

FHE background: schemes and operations

FHE schemes split into word-wise FHE (CKKS, BFV, BGV) which supports algebraic, SIMD-batched operations on word/message-encrypted data but is poor at non-polynomial functions like sigmoid/relu, and bit-wise FHE (TFHE, FHEW) which operates over boolean circuits and evaluates non-polynomial functions via lookup tables, at the cost of weaker SIMD support and heavier ciphertext inflation (more memory-bandwidth pressure). Real applications often use both together. The paper focuses on RLWE-based word-wise schemes (BFV/BGV/CKKS) and the bit-wise TFHE.

All ciphertexts and keys are polynomials, so the underlying operations are polynomial operations. A polynomial of degree-bound $n$ is written $A(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} a_j x^j$. Polynomials are stored in coefficient representation; multiplication is $O(n^2)$ there but $O(n)$ in point-value representation, with FFT/NTT converting between them in $O(n\log n)$. FFT works on complex numbers; RLWE schemes need the NTT (a generalization of FFT over finite fields). To avoid slow large-integer arithmetic, RNS decomposition splits the moduli $Q = \prod_{i=0}^{L} q_i$ and $P = \prod_{i=0}^{\alpha-1} p_i$ into products of smaller coprime moduli via the Chinese Remainder Theorem, reducing coefficient bit width at the cost of more polynomials. Since modular-multiplication cost is roughly proportional to the square of the coefficient bit width, the overall complexity drops. In word-wise FHE the value $L$ is also the multiplicative depth of a fresh ciphertext, since the moduli $q_0, q_1, \ldots, q_L$ are gradually consumed by homomorphic multiplications. Encryption, decryption, and homomorphic evaluation

Encoding packs a vector of messages into a plaintext polynomial $m$ (this packing is what lets BGV/BFV support SIMD-style operations); decoding recovers messages. In RLWE-based FHE the public key is an RLWE instance $\mathrm{pk} = (\mathrm{pk}_0, \mathrm{pk}_1) = (as + e, a)$ generated from the secret key $s$, where the coefficients of $a$ are uniform on $(-Q/2, Q/2]$ and the noise $e$ is sampled from a discrete Gaussian. Encryption obfuscates the plaintext with the public key and noise:

$$ct = (c_0, c_1) = v \cdot \mathrm{pk} + (\Delta \cdot m + e_1,\; e_0) \bmod Q$$

where $\Delta$ is a scaling factor in some schemes, $v$ is a random polynomial with small coefficients, and $e_0, e_1$ are noise polynomials (Equation 1). Decryption recovers the plaintext by removing $a \cdot s$; all schemes share the same core operation

$$c_0 - c_1 \cdot s$$

shown in Equation 2. For word-wise FHE, the major homomorphic evaluations are multiplication, addition, subtraction and permutation, which suffice for most applications; non-polynomial functions are reached by polynomial approximation. Bit-wise FHE instead evaluates boolean gates (NOT, AND, NAND, OR, XOR) exactly. However, homomorphic evaluation creates two problems: multiplication and permutation produce ciphertexts that cannot be decrypted directly, and noise grows (especially in multiplication) until it threatens correct decryption. Three ciphertext-maintenance operations fix this.

Ciphertext maintenance: key-switching, modulus switching, bootstrapping

  • Key-switching homomorphically switches the secret key of a ciphertext while preserving its plaintext, using a pre-generated switching key $\mathrm{swk}$ (a ciphertext of $P \cdot s'$ with modulus $P \cdot Q$, where $P$ controls noise scale). It is invoked after every homomorphic multiplication and permutation, so it is used intensively.
  • Modulus switching raises or lowers the ciphertext modulus $Q$ to control the noise proportion. Each multiplication consumes modulus, so $Q$ bounds the number of consecutive multiplications: the multiplicative depth $L$ (leveled FHE). Applications are described as deep (large depth, e.g. deep neural nets) or shallow (few multiplications, e.g. database lookup).
  • Bootstrapping refreshes a ciphertext to recover multiplicative depth. In bit-wise TFHE it runs on every gate (depth is not tracked), dominated by homomorphic LUT evaluation built from MUX gates (polynomial add/sub/mul). In word-wise FHE it is far more complex, run only when depth runs low, and follows four steps: (1) Modulus Switching raise $Q$ to a much larger $Q' \gg Q$; (2) CoeffToSlot homomorphic encoding / linear transformation to move plaintext coefficients into plaintext slots; (3) Homomorphic Evaluation of a non-polynomial modulo (digit extraction in BGV/BFV, modulus reduction in CKKS) approximated by Taylor or Chebyshev high-degree polynomials; (4) SlotToCoeff, the inverse of CoeffToSlot.

Per ARK, NTT plus fast basis conversion in key-switching take over 80% of compute time; per CraterLake, bootstrapping can take over 90% of an end-to-end FHE task (and itself contains key-switching).

Why FFT/NTT is hard to accelerate (with the butterfly math)

The root cause of FHE inefficiency is two-fold: (1) operations are built on polynomials, whose multiplication is intrinsically much costlier than integer/float arithmetic; and (2) the two ciphertext-maintenance operations (key-switching and bootstrapping) involve very complicated computation. The shared building block of all accelerators is the FFT/NTT module, and accelerating it faces three recurring challenges: high computation complexity, intensive memory access, and limited generality.

High computation complexity. Even at $O(n\log n)$, NTT/FFT can reach about 70% of runtime. It is built from butterfly operations. The Cooley-Tukey (CT) butterfly computes a pair of outputs from a pair of inputs by reusing one multiplication:

$$A_k = A_k^{[0]} + \omega_n^k A_k^{[1]}, \qquad A_{k+n/2} = A_k^{[0]} - \omega_n^k A_k^{[1]}$$

shown in Equation 3, where $\omega_n^k$ is a twiddle factor. The Gentlemen-Sande (GS) butterfly is its reverse:

$$a_k^{[0]} = a_k + a_{k+n/2}, \qquad a_k^{[1]} = (a_k - a_{k+n/2}) \cdot \omega_n^k$$

shown in Equation 4. Butterflies are arranged into $\log n$ stages of $n/2$ butterflies each, with a strict cross-stage dependency chain, as the $n=8$ workflow in Figure 2 illustrates (the figure also shows the two butterfly diagrams). Naive data parallelism fails because of those dependencies; naive pipeline parallelism would need $(n\log n)/2$ separate butterflies, infeasible on FPGA and very expensive on ASIC. Practical answers are the 4-step FFT/NTT algorithm (partial parallelism) and inter-polynomial parallelism (rPLP).

Intensive memory access. Memory bandwidth, not compute, is often the bottleneck. Example: a 1 GHz chip with $40960$ modular multipliers on HBM3 ($3$ TB/s) finishes CoeffToSlot multiplications ($n = 2^{16}$, $L = 23$, $\mathrm{dnum} = 4$) in $0.18$ ms but spends $2.1$ ms ($11.7\times$) just loading data. The cause is data inflation: NTT needs one group of twiddle factors per RNS modulus, the 4-step algorithm adds $n$ twisting factors per modulus, and stage temporaries plus read/write conflicts add more pressure.

Limited generality. Polynomial degree $n$ varies with security level and depth. There are $(n\log n)/2$ butterflies in a full pipeline, so a fixed pipeline either underutilizes resources (when $n$ is too small) or cannot fully pipeline (when $n$ is too large), and different $n$ means different data dependencies that static circuits cannot satisfy.

DFT/NTT formulas and the 4-step algorithm

The transforms behind the butterflies are the Discrete Fourier Transform and its finite-field analogue. Choosing the sample points to be the $n$-th roots of unity $\omega_n^k = e^{2\pi i k / n}$ turns the $O(n^2)$ evaluation into the DFT

$$A_k = A(\omega_n^k) = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} a_j \, \omega_n^{kj}, \qquad k \in [0, n)$$

shown in Equation 5. Using the root-of-unity identities $\omega_{2n}^{2k} = \omega_n^k$ and $\omega_n^{k + n/2} = -\omega_n^k$, the polynomial splits into even and odd parts $A^{[0]}$ and $A^{[1]}$, giving the divide-and-conquer recursion that the CT butterfly implements (Equation 3 above) and reducing complexity to $O(n\log n)$. The NTT replaces complex roots with the primitive $n$-th root of unity $g_n$ modulo a prime $p$:

$$A_k = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} a_j \, g_n^{kj} \bmod p, \qquad k \in [0, n)$$

shown in Equation 6, with the analogous identities $g_{2n}^{2k} \equiv g_n^k \pmod p$ and $g_n^{k+n/2} \equiv -g_n^k \pmod p$; the same butterfly scheme applies with modular arithmetic.

The 4-step FFT/NTT algorithm makes a single transform partly parallel. Writing $n = R \cdot C$ and treating the $n$ inputs as an $R \times C$ matrix, the steps are:

4-step FFT/NTT (n = R x C)

1. Transpose the R x C input into a C x R matrix; perform FFT on each
   row (C independent R-point FFTs). Call the result A'.
2. Transpose A' into an R x C matrix A''.
3. Build the R x C twisting-factor matrix F = [F_ij], F_ij = omega_n^(i*j);
   dyadic-multiply A'' by F to get A'''.
4. Perform FFT on each row of A''' (R independent C-point FFTs);
   transpose to obtain the final C x R result A.

The result equals the $n$-point transform up to a reordering that does not matter for element-wise polynomial add/mul, so the transposes in steps 1 and 4 can be skipped via column-major storage. With $R, C \approx \sqrt{n}$ (e.g. $R = C = 256$ at $n = 65536$), the per-step parallelism is high; only the global transpose in step 2 retains a global dependency, handled by a reusable transpose network. The trade-off is more pre-computed parameters: the algorithm cuts the number of twiddle factors but adds $n$ twisting factors per RNS modulus, so recent designs generate both on the fly rather than caching them.

Accelerating key-switching and bootstrapping

Accelerating key-switching. The main challenge is generality. State-of-the-art designs use generalized key-switching parameterized by $\mathrm{dnum} \in [1, L+1]$; the $L+1$ moduli are grouped into $\mathrm{dnum}$ blocks with per-block product

$$Q_j = \prod_{i=j\alpha}^{(j+1)\alpha-1} q_i, \qquad j \in [0, \mathrm{dnum}), \quad \alpha = \left\lfloor (L+1)/\mathrm{dnum} \right\rfloor$$

shown in Equation 7. Each block does fast basis conversion (mostly mul/add), with NTT dominating the cross-block accumulation. As $\mathrm{dnum}$ grows from $1$ to $L+1$, NTT workload and memory grow while basis-conversion workload shrinks, so no single resource split is optimal for all parameters. Designers must also pick a parallelism scheme: rPLP (residue-polynomial-level: one processor per residue polynomial; extra data exchange in basis conversion) versus CLP (coefficient-level: many processors per polynomial, the $n$ coefficients distributed across processors; global communication in NTT). Small $\mathrm{dnum}$ favors CLP.

Accelerating bootstrapping. Hardest of all, for two reasons. (1) Large consumption of multiplicative depth: in CraterLake's LSTM benchmark, bootstrapping uses 61% of max depth (35 of 57 levels); supporting it forces larger $L$, hence larger $Q$ and $n$, hence bigger ciphertexts/keys and more memory. (2) Enormous homomorphic permutations: CoeffToSlot/SlotToCoeff need many permutations (about 30 for a 4096-message ciphertext), and each rotation step needs a distinct switching key (about $\mathrm{dnum} \times$ ciphertext size). At $n = 2^{15}$, 40 distinct switching keys may need precomputing for the linear transform.

Accelerator landscape: GPU then FPGA then ASIC

Accelerators evolved from general hardware to specialized silicon. Early designs exploited features of existing hardware: Intel HEXL uses AVX-512 on CPUs; nuFHE and $100\times$ use CUDA on GPUs. To go further, FPGAs build dedicated circuits for NTT/iNTT and key-switching, but suffer from limited programmable resources and low clock frequency. ASICs then push the highest performance (roughly $1000\times$ NTT throughput over FPGA and $100\times$ over GPU) at very high cost (a 12nm/14nm tape-out runs into millions of dollars and is usually closed-source).

The common building block is the FFT/NTT module. Notably, most accelerators implement only one of FFT or NTT (FFT uses floating point, NTT only integers; different circuits), so each supports only the schemes that use its chosen transform. Only nuFHE offers both. Early works used fixed NTT units and workflows; later works adopt programmable NTT units and data paths (often via the 4-step algorithm) plus large customized on-chip memory, which pushed designers toward ASIC.

Survey of individual accelerators (approach and gains)

GPU-based. cuHE/cuFHE (Dai et al., 2015/2018) provide CUDA NTT/CRT; cuFHE accelerates TFHE but only supports NTT (FFT could be faster for TFHE) with fixed parameters. nuFHE (NuCypher, 2018) offers both FFT and NTT for TFHE with Python APIs; FFT beats cuFHE, but generality is still limited. $100\times$ (Jung et al., 2021) accelerates CKKS on a V100 GPU with hierarchical/4-step NTT split into two kernels, coalesced memory access, on-the-fly twiddle generation, and kernel fusion, reaching over $100\times$ over single-thread CPU; end-to-end it is still main-memory-bandwidth bound because GPUs lack large on-chip memory. TensorFHE (Fan et al., 2023) recasts NTT as batched general matrix multiplications to run on GPU Tensor Cores (V100/A100); claims to beat CPU/GPU/FPGA designs and match F1 in some cases thanks to modern process nodes, but still hits GPU architectural limits.

CPU-based. HEXL (Intel, 2021) uses AVX-512/AVX512IFMA for single-core NTT/iNTT and dyadic multiplication; integrated into PALISADE, SEAL and HElib; thread-safe but degrades with many threads (heat/TDP throttling) and slow memory.

FPGA-based. HEAT (Roy et al., 2019) targets BFV on a heterogeneous ARM-FPGA (Xilinx ZCU102, later AWS f1); ARM cores orchestrate FPGA coprocessors for add/sub/mul/modulus-switching/NTT, composing higher-level ops like key-switching. Limited BRAM/URAM/DSP forces just two CT butterfly units per NTT core (reused) and degree only $4096$, hurting generality. HEAX (Microsoft, 2020) accelerates CKKS with tunable NTT and multiplication cores and a dedicated key-switching module pipeline; closed-source, and adapting to new parameters requires physically reconfiguring the FPGA. HEXL-FPGA (Intel, 2021) is an HLS implementation of NTT/iNTT, multiplication and key-switching, compiled per-function to bitstreams and integrable with HEXL; HLS leads to redundant resource use and suboptimal performance, and it supports only a limited parameter range. Poseidon (Yang et al., 2023) is the first FPGA accelerator supporting bootstrapping, on a modern U280 FPGA with HBM, using radix-based NTT and optimized automorphism to improve resource efficiency. FAB (Agrawal et al., 2023) is also a bootstrapping-capable U280/HBM FPGA design that optimizes on-chip memory access around the bootstrapping workflow.

ASIC-based. F1 (Feldmann et al., 2021) is the first programmable FHE ASIC: 16 compute clusters of pipelined, 128-element-vectorized functional units (NTT, modular mul/add, automorphism), 4-step NTT, a 64 MB SRAM scratchpad over 16 banks, and three $16 \times 16$ crossbars. Weaknesses: heavy reliance on an under-described compiler, a fixed key-switching algorithm ($\mathrm{dnum} = L+1$, slow at high depth), and only non-packed bootstrapping (max degree $16384$, too small for fully packed bootstrapping at 80/128-bit security), so it is poor on deep workloads. CraterLake (Samardzic et al., 2022) is F1's successor targeting unbounded-depth multiplication via fully packed bootstrapping; logically one 2048-element processor (physically eight 256-element groups), max degree $65536$ and depth $60$. It adds two FUs over F1, Change-RNS-base (CRB) for fast basis conversion at small $\mathrm{dnum}$ and an on-the-fly switching-key generator (KSHGen), and switches from rPLP to CLP (no crossbar needed, depth-independent parallelism). The CRB unit takes 34% of on-chip area, so at large $\mathrm{dnum}$ it is underutilized and CraterLake even trails F1 on shallow tasks. BTS (Kim et al., 2022) follows $100\times$ with goals like CraterLake: a GPU-like $64 \times 32$ 2D array of 2048 PEs, each with a basis-conversion unit and a single 2-point butterfly, connected by vertical/horizontal crossbars; it does, for example, a $131072$-point NTT as a $64$-point per-PE step, then a $64$-point per-row step, then a $32$-point per-column step (generalized 4-step), with local per-PE scratchpads on HBM. Drawback: the 2D array cannot pipeline multiple NTTs. ARK (Kim et al., 2022) uses four CraterLake-like vectorized clusters and is the first to co-design the bootstrapping algorithm with hardware: multi-hop homomorphic rotation and on-the-fly residue extension shrink switching keys and plaintexts. It measures NTT at 54.8% and basis conversion at 34.2% of bootstrapping work, so it uses hybrid parallelism (CLP for basis conversion, rPLP for NTT and others) with a switching mechanism for key-switching.

Software support and evolution trends

Integrating diverse accelerators (each with its own data structures and workflows) into upper-layer FHE software is hard. The OpenFHE project is highlighted: it adds a Hardware Abstraction Layer (HAL) of C++ abstract classes for NTT/iNTT/vector ops and lattice/RNS subroutines so CPU/GPU/FPGA/ASIC backends can plug in (HEXL already has a backend), bridging applications and accelerators.

The authors distill four evolution trends. (1) From general hardware to ASIC: CPU/GPU have fixed architectures and small on-chip memory; FPGA has limited resources and low frequency; ASIC allows custom architecture (crossbars, HBM plus SRAM) but costs millions and is usually closed-source. (2) Software/hardware co-design: early designs ran single instructions with no scheduling and suffered generality and software-hardware interaction overhead; recent designs split resources into independently scheduled clusters with flexible software, though they do not yet leverage general FHE compilers. (3) Enhanced programmability: the 4-step NTT improves both parallelism and the ability to handle varying $n$, but key-switching generality is still unsolved, and deep-focused designs are slow on shallow tasks (CraterLake slower than F1 when $L$ is between 4 and 8). (4) Unlimited depth via bootstrapping: CraterLake, BTS and ARK converge on supporting bootstrapping, favoring 4-step NTT and generalized key-switching, dedicating area to global transpose and fast basis conversion, reducing memory cost via on-the-fly generation, and adopting CLP as the primary parallelism scheme.

Quantitative evaluation: NTT and key-switching

Testbed: an Intel Xeon Gold 5115 (2.40 GHz, AVX-512 FMA), 128 GB RAM, Ubuntu 18.04; Intel PAC D5005 with a Stratix 10 GX FPGA for HEXL-FPGA; NVIDIA V100 (32 GB) for GPU designs; all in Docker. Open-source designs (HEXL, HEXL-FPGA, $100\times$, cuFHE, nuFHE) were run on the testbed; HEAX, F1 and TensorFHE numbers are taken from their papers. SEAL with no accelerator is the baseline (No Acc).

For CraterLake and BTS, bootstrapping speedup rises with SRAM then plateaus once SRAM is large enough that no further data exchange is needed.

Open challenges and future directions

The authors propose five directions.

  • Application-driven design. Rather than always chasing faster, costlier silicon, co-design the whole stack with the application. Examples (Table 3): INSPIRE co-designs storage with the accelerator for private set intersection; Cheetah and FxHENN co-design HE-based CNN inference workflows.
  • Support both word-wise and bit-wise FHE on one accelerator, with efficient ciphertext switching between the two (e.g. CKKS and FHEW), since real ML needs both polynomial and non-polynomial evaluation and switching is currently very slow.
  • Enhanced software/hardware co-design. Co-design accelerator compilers with general FHE compilers such as EVA, integrating a hardware-aware cost model for better end-to-end performance.
  • From scale-up to scale-out. Connect multiple accelerators over the network; co-design FHE acceleration with high-performance networking on-chip to handle data inflation across nodes.
  • Accelerating NTRU-based schemes. Largely uncovered because few exist and older NTRU schemes were seen as insecure, but modern NTRU variants are low-memory and fast. Table 4 shows GPU and ASIC speedups on AES/Prince homomorphic evaluation versus a CPU baseline; NTRU accelerators currently reach about 3 orders of magnitude (ASIC), lower than the about 5 orders for CKKS/BFV/TFHE accelerators, because NTRU is already faster (less room) and current NTRU ASICs are simpler.

Conclusion. Across 14 accelerators the paper identifies four trends: increasing use of advanced hardware, widespread software/hardware co-design, enhanced programmability, and improved bootstrapping for unlimited-depth operation. It calls for NTRU-based accelerators and scale-out (rather than only scale-up) methodologies to guide future FHE accelerator design.
