SoK: Fully Homomorphic Encryption Accelerators

arXiv preprint (arXiv:2212.01713v4, 31 Jan 2024) · December 2022 · full view ↗

Overview and scope

This systematization-of-knowledge (SoK) paper by Junxue Zhang, Xiaodian Cheng, Liu Yang, Jinbin Hu, Ximeng Liu and Kai Chen (iSINGLab @ HKUST / Clustar / Fuzhou University) is the first systematic study dedicated specifically to FHE accelerators. Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) lets arbitrary computation run directly over encrypted data without decryption, which makes it attractive for privacy-preserving cloud computing, machine learning, and database search. Its fundamental drawback is inefficiency: FHE-enabled computation is orders of magnitude slower than plaintext computation, which restricts deployment in performance-critical systems.

The authors trace the root cause of FHE's inefficiency to two factors: (1) FHE operations are built on polynomials, whose multiplication is intrinsically far costlier than integer/floating-point arithmetic, and (2) two ciphertext-maintenance operations (key-switching and bootstrapping) involve extremely complicated computation. Beyond algorithm-level optimizations (modern schemes such as BGV, BFV and CKKS support SIMD-style batching), the paper focuses on the complementary direction of hardware acceleration - the two directions are complementary and combinable.

The paper surveys 14 accelerators in chronological order, makes qualitative observations about their evolution to establish a connection among them, then provides a quantitative testbed comparison of the open-source ones plus reported numbers from the closed-source ones, and closes with future directions. The 14 accelerators are cuHE/cuFHE, nuFHE, HEAT, HEAX, HEXL, HEXL-FPGA, $100\times$, F1, CraterLake, BTS, ARK, Poseidon, FAB and TensorFHE. A reproducibility Docker image is provided (hpfilter/sok-fhe-accelerator). Prior SoKs cover FHE libraries and compilers, but not accelerators.

FHE background: schemes and operations

FHE schemes split into word-wise FHE (CKKS, BFV, BGV) which supports algebraic, SIMD-batched operations on word/message-encrypted data but is poor at non-polynomial functions like sigmoid/ReLU, and bit-wise FHE (TFHE, FHEW) which operates over boolean circuits and evaluates non-polynomial functions exactly via lookup tables, at the cost of weaker SIMD support and heavier ciphertext inflation (more memory-bandwidth pressure). Real applications often use both together. The paper focuses on RLWE-based word-wise schemes (BFV/BGV/CKKS) and the bit-wise TFHE.

All ciphertexts and keys are polynomials, so the underlying operations are polynomial operations. A polynomial of degree-bound $n$ (the paper uses degree to mean degree-bound throughout) is written

$$A(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} a_j x^j$$

shown in Equation 1. Polynomials are stored in coefficient representation; multiplication is $O(n^2)$ there but $O(n)$ in point-value representation, with FFT/NTT converting between them in $O(n \log n)$. FFT works on complex numbers; RLWE schemes also need the NTT (a generalization of FFT over finite fields).

To avoid slow large-integer arithmetic, RNS decomposition splits the moduli $Q = \prod_{i=0}^{L} q_i$ (for ciphertext) and $P = \prod_{i=0}^{\alpha-1} p_i$ (special modulus for keys) into products of smaller coprime moduli via the Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT), replacing each large-coefficient polynomial with several small-coefficient ones. Since modular-multiplication cost is roughly proportional to the square of the coefficient bit width, the overall complexity drops. In word-wise FHE the value $L$ is also the multiplicative depth of a fresh ciphertext, since the moduli $q_0, q_1, \ldots, q_L$ are gradually consumed by homomorphic multiplications, and $L$ usually determines the maximum number of multiplications supported. Figure 1 maps the operation families (encryption/decryption/encoding/decoding, homomorphic evaluation, and ciphertext maintenance) onto their underlying polynomial computations and the three core challenges (high computation complexity, intensive memory access, limited generality).

Encryption, decryption, and homomorphic evaluation

Encoding packs a vector of messages into a plaintext polynomial $m$ (this packing is what lets BGV/BFV support SIMD-style operations); decoding recovers messages. In RLWE-based FHE the public key is an RLWE instance $\mathrm{pk} = (\mathrm{pk}_0, \mathrm{pk}_1) = (as + e,\, a)$ generated from the secret key $s$, where the coefficients of the random polynomial $a$ are uniform on $(-Q/2,\, Q/2]$ and the noise $e$ is independently sampled from a discrete Gaussian. Encryption obfuscates the plaintext with the public key and noise:

$$\mathrm{ct} = (c_0, c_1) = v \cdot \mathrm{pk} + (\Delta \cdot m + e_1,\; e_0) \bmod Q$$

shown in Equation 2, where $m$ is the encoding result, $\Delta$ is a scaling factor in some schemes (to control precision), $v$ is a random polynomial with small coefficients, and $e_0, e_1$ are noise polynomials. Decryption recovers the plaintext by removing $a \cdot s$; although workflows differ, all schemes share the same core decryption operation $c_0 - c_1 \cdot s$.

For word-wise FHE the major homomorphic evaluations are multiplication, addition, subtraction and permutation, which suffice for most applications; non-polynomial functions are reached by polynomial approximation. Bit-wise FHE instead evaluates boolean gates (NOT, AND, NAND, OR, XOR) exactly. However, homomorphic evaluation creates two problems: (1) multiplication and permutation produce special ciphertexts that cannot be directly decrypted or used as inputs to subsequent operations, and (2) noise grows (especially in multiplication) until it exceeds a threshold and threatens correct decryption. Three ciphertext-maintenance operations - key-switching, modulus switching and bootstrapping - fix these.

Ciphertext maintenance: key-switching, modulus switching, bootstrapping

  • Key-switching homomorphically switches the secret key of a ciphertext while preserving its plaintext: $\mathrm{ct}' = \mathrm{Keyswitch}(\mathrm{ct}, \mathrm{swk})$ converts a ciphertext decryptable under $s'$ into one decryptable under $s$. The switching key $\mathrm{swk}$ is a pre-generated public key, essentially a ciphertext of $P \cdot s'$ with modulus $P \cdot Q$, where the integer $P$ controls the scale of noise. It is invoked after every homomorphic multiplication and permutation, so it is used intensively.
  • Modulus switching raises or lowers the ciphertext modulus $Q$ to control the proportion of noise. Each multiplication consumes modulus, so the size of $Q$ bounds the number of consecutive homomorphic operations on a fresh ciphertext: the maximum multiplicative depth (budget) $L$. Such schemes are called leveled FHE and are efficient for shallow computations; the paper uses deep (large depth, e.g. deep neural nets) versus shallow (few multiplications, e.g. database lookup) to describe applications. When $Q$ becomes too small, bootstrapping is used to refresh the ciphertext and recover depth.
  • Bootstrapping homomorphically recrypts the old ciphertext to produce a fresh one. In bit-wise TFHE it runs on every bit-wise operation (so depth is not tracked), and its most time-consuming part is homomorphic LUT evaluation, implemented through many multiplexer (MUX) gates that mainly consist of polynomial add/sub/mul. In word-wise FHE it is far more complex, run only when current depth is insufficient, and follows four common steps (detailed below).

Word-wise bootstrapping, four steps. (1) Modulus Switching: given $\mathrm{ct}$ under modulus $Q$, generate a new ciphertext encrypting the same plaintext while raising the modulus to $Q' \gg Q$. (2) CoeffToSlot (homomorphic encoding / linear transformation): because the decryption formula no longer holds (plaintext coefficients may exceed the plaintext modulus after modulus switching) and FHE can only operate on plaintext slots rather than polynomial coefficients, this step moves the coefficients of the plaintext polynomial into the plaintext slots. (3) Homomorphic Evaluation of a non-polynomial modulo of the coefficients - digit extraction in BGV/BFV, modulus reduction in CKKS - approximated by high-degree polynomials via Taylor expansion or the optimized Chebyshev method. (4) SlotToCoeff (inverse linear transformation): the inverse of CoeffToSlot, placing the results back into the polynomial coefficients.

Per ARK, NTT plus fast basis conversion in key-switching take more than 80% of compute time; per CraterLake, bootstrapping can take over 90% of an end-to-end FHE task (and bootstrapping itself contains key-switching operations).

Why FFT/NTT is hard to accelerate (with the butterfly math)

The shared building block of all accelerators is the FFT/NTT module, and accelerating it faces three recurring challenges: high computation complexity, intensive memory access, and limited generality.

High computation complexity. Even at $O(n \log n)$, NTT/FFT can reach about 70% of runtime. It is built from butterfly operations. The Cooley-Tukey (CT) butterfly (Figure 2, left) computes a pair of outputs from a pair of inputs while reusing one multiplication:

$$A_k = A_k^{[0]} + \omega_n^k A_k^{[1]}, \qquad A_{k+n/2} = A_k^{[0]} - \omega_n^k A_k^{[1]}$$

shown in Equation 3, where $\omega_n^k$ is a twiddle factor. The Gentlemen-Sande (GS) butterfly (Figure 2, middle) exploits a similar structure and acts as the reverse operation:

$$a_k^{[0]} = a_k + a_{k+n/2}, \qquad a_k^{[1]} = (a_k - a_{k+n/2}) \cdot \omega_n^k$$

shown in Equation 4. Butterflies are arranged into $\log n$ stages of $n/2$ butterflies each, with a strict cross-stage dependency chain that continuously changes as the calculation proceeds, as the $n = 8$ workflow with CT butterflies in Figure 2 (right) illustrates. Naive data parallelism fails because of those dependencies; naive pipeline parallelism would need $(n \log n)/2$ separate butterflies, which is impossible to place on FPGA and extremely expensive on ASIC (and the data movement is itself nearly infeasible). The two practical answers are the 4-step FFT/NTT algorithm (partial parallelism) and inter-polynomial parallelism via residue-polynomial-level parallelism (rPLP).

Intensive memory access. Memory bandwidth, not compute, is often the bottleneck even on FPGA/ASIC. Worked example: a 1 GHz chip with $40960$ on-chip modular multipliers connected to HBM3 ($3$ TB/s) finishes the CoeffToSlot multiplications (with bootstrappable parameters $n = 2^{16}$, $L = 23$, $\mathrm{dnum} = 4$) in $0.18$ ms but spends $2.1$ ms ($11.7\times$ the compute time) just loading the data. The root cause is data inflation: NTT needs one group of twiddle factors per RNS modulus (unlike FFT, where there are fewer twiddle factors than input coefficients), the 4-step algorithm adds $n$ twisting factors per modulus, and per-stage temporaries plus read/write conflicts on the same memory block add further pressure.

Limited generality. Polynomial degree $n$ varies with security level and depth. There are $(n \log n)/2$ butterflies in a full pipeline, so a fixed-$n$ pipeline either fails to fully pipeline (when applied to a larger $n$, for lack of multipliers) or wastes computing resources in redundant stages (when applied to a smaller $n$), and different $n$ implies different data dependencies that static circuit connections cannot satisfy.

DFT/NTT formulas and the 4-step algorithm

The transforms behind the butterflies are the Discrete Fourier Transform and its finite-field analogue (Appendix A of the paper). Choosing the sample points to be the $n$-th roots of unity, $\omega_n^k = e^{2\pi i k / n}$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$ (powers of $\omega_n = e^{2\pi i / n}$), turns the $O(n^2)$ evaluation into the DFT:

$$A_k = A(\omega_n^k) = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} a_j \, \omega_n^{kj}, \qquad k \in [0, n)$$

shown in Equation 5. The roots satisfy the identities $\omega_{2n}^{2k} = \omega_n^k$ and $\omega_n^{k + n/2} = -\omega_n^k$. Splitting $A$ into its even and odd parts, $A^{[0]}$ and $A^{[1]}$ with $A(x) = A^{[0]}(x^2) + x A^{[1]}(x^2)$, and applying those identities gives, for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n/2 - 1$, the divide-and-conquer pair

$$A(\omega_n^k) = A^{[0]}\!\left(\omega_{n/2}^{k}\right) + \omega_n^k A^{[1]}\!\left(\omega_{n/2}^{k}\right), \qquad A\!\left(\omega_n^{k+n/2}\right) = A^{[0]}\!\left(\omega_{n/2}^{k}\right) - \omega_n^k A^{[1]}\!\left(\omega_{n/2}^{k}\right)$$

shown in Equation 6, which is exactly what the CT butterfly implements (Equation 3) and which the master theorem reduces to $O(n \log n)$. The NTT replaces the complex roots with the primitive $n$-th root of unity $g_n$ modulo a prime $p$:

$$A_k = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} a_j \, g_n^{kj} \bmod p, \qquad k \in [0, n)$$

shown in Equation 7, with the analogous identities $g_{2n}^{2k} \equiv g_n^k \pmod p$ and $g_n^{k+n/2} \equiv -g_n^k \pmod p$; the same butterfly scheme applies with modular multiplication and modular addition/subtraction.

The 4-step FFT/NTT algorithm (Appendix B) makes a single transform partly parallel. Writing $n = R \cdot C$ and treating the $n$ inputs as an $R \times C$ matrix, the algorithm proceeds as follows (the transposes in steps 1 and 4 can be skipped via column-major storage and order-independent element-wise operations).

4-step FFT/NTT  (n = R x C)

1. Transpose the R x C input into a C x R matrix; perform FFT/NTT on
   each row (C independent R-point transforms). Call the result A'.
2. Transpose A' into an R x C matrix A''.   (global dependency here)
3. Build the R x C twisting-factor matrix F = [F_ij], F_ij = omega_n^(i*j);
   dyadic-multiply A'' by F element-wise to get A'''.
4. Perform FFT/NTT on each row of A''' (R independent C-point transforms);
   transpose to obtain the final C x R result A.

The result equals the $n$-point transform up to a reordering that does not matter for element-wise polynomial add/mul. With $R, C \approx \sqrt{n}$ (e.g. $R = C = 256$ at $n = 65536$), the per-step parallelism is high; only the global transpose in step 2 retains a global dependency, handled by a reusable transpose network (cheaper than FFT/NTT logic and reusable for permutation, e.g. in F1). The trade-off is more pre-computed parameters: the algorithm reduces the number of twiddle factors (only $R$-point and $C$-point ones are needed) but introduces $n$ twisting factors per RNS modulus whose total size is close to the original twiddle-factor size, so recent designs (BTS, CraterLake) generate both on the fly rather than caching them.

Accelerating key-switching and bootstrapping

Accelerating key-switching. The main challenge is generality. State-of-the-art designs use generalized key-switching parameterized by $\mathrm{dnum} \in [1, L+1]$ (the decomposition number). Based on RNS decomposition, the $L+1$ moduli $q_0, q_1, \ldots, q_L$ are grouped into $\mathrm{dnum}$ blocks, with per-block partial product

$$Q_j = \prod_{i=j\alpha}^{(j+1)\alpha-1} q_i, \qquad j \in [0, \mathrm{dnum}), \quad \alpha = \left\lfloor (L+1)/\mathrm{dnum} \right\rfloor$$

shown in Equation 8. Key-switching decomposes into per-block calculations followed by accumulation across blocks. Each block does fast basis conversion (mostly modular mul/add), while the dominant operation in the cross-block accumulation is NTT. As $\mathrm{dnum}$ grows from $1$ to $L+1$, the NTT workload and overall memory consumption strictly grow while the basis-conversion workload shrinks; the balance also depends on the depth $L$, so no single resource split is optimal for all parameters. Designers must also pick a parallelism scheme: rPLP (residue-polynomial-level: one processor per residue polynomial; introduces extra data exchange in basis conversion) versus CLP (coefficient-level: many processors cooperate on a single residue polynomial, with its $n$ coefficients distributed across processors; introduces global communication in NTT). Neither is superior for coefficient-wise operations like polynomial addition and dyadic multiplication; small $\mathrm{dnum}$ (which raises the importance of basis conversion) favors CLP.

Accelerating bootstrapping. Hardest of all, for two reasons. (1) Large consumption of multiplicative depth: in CraterLake's LSTM benchmark, bootstrapping uses 61% of the maximum depth (35 of 57 levels), mostly from the deep approximation schemes. Supporting it forces a larger $L$ (and even larger, to reduce bootstrapping frequency), hence larger $Q$ and a larger $n$ to keep RLWE security, hence bigger ciphertexts and keys and more memory. (2) Enormous homomorphic permutations: CoeffToSlot/SlotToCoeff need many permutations (about 30 for a ciphertext packing 4096 messages), and each rotation step needs a distinct switching key (a single switching key is about $\mathrm{dnum}\times$ the size of a ciphertext). At $n = 2^{15}$, about 40 distinct switching keys may need precomputing and storing for the linear transform of bootstrapping.

Accelerator landscape: GPU then FPGA then ASIC

Accelerators evolved from general hardware to specialized silicon. Early designs exploited features of existing hardware: Intel HEXL uses AVX-512 on CPUs; nuFHE and $100\times$ use CUDA on GPUs. To go further, FPGAs build dedicated circuits for NTT/iNTT and key-switching, but suffer from limited programmable resources and low clock frequency. ASICs then push the highest performance (roughly $1000\times$ NTT throughput over FPGA and $100\times$ over GPU) at very high cost (a 12nm/14nm tape-out of a 100 mm$^2$ chip runs into millions of US dollars and is usually closed-source due to IP restrictions).

The common building block is the FFT/NTT module. Notably, most accelerators implement only one of FFT or NTT (FFT uses floating point, NTT only integers; they need completely different circuits), so each supports only the schemes that use its chosen transform. Only the GPU-based nuFHE offers both. Early works used fixed NTT/FFT units and fixed workflows; later works adopt programmable NTT/FFT units and data paths (often via the 4-step algorithm) plus large customized on-chip memory, which pushed designers toward ASIC. Works that merely accelerate a basic module (e.g. generic FFT for signal processing) are not counted as FHE accelerators.

Survey of individual accelerators (approach and gains)

GPU-based. cuHE/cuFHE (Dai et al., 2015/2018) provide CUDA NTT/CRT; cuFHE accelerates TFHE but only supports NTT (an FFT-based implementation could be faster for TFHE) with fixed cryptographic parameters, so its generality is low. nuFHE (NuCypher, 2018) offers both FFT and NTT for TFHE with Python APIs; with FFT it beats cuFHE, but generality is still limited. $100\times$ (Jung et al., 2021) accelerates CKKS on a V100 GPU with a hierarchical/4-step NTT split into two kernels (so all input data fits in shared memory), a further generalized 4-step decomposition for the small register file, coalesced memory access, on-the-fly twiddle generation, and kernel fusion, reaching over $100\times$ over single-thread CPU; end-to-end it is still bottlenecked by main-memory bandwidth because GPUs lack large on-chip memory. TensorFHE (Fan et al., 2023) recasts NTT as multiple sequential general matrix multiplications to run on GPU Tensor Core Units (V100/A100) using fine-grained operation batching; it claims to beat CPU/GPU/FPGA designs and match F1 in some cases thanks to modern process nodes, but still hits GPU architectural limits.

CPU-based. HEXL (Intel, 2021) uses AVX-512/AVX512IFMA for single-core NTT/iNTT and dyadic multiplication; it is integrated into PALISADE, Microsoft SEAL and HElib by replacing the underlying arithmetic. It is thread-safe, but performance degrades with many threads (SIMD heat dissipation throttles core frequency at TDP) and it suffers from slow memory access.

FPGA-based. HEAT (Roy et al., 2019) targets BFV on a heterogeneous ARM-FPGA (Xilinx ZCU102, later AWS f1); ARM cores orchestrate FPGA coprocessors for add/sub/mul/modulus-switching/NTT, composing higher-level ops like key-switching in software. Limited BRAM/URAM/DSP forces just two CT butterfly units per NTT core (reused) and degree only $4096$, badly hurting generality. HEAX (Microsoft, 2020) accelerates CKKS with tunable NTT and multiplication cores plus a dedicated key-switching module pipeline (minimizing software-hardware interaction); it is closed-source, and adapting to new parameters requires physically reconfiguring the FPGA. HEXL-FPGA (Intel, 2021) is an HLS implementation of NTT/iNTT, multiplication and key-switching, compiled per-function to individual bitstreams and integrable with HEXL; HLS leads to redundant resource use and suboptimal performance, and it supports only a limited parameter range. Poseidon (Yang et al., 2023) is the first FPGA accelerator supporting bootstrapping, on a modern U280 FPGA with HBM, using radix-based NTT and optimized automorphism to improve resource efficiency. FAB (Agrawal et al., 2023) is also a bootstrapping-capable U280/HBM FPGA design that optimizes on-chip memory access around the bootstrapping workflow.

ASIC-based. F1 (Feldmann et al., 2021) is the first programmable FHE ASIC: 16 compute clusters of pipelined, 128-element-vectorized functional units (NTT, modular mul/add, automorphism), a 4-step NTT to decompose into 128-element vector ops, a 64 MB SRAM scratchpad over 16 banks, and a fully connected network of three $16 \times 16$ crossbars. Weaknesses: heavy reliance on an under-described compiler, a fixed key-switching algorithm ($\mathrm{dnum} = L+1$, very slow at high depth), and only non-packed bootstrapping (max degree $16384$, too small for fully packed bootstrapping at 80/128-bit RLWE security), so it struggles on deep workloads. CraterLake (Samardzic et al., 2022) is F1's successor targeting unbounded-depth multiplication via fully packed bootstrapping; logically one 2048-element vectorized processor (physically eight 256-element groups), max degree $65536$ and depth $60$. It adds two FUs over F1: Change-RNS-base (CRB) for fast basis conversion at small $\mathrm{dnum}$ (e.g. $\mathrm{dnum}=1$), and an on-the-fly switching-key generator (KSHGen). It switches from rPLP to CLP with a static coefficient distribution, eliminating the crossbars and making parallelism independent of depth and concurrency. The CRB unit occupies 34% of on-chip area, so at large $\mathrm{dnum}$ it is underutilized and CraterLake even trails F1 on shallow tasks; full resource use needs $n = 65536$. BTS (Kim et al., 2022) follows $100\times$ with goals like CraterLake: a GPU-like $64 \times 32$ 2D array of 2048 processing elements (PEs), each with a basis-conversion unit and a single 2-point butterfly (one butterfly unit), connected by vertical/horizontal crossbars. It does, for example, a $131072$-point NTT in three generalized-4-step phases - a $64$-point NTT per PE, then a $64$-point NTT per row (horizontal-crossbar sync), then a $32$-point NTT per column (vertical-crossbar sync) - with multiple small local scratchpads per PE directly on HBM. Drawback: the 2D array cannot pipeline multiple NTTs (all PEs participate in each phase). ARK (Kim et al., 2022) uses four CraterLake-like vectorized clusters and is the first to co-design the bootstrapping algorithm with the hardware: multi-hop homomorphic rotation and on-the-fly residue extension shrink the switching keys and plaintexts. ARK measures NTT at 54.8% and basis conversion at 34.2% of bootstrapping work, so it uses hybrid parallelism (CLP for basis conversion, rPLP for NTT and other operations) with a switching mechanism between the two patterns for functions like key-switching that contain both.

Software support and evolution trends

Integrating diverse accelerators (each with its own data structures and workflows) into upper-layer FHE software is hard, and transparency to upper-layer software is essential for real-world deployment. The OpenFHE project is highlighted: it adds a Hardware Abstraction Layer (HAL) of C++ abstract classes for NTT/iNTT/vector ops and lattice/RNS subroutines so CPU/GPU/FPGA/ASIC backends can plug in their own logic (a HEXL backend already exists), bridging applications and accelerators.

The authors distill four evolution trends. (1) From general hardware to ASIC: CPU/GPU have fixed architectures and small on-chip memory; FPGA has limited resources and low frequency; ASIC allows custom architecture (crossbars, HBM plus SRAM) but costs millions and is usually closed-source. (2) Software/hardware co-design: early designs ran single instructions with no scheduling (static workflow) and suffered generality and software-hardware interaction overhead; recent designs split resources into independently scheduled clusters with flexible software, though they do not yet leverage general FHE compilers. (3) Enhanced programmability: the 4-step NTT improves both parallelism and the ability to handle varying $n$, but the key-switching generality challenge remains unsolved, and deep-focused designs are slow on shallow tasks (CraterLake slower than F1 when $L$ is between 4 and 8, because its large basis-conversion units are underutilized). (4) Unlimited depth via bootstrapping: CraterLake, BTS and ARK converge on supporting bootstrapping, favoring 4-step NTT and generalized key-switching, dedicating area to global transpose and fast basis conversion, reducing memory cost via on-the-fly generation, and adopting CLP as the primary parallelism scheme (in contrast to the rPLP of earlier designs).

Findings: (1) despite strong NTT, F1 is poor on deep tasks needing depth - one LR iteration takes F1 $1024$ ms, slower than $100\times$ ($775$ ms), Poseidon ($73$ ms), FAB ($103$ ms) and TensorFHE ($222$ ms); (2) ASICs with efficient bootstrapping dominate FPGA/GPU on deep work - BTS is $2.6\times$ faster than FAB and $7.8\times$ faster than TensorFHE on LR, and with bootstrapping at 76.2% of ResNet20, ARK (algorithm plus architecture co-design of bootstrapping) is $6.5\times$ faster than BTS.

Shallow end-to-end. Most recent works (BTS, ARK, Poseidon, ...) ignore shallow tasks. F1 excels here (no bootstrapping): $5{,}011\times$ on LoLa-CIFAR (unencrypted weights), and $17{,}412\times$ / $15{,}086\times$ on LoLa-MNIST (unencrypted / encrypted weights) vs CPU. CraterLake has no real advantage over F1 on shallow tasks because it spends area on deep-specific operations.

On-chip storage (Figure 5, bottom chart). ASIC performance is strongly tied to on-chip SRAM, since storing ciphertexts and switching keys on-chip avoids costly on/off-chip data exchange. For CraterLake and BTS, bootstrapping speedup rises with SRAM then plateaus once SRAM is large enough that no further data exchange is needed (even with high-speed HBM).

Open challenges and future directions

The authors propose five directions.

  • Application-driven design. Rather than always chasing faster, costlier silicon, co-design the whole architecture stack with the application. Examples (Table 3): INSPIRE co-designs in-storage architecture with the accelerator for private information retrieval (PIR); Cheetah and FxHENN co-design HE-based CNN inference workflows with the HE algorithms.
  • Support both word-wise and bit-wise FHE on one accelerator, with efficient ciphertext switching between the two (e.g. CKKS and FHEW), since real ML needs both polynomial and non-polynomial evaluation and switching between forms is currently very slow.
  • Enhanced software/hardware co-design. Co-design accelerator compilers with general FHE compilers such as EVA, integrating a hardware-level cost model for better end-to-end performance.
  • From scale-up to scale-out. Connect multiple accelerators over the network rather than only improving a single accelerator vertically; co-design FHE acceleration with high-performance on-chip networking to handle data inflation across nodes.
  • Accelerating NTRU-based schemes. Largely uncovered because few exist and older NTRU schemes were seen as insecure, but modern NTRU variants are low-memory and fast. Table 4 shows GPU and ASIC speedups on homomorphic AES/Prince evaluation versus a CPU baseline; current NTRU accelerators reach about 3 orders of magnitude (ASIC), lower than the about 5 orders for CKKS/BFV/TFHE accelerators, because NTRU is already faster (less room) and current NTRU ASICs are simpler.

Conclusion. Across 14 accelerators the paper identifies four trends: increasing use of advanced hardware, widespread software/hardware co-design, enhanced programmability, and improved bootstrapping for unlimited-depth operation. It calls for NTRU-based accelerators and scale-out (rather than only scale-up) methodologies to guide future FHE accelerator design.

Algorithms

This is a survey, not a single-construction paper, so it contains no formal numbered algorithm blocks. For an at-a-glance reference, the listings below assemble the core procedures the body relies on: the two butterflies, the radix-2 FFT/NTT, the 4-step transform, and the high-level generalized key-switching and word-wise bootstrapping workflows the accelerators implement.

ALG 1. Cooley-Tukey (CT) butterfly  -  decimation-in-time
Input : A0 = A_k^[0], A1 = A_k^[1], twiddle w = omega_n^k
Output: (A_k, A_{k+n/2})
  1. t   <- w * A1            // one shared multiplication
  2. A_k       <- A0 + t
  3. A_{k+n/2} <- A0 - t
  4. return (A_k, A_{k+n/2})

ALG 2. Gentlemen-Sande (GS) butterfly  -  decimation-in-frequency (reverse of CT)
Input : a0 = a_k, a1 = a_{k+n/2}, twiddle w = omega_n^k
Output: (a_k^[0], a_k^[1])
  1. a_k^[0] <- a0 + a1
  2. a_k^[1] <- (a0 - a1) * w
  3. return (a_k^[0], a_k^[1])
ALG 3. Radix-2 FFT / NTT  (n a power of two)
Input : coefficient vector a = (a_0,...,a_{n-1});
         for NTT, prime p and primitive n-th root g_n (use g in place of omega, mod p)
Output: transform A = (A_0,...,A_{n-1}),  A_k = sum_j a_j * w_n^{kj}
  1. if n == 1: return a
  2. split a into even part A^[0] and odd part A^[1]   // a(x) = A^[0](x^2) + x*A^[1](x^2)
  3. Ye <- FFT/NTT(A^[0])          // size n/2
  4. Yo <- FFT/NTT(A^[1])          // size n/2
  5. for k = 0 .. n/2 - 1:
  6.     (A_k, A_{k+n/2}) <- CT_butterfly(Ye[k], Yo[k], w_n^k)   // ALG 1
  7. return A
  // identities used: w_{2n}^{2k} = w_n^k ;  w_n^{k+n/2} = -w_n^k
  // NTT: replace mul/add/sub by modular mul/add/sub mod p; identities hold mod p
ALG 4. 4-step FFT / NTT  (n = R * C, partial parallelism)
Input : a of length n, viewed as an R x C matrix M (column-major skips step-1 transpose)
Output: A, the n-point transform (reordered; harmless for element-wise poly ops)
  1. M' <- transpose(M)                       // C x R  (skippable via column-major store)
  2. for each of the C rows: row <- FFT/NTT(row)   // C independent R-point transforms -> A'
  3. A'' <- transpose(A')                     // R x C   (global-dependency step)
  4. build twisting matrix F, F_ij = omega_n^{i*j}
  5. A''' <- A'' (.*) F                        // element-wise (dyadic) multiply
  6. for each of the R rows: row <- FFT/NTT(row)   // R independent C-point transforms
  7. A <- transpose(result)                   // (skippable for element-wise poly ops)
  8. return A
ALG 5. Generalized key-switching  (parameter dnum in [1, L+1])
Input : ct decryptable under s'; switching key swk (ciphertext of P*s', modulus P*Q);
         moduli q_0..q_L with Q = prod q_i ; alpha = floor((L+1)/dnum)
Output: ct' decryptable under s, same plaintext
  1. group q_0..q_L into dnum blocks; Q_j = prod_{i=j*alpha}^{(j+1)alpha-1} q_i
  2. for each block j in [0, dnum):
  3.     fast basis conversion of ct's block (mainly modular mul/add)
  4. accumulate the per-block results across blocks   // dominant op: NTT
  5. rescale by P (special modulus controls noise scale)
  6. return ct'
  // larger dnum -> more NTT work + memory, less basis-conversion work

ALG 6. Word-wise bootstrapping  (refresh multiplicative depth)
Input : ct under modulus Q (depth nearly exhausted)
Output: ct_fresh encrypting the same plaintext with recovered depth
  1. Modulus Switching : raise modulus Q -> Q' >> Q
  2. CoeffToSlot       : homomorphic encoding / linear transform
                          (move plaintext coefficients into plaintext slots;
                           uses ~30 homomorphic permutations for 4096 packed msgs)
  3. Homomorphic Eval  : approximate the non-polynomial modulo of the coefficients
                          (digit extraction in BGV/BFV, modulus reduction in CKKS;
                           via Taylor / optimized Chebyshev high-degree polynomials)
  4. SlotToCoeff       : inverse linear transform (slots -> coefficients)
  5. return ct_fresh
  // bit-wise TFHE bootstrapping instead refreshes every gate via a MUX-based LUT
