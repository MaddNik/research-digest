Improved Programmable Bootstrapping with Larger Precision and Efficient Arithmetic Circuits for TFHE

ASIACRYPT 2021 (IACR ePrint 2021/729) · full view ↗

Overview and Motivation

By Ilaria Chillotti, Damien Ligier, Jean-Baptiste Orfila and Samuel Tap (Zama, Paris, November 2021). TFHE [CGGI17] is an (R)LWE-based fully homomorphic encryption scheme whose distinguishing feature is a very fast programmable bootstrapping (PBS): a single operation that both reduces ciphertext noise and evaluates a univariate function expressed as a look-up table (LUT). In TFHE a message $m$ is encoded in the most significant bits, rescaled by a scaling factor $\Delta$ and reduced modulo $q$, with small noise $e$ added in the least significant bits, so a noisy plaintext looks like $\Delta \cdot m + e \bmod q$. The precision is defined as $p = \log_2(q/\Delta)$. This encoding is shown in Figure 1.

The paper attacks a list of concrete TFHE limitations (labelled A through H in the introduction):

  • A. To bootstrap correctly the MSB of the plaintext must be known (set to zero), unless the evaluated function is negacyclic. This costs one bit of message space (a bit of padding).
  • B. One cannot efficiently bootstrap a large-precision message (more than about 6 bits): the bootstrapped precision is tied to the ring dimension $N$, so more precision forces larger $N$ and slower computation (FFT cost scales like $N \log_2 N$).
  • C. The PBS is a sequential accumulator loop and is not multi-thread friendly.
  • D. There is no native LWE-by-LWE multiplication; existing approaches use PBS (the $x \mapsto x^2/4$ trick, giving $x \cdot y = \frac{(x+y)^2}{4} - \frac{(x-y)^2}{4}$) or circuit bootstrapping, both inheriting limitations A and B.
  • E. Because of A and B one cannot efficiently split a single ciphertext into several ciphertexts each holding a chunk of the message.
  • F. A PBS evaluates a single function per call; the multi-value trick of [CIM19] adds function-dependent noise.
  • G. Gate bootstrapping requires a PBS per Boolean gate (costly), with no native solution for larger integers.
  • H. Circuit bootstrapping needs $\ell$ PBS plus $(k+1)\ell$ private key switches.

The contributions remove these limitations by adding a BFV-like multiplication directly into the TFHE context (no scheme switching as in Chimera [BGGJ20]), generalizing the PBS, and combining the two into a Programmable Bootstrapping Without Padding (WoP-PBS). The result also yields fast Boolean/arithmetic circuits, a faster circuit bootstrapping, and large-precision bootstrapping with much smaller parameters than naively scaled TFHE. The authors note their noise analysis is also valid for BFV-like schemes.

Background and Notation

$q$ is the integer modulus; $N$ is a power of two and $R_q = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^N+1)$. $\chi_\sigma$ is a centered Gaussian with standard deviation $\sigma$. The paper works over $\mathbb{Z}_q$ ($q = 2^{32}$ or $2^{64}$) rather than the torus $\mathbb{T}$, using the isomorphism between $\mathbb{Z}_q$ and $(1/q)\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}$.

Ciphertext families. A GLWE ciphertext of $M \in R_q$ with scaling factor $\Delta$ under secret key $S = (S_1, \ldots, S_k) \in R_q^k$ is given by Equation 1:

$$\mathrm{CT} = \Big(A_1, \ldots, A_k,\ B = \sum_{i=1}^{k} A_i \cdot S_i + \lfloor M \cdot \Delta \rceil_q + E\Big) = \mathrm{GLWE}_S(M \cdot \Delta) \in R_q^{k+1}$$

where the $A_i$ are uniform in $R_q$, $E \in R_q$ has coefficients from $\chi_\sigma$, and one sometimes sets $S_{k+1} = -1$. Setting $N = 1$ gives an LWE ciphertext (with key size $n = k$, written in lower case $\mathrm{ct}, s$); setting $k = 1, N \gt 1$ gives RLWE. A GLev ciphertext with base $B \in \mathbb{N}^*$ and $\ell \in \mathbb{N}^*$ levels is the vector $\mathrm{CT} = (\mathrm{CT}_1, \ldots, \mathrm{CT}_\ell)$ where $\mathrm{CT}_i = \mathrm{GLWE}_S(M \cdot q/B^i)$. A GGSW ciphertext is the vector of $k+1$ GLev ciphertexts $\mathrm{CT}_i = \mathrm{GLev}_S^{(B,\ell)}(-S_i \cdot M)$ (with $S_{k+1} = -1$).

Decomposition. $\mathrm{dec}^{(B,\ell)}(x)$ returns the MSB-first base-$B$ decomposition vector of an integer (or polynomial) $x$, satisfying Equation 2:

$$\Big\langle \mathrm{dec}^{(B,\ell)}(x),\ \big(\tfrac{q}{B^1}, \ldots, \tfrac{q}{B^\ell}\big) \Big\rangle = \Big\lfloor \frac{x \cdot B^\ell}{q} \Big\rceil \cdot \frac{q}{B^\ell} \in \mathbb{Z}_q$$

Key switching changes keys/parameters using a public key-switching key; the paper uses three flavours: PrivateKS (applies a private linear function $f$ over $p$ LWE inputs to build a GLWE), PublicKS (public linear function; the identity-function LWE instance is the key switch used inside the TFHE PBS), and PackingKS (packs $p$ LWE ciphertexts into one GLWE at chosen monomial indices $\{i_j\}$ via $f(\{m_j\}) = \sum_{j} m_j \cdot X^{i_j}$). The LWE-to-GLWE key-switching key is $\mathrm{KSK} = \{\mathrm{CT}_i = \mathrm{GLev}_{S'}^{(B,\ell)}(s_i)\}_{1 \le i \le n}$.

TFHE PBS has three steps: (1) Modulus Switching from $\mathbb{Z}_q^{n+1}$ to $\mathbb{Z}_{2N}^{n+1}$; (2) Blind Rotation of a GLWE-encrypted redundant LUT by a CMux loop driven by the LWE coefficients and the bootstrapping key (a list of GGSW encryptions of the secret-key bits); (3) Sample Extraction of the constant coefficient as an LWE ciphertext. A redundant LUT repeats each entry $r$ times so the rounding/noise removal works during the rotation. TFHE circuit bootstrapping [CGGI17, Alg. 6/11] converts an LWE ciphertext into a GGSW with $\ell$ PBS followed by $(k+1)\ell$ private key switchings.

Building Block 1: GLWE / LWE Multiplication (BFV-like)

The first building block is a BFV-like multiplication (tensor product followed by relinearization) imported into the TFHE context. Algorithm 1 multiplies two GLWE ciphertexts under the same key, producing a ciphertext of $\mathrm{PT}_1 \cdot \mathrm{PT}_2 / \Delta$ where $\Delta = \min(\Delta_1, \Delta_2)$, using a relinearization key $\mathrm{RLK} = \{\mathrm{CT}_{i,j} = \mathrm{GLev}_S^{(B,\ell)}(S_i \cdot S_j)\}_{1 \le j \le i \le k}$. The paper gives a full noise analysis (Theorem 1) and a parameter table (Table 1) giving $N$, base $B$ and level $\ell$ as a function of precision (1 to 24 bits) and maximum multiplicative depth. For example precision 6 admits depth 8 with $\log_2 N = 12,\ \log_2 B = 8,\ \ell = 8$; precision 24 admits depth 2 with $\log_2 N = 12,\ \log_2 B = 20,\ \ell = 2$; precision 1 admits depth 32. The GLWE-multiplication noise variance, where $\mathrm{Var}(S)$ is the variance of a key polynomial coefficient and $S', S''$ denote the parity-split and cross-product key statistics, is given by Equation 3:

$$\mathrm{Var}_{\mathrm{GLWEMult}} = \frac{N}{\Delta^2}\Big( \Delta_1^2 \lVert M_1 \rVert_\infty^2 \sigma_2^2 + \Delta_2^2 \lVert M_2 \rVert_\infty^2 \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 \Big) + \frac{N}{\Delta^2}\Big( \tfrac{q^2-1}{12}\big(1 + kN\,\mathrm{Var}(S) + kN\,\mathbb{E}^2(S)\big) + \tfrac{kN}{4}\mathrm{Var}(S) + \tfrac14(1+kN\,\mathbb{E}(S))^2 \Big)(\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2) + \frac{1}{12} + (\text{key-dependent and relinearization terms})$$

The complexity (Equation 4) splits into a tensor product and a relinearization step, with $k^* = \tfrac{k(k+1)}{2}$ and $k^+ = \tfrac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}$:

$$C_{\mathrm{GLWEMult}} = C_{\mathrm{TensorProduct}} + C_{\mathrm{Relin}}, \quad C_{\mathrm{TensorProduct}} = 2(k+1)C_{\mathrm{FFT}} + k^+ C_{\mathrm{iFFT}} + (k+1)^2 N\, C_{\mathrm{multFFT}} + k^* N\, C_{\mathrm{addFFT}}$$

The full Algorithm 1 is given at-a-glance in the final Algorithms section. A more efficient GLWE square (Algorithm 9 in the supplementary material) shares the cross-term computation: each $A'_i$ and $R'_{i,j}$ is obtained with a single multiplication ($A_i^2$, $2 A_i A_j$, $2 A_i B$, $B^2$) instead of two, then the same relinearization step is applied. Its noise analysis is essentially that of Theorem 1.

Building Block 1 (cont.): Single, Packed and Sum-of-Products LWE Multiplication

Algorithm 2 multiplies two LWE ciphertexts by (i) packing each input into a GLWE via a PackingKS at index $\{0\}$, (ii) calling the GLWE multiplication (Algorithm 1), and (iii) sample-extracting the constant term. The output scaling factor is $\Delta_{\mathrm{out}} = \max(\Delta_1, \Delta_2)$. The sample extraction adds no noise; it merely rearranges coefficients of the input GLWE to build an LWE encryption of one chosen polynomial coefficient.

Theorem 2 gives two post-packing variances - a filled-coefficient variance and an empty-coefficient variance - given (for a single packed ciphertext) by Equation 5:

$$\mathrm{Var}^{(1)}_{\mathrm{fill}} = \sigma^2 + n\Big(\tfrac{q^2}{12 B^{2\ell}} - \tfrac{1}{12}\Big)\big(\mathrm{Var}(s_i) + \mathbb{E}^2(s_i)\big) + \tfrac{n}{4}\mathrm{Var}(s_i) + n\ell\,\sigma_{\mathrm{KSK}}^2\,\tfrac{B^2+2}{12}, \qquad \mathrm{Var}^{(1)}_{\mathrm{emp}} = n\ell\,\sigma_{\mathrm{KSK}}^2\,\tfrac{B^2+2}{12}$$

For $1 \le \alpha \le N$ packed ciphertexts, $\mathrm{Var}^{(\alpha)}_{\mathrm{fill}} = \mathrm{Var}^{(1)}_{\mathrm{fill}} + (\alpha-1)\mathrm{Var}^{(1)}_{\mathrm{emp}}$ and $\mathrm{Var}^{(\alpha)}_{\mathrm{emp}} = \alpha \cdot \mathrm{Var}^{(1)}_{\mathrm{emp}}$. Theorem 3 obtains the LWEMult noise by substituting these packing variances for $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$ in the GLWE-multiplication formula (Equation 3). Its complexity is $C_{\mathrm{LWEMult}} = 2\, C_{\mathrm{PackingKS}}^{(1)} + C_{\mathrm{GLWEMult}} + C_{\mathrm{SampleExtract}}$. Algorithm 2 is listed in full in the Algorithms section.

Packed variants. By slightly modifying Algorithm 2, one can compute many products, many squares, a sum of products or a sum of squares with a single GLWE multiplication, by packing the inputs at carefully chosen monomial-index sets and extracting the right coefficients (Algorithms 10 to 13, given at-a-glance in the Algorithms section):

  • PackedMult ($\alpha^2 \le N$): pack input set 1 at indices $\mathcal{L}_1 = \{0,1,\ldots,\alpha-1\}$ and set 2 at $\mathcal{L}_2 = \{0,\alpha,2\alpha,\ldots,(\alpha-1)\alpha\}$, multiply, then extract coefficients at indices $i(\alpha+1)$ for $0 \le i \lt \alpha$, yielding $\mathrm{LWE}(m_i^{(1)} m_i^{(2)} \Delta_{\mathrm{out}})$.
  • PackedSumProducts ($\alpha \le N$): pack set 1 at $\{0,1,\ldots,\alpha-1\}$ and set 2 at $\{\alpha-1,\alpha-2,\ldots,0\}$, multiply, then extract the single coefficient at index $\alpha-1$, giving $\mathrm{LWE}\big(\sum_{i=0}^{\alpha-1} m_i^{(1)} m_i^{(2)} \Delta_{\mathrm{out}}\big)$.
  • PackedSquares ($2\alpha \le N$): pack at indices $\{2^0-1, 2^1-1, \ldots, 2^{\alpha-1}-1\}$, square (GLWE square), extract coefficients at indices $2^{i+1}-2$, giving $\{\mathrm{LWE}(m_i^2 \Delta)\}$.
  • PackedSumSquares ($2\alpha \le N$): pack with redundancy using $\mathcal{L}_1 = \{0,\ldots,\alpha-1\}$ and $\mathcal{L}_2 = \{2\alpha-1,2\alpha-2,\ldots,\alpha\}$, square, extract coefficient at index $2\alpha-1$, giving $\mathrm{LWE}\big(\sum_{i=0}^{\alpha-1} m_i^2 \cdot 2\Delta\big)$.

Building Block 2: Generalized PBS (GenPBS)

The second building block generalizes the TFHE PBS so it can bootstrap an arbitrary chunk of bits rather than only the MSB, and evaluate several functions at once. Two new integer parameters redefine the modulus-switching step: $\kappa$ is the number of MSB ignored by the PBS, and $2^\vartheta$ is the number of functions that can be evaluated simultaneously ($\vartheta$ LSB are forced to zero). The original TFHE PBS is the special case $(\kappa, \vartheta) = (0,0)$. Figure 2 illustrates how this modulus switching maps the input plaintext (with the leading $\kappa$ MSB ignored and $\vartheta$ trailing bits cleared) onto the rotation window of size $2N$.

A helper $\mathrm{PTModSwitch}_q(m, \Delta, \kappa, \vartheta)$ returns $(\beta, m')$. With $q' = q/(\Delta \cdot 2^\kappa)$, for $\kappa \ge 0$ it sets $m' = (m \bmod q') \bmod (q'/2)$ and $\beta = 0$ if $m \bmod q' \lt q'/2$ else $\beta = 1$ (shown in Figure 3); for $\kappa \lt 0$ it sets $m' = m$ and $\beta$ a random bit. The modulus-switched coefficient is $a'_i = \big\lfloor \lceil a_i \cdot 2N \cdot 2^{\kappa-\vartheta} / q \rceil \cdot 2^\vartheta \big\rceil_{2N}$.

Theorem 4 states the correctness condition: with $w = 2N \cdot 2^{-\vartheta}$ and $q' = q \cdot 2^{-\kappa}$, requiring $\tfrac{q\, 2^\vartheta}{\Delta_{\mathrm{in}} 2^\kappa} \lt 2N$, the input noise variance must satisfy Equation 6:

$$\sigma_{\mathrm{in}}^2 \lt \frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{in}}^2}{4\Gamma^2} - \frac{q'^2}{12 w^2} + \frac{1}{12} - \frac{n\,q'^2}{24 w^2} - \frac{n}{48},$$

where $\Gamma$ relates to the correctness probability $P = \mathrm{erf}(\Gamma/\sqrt{2})$. The output ciphertext encrypts $(-1)^\beta \cdot f(m') \cdot \Delta_{\mathrm{out}}$, and the output noise (binary keys) is given by Equation 7:

$$\mathrm{Var}_{\mathrm{PBS}} = n\,\ell\,(k+1)\,N\,\frac{B^2+2}{12}\,\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{BSK}) + n\,\frac{q^2 - B^{2\ell}}{24\,B^{2\ell}}\Big(1 + \frac{kN}{2}\Big) + \frac{n\,kN}{32} + \frac{n}{16}\Big(1 - \frac{kN}{2}\Big)^2$$

Its complexity equals that of TFHE bootstrapping (modulus switching + $n$ CMux + sample extract), the CMux being built from an external product whose noise the appendix derives in detail. The full Algorithm 3 (GenPBS) appears in the Algorithms section. The paper gives the binary-key noise formula in the body and ternary/Gaussian variants in Supplementary Material B.

WoP-PBS, First Version

The negacyclicity of the LUT rotation is exactly what forces a padding bit (Limitation A). WoP-PBS removes it. Version 1 (Algorithm 4) runs two generalized PBS in parallel: one (with LUT $P_f$) produces $\mathrm{ct}_f$ encrypting $(-1)^\beta \cdot f(m') \cdot \Delta_{\mathrm{out}}$, the other (with the constant-1 LUT $P_1$) produces a sign ciphertext $\mathrm{ct}_{\mathrm{Sign}}$ encrypting $(-1)^\beta \cdot \Delta_{\mathrm{out}}$. Both GenPBS use $\kappa - 1$ (one bit shifted). Multiplying them with LWEMult cancels the sign, since both share the same $\beta$ and $(-1)^{2\beta} = 1$, leaving an encryption of $f(m') \cdot \Delta_{\mathrm{out}}$ (Theorem 5). The output noise equals $\mathrm{Var}_{\mathrm{LWEMult}}$ with the two GenPBS variances $\sigma_i = \mathrm{Var}_{\mathrm{GenPBS}}$ as inputs, and the complexity is $C_{\mathrm{WoP\text{-}PBS1}} = 2\,C_{\mathrm{GenPBS}} + C_{\mathrm{LWEMult}}$. The full Algorithm 4 is in the Algorithms section.

Practical optimizations (Remark 2 and following):

  • The two GenPBS share the input ciphertext, so they can be fused into one PBS via the multi-value bootstrap of [CIM19] (faster, more noise) or via PBSmanyLUT (Algorithm 6, when the message is small enough so $N$ has spare bits).
  • One of the two key switchings inside LWEMult can be dropped if an input arrives already as a GLWE; the KSK target key $S''$ need not equal $s'$ (the RLK is then adapted to $S''$).
  • The LWEMult on the final line can be replaced by an LWESquare (PackedSquares with $\alpha = 2$).

WoP-PBS, Second Version

Version 2 (Algorithm 5) targets Limitation B (the precision-versus-$N$ link). The message $m$ is written as $\beta \,\|\, m'$ (top bit $\beta$, remainder $m'$). The function $f$ is split into $f_0$ and $f_1$ with $f_0(x) = f(x) = f_1(x - p)$ for a certain $p$; the encrypted decision bit $\beta$ selects which to apply. Three GenPBS run in parallel ($P_{f_0}$, $P_{f_1}$, constant $P_1$ for the sign), two trivial additions form decision ciphertexts $\mathrm{ct}_{\beta_0} = \mathrm{LWE}((1-\beta) \cdot \Delta_{\mathrm{out}})$ and $\mathrm{ct}_{\beta_1} = \mathrm{LWE}(-\beta \cdot \Delta_{\mathrm{out}})$, two LWEMult select the branch, and a final addition combines them (Theorem 6). The output noise is $2 \cdot \mathrm{Var}_{\mathrm{LWEMult}}$.

Table 2 lists 128-bit-security parameter sets for a WoP-PBS2 followed by a GLWE multiplication for precisions 1 to 7. In every set $n = 550$; $\log_2 N = 11$ for 1 to 5 bits and $\log_2 N = 12$ for 6 and 7 bits; separate base/level pairs are given for the blind rotation (BR), key switching (KS) and relinearization (Relin). For example precision 1 uses BR $(\log_2 B, \ell) = (17, 2)$, KS $(21, 2)$, Relin $(30, 1)$; precision 7 uses BR $(4, 13)$, KS $(10, 5)$, Relin $(24, 2)$. The full Algorithm 5 is in the Algorithms section.

Optimizations: fuse the three GenPBS via [CIM19] or PBSmanyLUT (when $N$ is large enough); drop two of the four key switches; use lazy relinearization [LLK+20] (relinearize only after the final addition); and replace the two LWEMult plus the final addition by a single PackedSumProducts (Algorithm 11).

Multi-output PBS (PBSmanyLUT)

Because $\vartheta$ LSB are forced to zero by the modulus switching, the post-modulus-switch plaintext has the form $m^* = m \cdot \Delta + e \cdot 2^\vartheta$, so a single GenPBS can evaluate $2^\vartheta$ functions for free (no extra noise versus an ordinary PBS) by encoding all of them into one LUT polynomial and extracting the first $2^\vartheta$ output coefficients (Algorithm 6, Theorem 7). With $p = q/(\Delta_{\mathrm{in}} \cdot 2^{\kappa+1})$, the LUT is built as in Equation 8:

$$P_{(f_1, \ldots, f_{2^\vartheta})} = X^{N/(2p)} \sum_{j=0}^{p-1} X^{j N/p} \sum_{a=0}^{N/(p\,2^\vartheta) - 1} X^{a\,2^\vartheta} \sum_{i=0}^{2^\vartheta - 1} f_{i+1}(j) \cdot X^i$$

Each block of $2^\vartheta$ function values is repeated along the polynomial for redundancy. Correctness needs $\tfrac{q\,2^\vartheta}{\Delta_{\mathrm{in}} 2^\kappa} \lt 2N$, and the complexity is $C_{\mathrm{PBSmanyLUT}} = C_{\mathrm{GenPBS}} + 2^\vartheta \cdot C_{\mathrm{SampleExtract}}$ (the extra extractions are negligible, so it costs essentially one PBS). The full Algorithm 6 is in the Algorithms section.

Remark 3: PBSmanyLUT and WoP-PBS compose both ways. PBSmanyLUT can fuse the several GenPBS inside a WoP-PBS (when $N$ is large enough), improving complexity without affecting noise; conversely the implicit GenPBS inside PBSmanyLUT can be replaced by a WoP-PBS, yielding what the authors call WoPBSmanyLUT. Remark 4: unlike the [CIM19] multi-value technique, PBSmanyLUT's output noise is independent of the evaluated functions, at the cost of needing $\vartheta$ free LSB (hence large enough $N$); [CIM19] imposes no strong $N$ constraint but its output noise depends on the function.

Application 1: Fast Boolean and Modular Arithmetic

Combining GenPBS and LWEMult lets one evaluate Boolean circuits with leveled gates between bootstraps, instead of a PBS per gate (Limitation G). With a bit $b_i$ encoded as $0$ if $0$ and $q/2$ if $1$ (Lemma 1):

ct1 XOR ct2 = ct1 + ct2
ct1 AND ct2 = LWEMult(ct1, ct2, RLK, KSK)
NOT  ct1    = ct1 + (0, q/2)

XOR, NOT and AND give functional completeness; noise grows until a refresh is needed. Lemma 2 gives two sign-function refresh operators using GenPBS, shown as Equation 9 and Equation 10:

$$\mathrm{ct}_{\mathrm{out}} \leftarrow \mathrm{GenPBS}\big(\mathrm{ct}_{\mathrm{in}}, \mathrm{BSK},\ P_1 \cdot X^{N/2},\ \Delta_{\mathrm{out}} = \tfrac{q}{4},\ \kappa = 0,\ \vartheta = 0\big) + \big(0, \tfrac{q}{4}\big)$$

$$\mathrm{ct}_{\mathrm{out}} \leftarrow \mathrm{GenPBS}\Big(\mathrm{ct}_{\mathrm{in}}, \mathrm{BSK},\ P_f = \sum_{i=N/4}^{3N/4 - 1} X^i,\ \Delta_{\mathrm{out}} = \tfrac{q}{2},\ \kappa = -1,\ \vartheta = 0\Big)$$

The construction generalizes to modular arithmetic over $2^p$: $\mathrm{Add}_{2^p}$ is an LWE addition, $\mathrm{Mul}_{2^p}$ is an LWEMult, and $\mathrm{Opp}_{2^p}$ negates the input. Because functions on multi-bit integers are no longer negacyclic, doing this without WoP-PBS would need two padding bits (one for the linear combination, one for the non-negacyclic PBS) and thus a large $N$; with WoP-PBS no padding is required and the bootstrap is only invoked to reset noise. Table 3 summarizes how each TFHE gate-bootstrapping operator maps to the binary ($p=1$) and integer ($p\gt 1$) cases - for instance $\mathrm{Add}_{2^p}^{\mathrm{MSB}}$ in the integer case costs 3 WoP-PBS, 2 homomorphic additions and 2 public key switches.

Application 2: Exact Integer Arithmetic (carry extraction)

To build exact arithmetic on large integers represented by several ciphertexts, one needs the MSB (carry) of additions and multiplications: $\mathrm{AddMSB}_{2^p}(m_1, m_2) = \big\lfloor (m_1+m_2)/2^p \big\rfloor \bmod 2^p$ and $\mathrm{MulMSB}_{2^p}(m_1, m_2) = \big\lfloor (m_1 \cdot m_2)/2^p \big\rfloor \bmod 2^p$, with each input in $[0, 2^p)$ at scaling $\Delta = q/2^p$. Algorithm 7 first uses two WoP-PBS to drop each input to a lower scaling factor $\Delta / 2^p = q/2^{2p}$ (adding $p$ padding bits), performs the leveled operation (addition for AddMSB, LWEMult for MulMSB) at the doubled precision, key-switches with $\mathrm{KSK}_2$ (from $s'$ to $s$), uses a third WoP-PBS to extract only the LSB, subtracts it to isolate the MSB, and key-switches again. The full Algorithm 7 is in the Algorithms section.

Optimization: both PublicKS can be folded into the relinearization steps of the preceding WoP-PBS, and if parameters allow, the LSB-extraction and subtraction (lines 6 to 7) can be replaced by a single WoP-PBS that directly extracts the MSB.

Application 3: Faster Circuit Bootstrapping

TFHE circuit bootstrapping (converting an LWE ciphertext to a GGSW) has a first step of $\ell$ independent PBS and a second step of $(k+1)\ell$ private key switchings. Because the $\ell$ PBS dominate the cost and differ only in their output scaling, they can be replaced by a single PBSmanyLUT producing all $\ell$ outputs at once, cutting the PBS cost by roughly a factor $\ell$ (factor 2 for the typical $\ell = 2$) with no noise penalty. Lemma 4 specifies the replacement, with $\rho = \lceil \log_2 \ell \rceil$ (Equation 11):

$$\{\mathrm{ct}_i\}_{i \in [1,\ell]} \leftarrow \mathrm{PBSmanyLUT}\big(\mathrm{ct}_{\mathrm{in}}, \mathrm{BSK},\ P \cdot X^{N/2^{\rho+1}},\ 1,\ \kappa = 0,\ \rho = \lceil \log_2 \ell \rceil\big), \qquad \forall i,\ \mathrm{ct}_i + \Big(0, \frac{q}{2 B^i}\Big)$$

$$\text{with } P(X) = \sum_{i=0}^{N/2^\rho - 1} \sum_{j=0}^{2^\rho - 1} \frac{q}{2 B^j}\, X^{2^\rho i + j}$$

The LUT encodes the values $\tfrac{q}{2B^1}, \ldots, \tfrac{q}{2B^\ell}$ followed by zeros, repeated in $N/2^\rho$ blocks; after the rotation each $\mathrm{ct}_i$ is $\mathrm{LWE}(\pm \tfrac{q}{2B^i})$, and adding the trivial $(0, \tfrac{q}{2B^i})$ yields $\mathrm{LWE}(\tfrac{q}{B^i})$ or $\mathrm{LWE}(0)$ as required by the second (private-key-switching) step.

Application 4: Large-Precision Bootstrapping (Decomp) and Large-Precision WoP-PBS

Algorithm 8 (Decomp) homomorphically splits one ciphertext into $\alpha$ ciphertexts each holding a chunk of the message (Limitations E and B). The trick is to extract the least significant bits first: parameter $\kappa$ removes some MSB, a WoP-PBS bootstraps the remaining low bits, the result is subtracted from the running ciphertext to peel off those bits, and the process repeats. This is analogous to BGV/BFV digit extraction [HS15, CH18]. Concretely, with chunk widths $\mathcal{L} = \{d_i\}$, scaling factors $\Delta_i = \Delta_{\mathrm{in}} \cdot 2^{\sum_{j=1}^{i-1} d_j}$ and $m_{\mathrm{in}}\Delta_{\mathrm{in}} = \sum_{i=0}^{\alpha-1} m_i \Delta_i$, iteration $i$ uses $\kappa_i = \sum_{j=i+1}^{\alpha-1} d_j$. Each bootstrap only needs $N$ large enough for one chunk, not for the whole message, giving complexity $C_{\mathrm{Decomp}} = \alpha\, C_{\mathrm{WoP\text{-}PBS1}} + \alpha(n+1)C_{\mathrm{add}} + \tfrac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{2}C_{\mathrm{add}}$, far better than scaling a single bootstrap to the whole precision. The full Algorithm 8 is in the Algorithms section.

Summing the $\mathrm{ct}_{\mathrm{out},i}$ after a Decomp gives a high-precision noise-reduction bootstrap (with the right parameters the output noise is smaller than the input). For a high-precision programmable bootstrap, Decomp efficiently produces the per-chunk ciphertexts that the Tree-PBS and ChainPBS algorithms of [GBA21] need; replacing the internal PBS calls of those algorithms with WoP-PBS removes their padding-bit requirement, yielding the Tree-WoP-PBS and Chained-WoP-PBS (Limitation B). These also implement the $\mathrm{AddMSB}_{2^p}$ and $\mathrm{MulMSB}_{2^p}$ operators.

Conclusion and Open Problems

The paper extends TFHE on several fronts simultaneously. A BFV-like GLWE/LWE multiplication (tensor product plus relinearization) is shown, via a thorough noise analysis, to be correct with practical TFHE parameters, giving a native LWE multiplication without a PBS (Limitation D). A generalized PBS bootstraps any chunk of bits and can evaluate $2^\vartheta$ LUTs at once for free (Limitations F, H). Their combination, the WoP-PBS, removes the padding-bit requirement (Limitation A) and is more parallel than the sequential PBS (Limitation C). Downstream, these enable leveled Boolean and modular-$2^p$ arithmetic with bootstraps only when noise demands it (Limitation G), exact large-integer arithmetic via carry extraction, a circuit bootstrapping faster by roughly a factor $\ell$ with no noise penalty, and large-precision (programmable) bootstrapping using far smaller parameters than scaling TFHE (Limitations B, E). All of this stays inside the TFHE context, without bridging to other schemes as Chimera does. The noise analysis also applies to BFV-like schemes.

Open problem. The main bottleneck remains the negacyclic polynomial convolutions: the most efficient FFT-over-64-bit-floats method inherently injects noise into ciphertexts, and wider floating-point representations collapse performance, so the study of alternative TFHE-compatible convolution methods is the principal avenue for further improvement.

Algorithms

At-a-glance pseudocode for every algorithm referenced above, including the supplementary-material listings (Algorithms 9 to 13). Inputs, outputs and numbered steps are reproduced; $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ denotes the inner product used in relinearization, and $[\,\cdot\,]_Q$ denotes computation at the larger modulus $Q$ before rounding back to $q$.

Algorithm 1: CT <- GLWEMult(CT1, CT2, RLK)

Context:
  S = (S_1, ..., S_k) in R_q^k          : a GLWE secret key
  Delta = min(Delta_1, Delta_2) in Z_q
  PT1 = M1 * Delta_1 in R_q,   PT2 = M2 * Delta_2 in R_q
