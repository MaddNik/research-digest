TFHE: Fast Fully Homomorphic Encryption over the Torus

At a glance

Authors: Ilaria Chillotti (imec-COSIC, KU Leuven), Nicolas Gama (LMV/UVSQ & Inpher), Mariya Georgieva (EPFL & Inpher), Malika Izabachène (CEA, LIST). This is the complete, unified journal version of the authors' two Asiacrypt papers (2016 and 2017), with full proofs and an experimental validation of the Gaussian-noise heuristic.

One sentence: TFHE rebuilds fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) directly over the real torus T = R/Z, abstracts the GSW scheme down to a single external product operation, and uses that to make both gate bootstrapping and a new circuit bootstrapping fast enough to be practical.

Headline numbers (single core):

  • Gate bootstrapping (one bootstrapped binary gate): 13 ms with a 16 MB bootstrapping key — vs. FHEW's prior 690 ms and 1 GB key, at the same ciphertext overhead and higher security.
  • A native bootstrapped MUX gate: 26 ms.
  • New circuit bootstrapping (LWE → low-noise RingGSW): 137 ms, making the leveled mode composable.
  • Evaluating an arbitrary function {0,1}10→{0,1} in leveled mode: 340 µs.

Keywords: Fully Homomorphic Encryption, Bootstrapping, Lattices, LWE, GSW, Boolean circuit, deterministic automata.

The problem and the core idea

FHE lets you compute on encrypted data. The catch is noise: every homomorphic operation grows the random noise that hides the plaintext, until decryption fails. Gentry's bootstrapping (2009) fixes this by homomorphically running the decryption circuit, producing a fresh ciphertext whose noise depends only on that circuit, not on the input — the one operation that can actually reduce noise. The simplest FHE is therefore just a NAND gate followed by a bootstrap, repeated.

TFHE's unifying move is to work entirely on the real torus T = R/Z (real numbers mod 1) rather than over integers mod q. The torus is a Z-module: you can add torus elements and multiply them by integers, which is exactly the structure LWE needs. This 'scale-invariant' view removes the modulus bookkeeping and a recurring √2 factor that had caused confusion in earlier implementations. Noise is always quantified by its variance / standard deviation, giving clean, additive propagation rules.

A single primitive — the phase, a secret linear function from the ciphertext space to the torus — defines the scheme abstractly. Swapping the phase's underlying structure recovers LWE, RingLWE, Module-LWE, and even scale-invariant approx-GCD or NTRU variants from one framework.

Building blocks: TLWE, TRLWE, TGSW and the external product

TFHE defines torus-native versions of the standard ciphertext types:

  • TLWE — scalar LWE samples over T (messages in T).
  • TRLWE — the ring variant over TN[X] = R[X]/(XN+1) mod 1, which packs N coefficients into one ciphertext.
  • TGSW / TRGSW — the GSW-style matrix ciphertexts, here recast over the torus, carrying integer (often binary) messages.

The central theoretical contribution is showing that the whole GSW machinery reduces to one operation: the external product ⊠, which multiplies a TGSW ciphertext (integer message) by a TRLWE ciphertext (torus message) to yield a TRLWE ciphertext of the product. The crucial property is that its noise growth is additive, not multiplicative — this is what keeps deep leveled circuits viable.

From the external product comes the CMux gate (controlled multiplexer): a TGSW control bit selects between two TRLWE data inputs. CMux is the bridge from low-level arithmetic to actual circuits, and it is the workhorse inside bootstrapping.

Glue operations: key switching, sample extraction, blind rotation

Three procedures let TFHE move data between its ciphertext types as needed:

  • Key switching (revisited and generalized): not just re-keying, but homomorphically evaluating arbitrary linear morphisms from any Z-module to TN[X]. This is how TFHE moves between scalar (T) and polynomial (TN[X]) message spaces — i.e. how it packs and unpacks data across slots. A private key-switching variant is used inside circuit bootstrapping.
  • Sample extraction: pulls a single coefficient of a packed TRLWE ciphertext out as a TLWE ciphertext, with no noise cost.
  • Blind rotation: multiplies the polynomial inside a TRLWE ciphertext by an encrypted power of X, rotating its coefficients by a secret amount. It is implemented as a sequence of CMux gates driven by the (encrypted) bits of the rotation, and it is the heart of the bootstrapping loop.

Gate bootstrapping mode (the FHE workhorse)

By re-expressing FHEW's bootstrapping purely in terms of the external product, and combining it with optimized parameters, TFHE bootstraps a binary gate in 13 ms on a single core with a 16 MB key. The pipeline is: blind-rotate a fixed test-polynomial by the (encrypted) phase of the input, extract the relevant coefficient, then key-switch back — producing a fresh, low-noise encryption of the gate's output.

All standard binary gates run in 13 ms; a single native bootstrapped MUX gate (which would otherwise cost two gates) runs in 26 ms. Despite the >50× speed-up over FHEW, the authors note bootstrapped bit operations are still ~109× slower than their plaintext equivalents — which motivates the leveled mode below for arithmetic-heavy workloads.

Leveled mode: packing, look-up tables, automata and the TBSR counter

For workloads that are not naturally SIMD, TFHE shows how to keep the N packing slots fully used. Section 5 introduces horizontal and vertical packing, demonstrated on homomorphic look-up-table (LUT) evaluation: an arbitrary function {0,1}10→{0,1} evaluates in 340 µs in leveled mode.

Two new computation models extend the expressivity of the leveled mode while keeping additive noise overhead:

  • Weighted / deterministic automata: generalizes the deterministic-automata logic of prior work to efficient leveled evaluation of weighted automata via chains of CMux gates.
  • TBSR counter (a new homomorphic counter): supports all the elementary operations that occur inside a multiplication (the schoolbook multi-addition / multiplication algorithms), so arithmetic functions can be evaluated in packed leveled mode cheaply.

Practical guidance from the paper: for leveled multiplication, the weighted-automata technique wins below ~128-bit operands and the TBSR counter wins above. In FHE (bootstrapped) mode, weighted automata beat gate-bootstrapping past 4-bit inputs, and TBSR overtakes for >64-bit inputs.

Circuit bootstrapping: making leveled mode composable

The leveled tricks above need their inputs as low-noise TRGSW ciphertexts, but bootstrapping naturally outputs TLWE ciphertexts. Circuit bootstrapping closes the loop: it converts a high-noise TLWE sample (amplitude up to ¼ over a binary space) into a low-noise TRGSW sample (amplitude < 2-20), in 137 ms (110-bit security parameter; benchmarked on a single-core Intel Core i7-4910MQ at 2.90 GHz).

Internally it spends ~70% of its time on level-2 operations and ~30% on private key-switchings. The payoff: TFHE's fast leveled operations become fully composable, so for arithmetic functions the leveled-plus-circuit-bootstrapping route beats the gate-bootstrapping route. The authors stress this is far cheaper than alternatives that output bootstrapped GSW ciphertexts directly, whose cost would be quadratic in the security parameter (days of computation vs. 0.137 s).

Figure 8 of the paper: the three encryption levels and the operations that move ciphertexts between them — Bootstrapping, private key-switching (PrivKS), SampleExtract, and pre-PubKS — which together make circuit bootstrapping (and hence the leveled mode) composable.

Security analysis and concrete parameters

The paper gives a dedicated, practical security analysis that ties the security level directly to two intrinsic quantities: the LWE error rate and the entropy of the secret key — deliberately minimizing the number of free parameters to avoid circular dependencies between them. Estimates use the LWE estimator and the parameters recommended in the Homomorphic Encryption Security Standard white paper.

  • Their lattice-reduction model is consistent with FHEW's claims (root-Hermite factor δ ≈ 1.0052 in their analysis vs. 1.0064 in FHEW — i.e. slightly conservative).
  • New gate-bootstrapping parameters (Table 4) achieve >128-bit security classically and >80-bit against quantum attacks.
  • The three TRLWE/TRGSW parameter levels used in the implementation (Table 5) provide at least 152-bit security.

FFT is used for the polynomial products; its accuracy was cross-checked against a slower exact Karatsuba implementation. The constructions rely on a circular-security assumption (the key-switching key encrypts its own key bits), which could be avoided in leveled mode at the cost of using many keys.

Conclusion and takeaways

TFHE delivers a single, clean framework — everything over the torus, GSW abstracted to one additive-noise external product — that improves every quality metric of a homomorphic scheme at once: expressivity, key size, running time, and ciphertext overhead. It contributes (1) a >50× faster gate bootstrapping (13 ms, 16 MB key), (2) richer leveled computation models (weighted automata, TBSR counter, packed LUTs) with additive noise, and (3) a fast circuit bootstrapping (137 ms) that makes the leveled mode composable. All of it ships in the open-source TFHE library with concrete, >128-bit-secure parameters. The work remains a foundation of modern practical FHE; open problems noted include further reducing bootstrapping cost and broadening the supported computation models.
