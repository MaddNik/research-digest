TFHE: Fast Fully Homomorphic Encryption over the Torus

IACR ePrint 2018/421 (unified journal version of the authors' Asiacrypt 2016 and 2017 papers) · full view ↗

At a glance

Authors: Ilaria Chillotti (imec-COSIC, KU Leuven), Nicolas Gama (LMV/UVSQ and Inpher), Mariya Georgieva (EPFL and Inpher), Malika Izabachène (CEA, LIST). This is the complete, unified journal version of the authors' two Asiacrypt papers (2016 and 2017), with full proofs and an experimental validation of the Gaussian-noise heuristic.

One sentence: TFHE rebuilds fully homomorphic encryption directly over the real torus $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$, abstracts the GSW scheme down to a single external product $\boxdot$ whose noise grows additively, and uses that to make both gate bootstrapping and a new circuit bootstrapping fast enough to be practical.

Headline numbers (single core, Intel Core i7-4910MQ at $2.90$ GHz, compared with the prior FHEW scheme):

  • Gate bootstrapping of one binary gate: 13 ms with a 16 MB bootstrapping key, versus FHEW's 690 ms and roughly 1 GB key, at the same ciphertext overhead and the same security parameter (about a $53\times$ speed-up and a $64\times$ smaller key).
  • A native bootstrapped MUX gate: 26 ms (which would otherwise cost two gate bootstrappings).
  • New circuit bootstrapping (high-noise TLWE to low-noise TRGSW): 137 ms, which makes the leveled mode composable.
  • Evaluating an arbitrary function $\{0,1\}^{10} \to \{0,1\}$ in leveled mode: 340 µs.

Keywords: Fully Homomorphic Encryption, Bootstrapping, Lattices, LWE, GSW, Boolean circuit, deterministic automata.

The problem and the core idea

Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) lets you compute on encrypted data. The catch is noise: every homomorphic operation grows the random error that hides the plaintext, until decryption fails. Gentry's bootstrapping (2009) is the fix - it homomorphically runs the decryption circuit on an encrypted ciphertext, producing a fresh ciphertext whose noise depends only on that circuit and not on the input's accumulated noise. It is the one operation that can actually reduce noise, and the simplest FHE is therefore just a universal gate (e.g. NAND) followed by a bootstrap, repeated.

TFHE's unifying move is to work entirely over the real torus $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ (real numbers modulo $1$) instead of over integers modulo $q$. The torus is a $\mathbb{Z}$-module: you can add two torus elements and multiply a torus element by an integer, which is exactly the structure that LWE needs, but you cannot multiply two torus elements together (there is no ring product). This scale-invariant view removes the modulus bookkeeping and a recurring $\sqrt{2}$ factor that had caused confusion in earlier implementations. Noise is always quantified by its variance or standard deviation, which gives clean additive propagation rules.

A single primitive defines the scheme abstractly: the phase $\varphi_s$, a secret $\kappa$-Lipschitz $\mathbb{Z}$-module morphism from the ciphertext space (a $\mathbb{Z}$-module $M$) to the torus. A ciphertext $c$ encrypts a message $\mu$ when its phase $\varphi_s(c)$ is concentrated around $\mu$; (approximate) decryption is just evaluating $\varphi_s(c)$, and the error is given by Equation 1:

$$\mathrm{Err}(c) = \varphi_s(c) - \mathrm{msg}(c).$$

Because the phase is a $\mathbb{Z}$-module morphism, the cryptosystem is additively homomorphic for free: $\varphi_s(c_1 + c_2) = \varphi_s(c_1) + \varphi_s(c_2)$. Choosing the algebraic structure underlying the phase recovers every standard variant from one framework: scalar LWE (set the ring degree $N = 1$), RingLWE (set the module rank $k = 1$), Module-LWE (intermediate $N, k$), and even scale-invariant approximate-GCD or NTRU variants.

Building blocks: TLWE, TRLWE, TGSW and the gadget decomposition

TFHE defines torus-native versions of the standard ciphertext types. In the canonical instantiation, fix $N$ a power of two and $k \ge 1$, and write $\mathbb{T}_N[X] = \mathbb{R}[X]/(X^N+1) \bmod 1$ for the ring of torus polynomials, $\mathbb{Z}_N[X] = \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^N+1)$, and $\mathbb{B}_N[X]$ for the polynomials with binary coefficients.

  • TLWE (scalar LWE over the torus). A sample is a pair $(\mathbf{a}, b) \in \mathbb{T}^n \times \mathbb{T}$ with phase $\varphi_s(\mathbf{a}, b) = b - \mathbf{s} \cdot \mathbf{a}$ for a binary secret $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{B}^n$. A fresh (homogeneous) sample of $0$ is $(\mathbf{a},\, \mathbf{s}\cdot\mathbf{a} + e)$ with $e$ drawn from a centered modular Gaussian of standard deviation $\alpha$.
  • TRLWE (the ring variant over $\mathbb{T}_N[X]$). A sample is $(\mathbf{a}, b) \in \mathbb{T}_N[X]^k \times \mathbb{T}_N[X]$ with phase $\varphi_s(\mathbf{a}, b) = b - \mathbf{s} \cdot \mathbf{a}$ and key $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{B}_N[X]^k$. A single TRLWE ciphertext packs $N$ torus slots. The canonical TLWE problem interpolates between LWE ($N=1$) and RingLWE ($k=1$), exactly the torus analogue of General- / Module-LWE.
  • TGSW / TRGSW (the GSW-style matrix ciphertexts, recast over the torus). A TGSW sample of message $\mu_A$ has the form $A = Z_A + \mu_A \cdot H$, where $Z_A$ is a stack of $(k+1)\ell$ TRLWE encryptions of $0$ and $H \in \mathcal{M}_{(k+1)\ell,\,k+1}(\mathbb{T}_N[X])$ is the fixed gadget matrix. Its message is an integer (often binary) polynomial in $\mathbb{Z}_N[X]$.

The gadget matrix $H$ stacks the powers $1/B_g, 1/B_g^2, \dots, 1/B_g^\ell$ of a fixed base $B_g$ across $k+1$ blocks. It comes with an efficient approximate gadget decomposition $\mathrm{Dec}_{H,\beta,\epsilon}$ (Algorithm 1) of quality $\beta$ and precision $\epsilon$: given a TLWE/TRLWE sample $v$ it returns a small-coefficient vector $u$ with $\lVert u \rVert_\infty \le \beta$ (typically $\beta = B_g/2$) and $\lVert u \cdot H - v \rVert_\infty \le \epsilon$ (typically $\epsilon = 1/(2B_g^\ell)$). This single decomposition is what keeps GSW noise growth small. Algorithm 1 is reproduced below.

Algorithm 1  Gadget Decomposition of a TLWE/TRLWE sample
Input:  a sample v = (a_1, ..., a_k, b), gadget base Bg, depth l,
        with gadget matrix H (quality beta, precision epsilon)
Output: u = [u_{1,1}, ..., u_{k+1,l}] in Z_N[X]^{(k+1)l} such that
        ||u||_inf <= beta and ||u . H - v||_inf <= epsilon
  1: for each polynomial component a_i of v (i = 1 .. k+1):
  2:    round a_i to the nearest multiple of 1/Bg^l
  3:    write the rounded value in base Bg as digits u_{i,1}, ..., u_{i,l}
  4:    re-center each digit into (-Bg/2, Bg/2]
  5: return the concatenated digit vector u

The external product and the CMux gate

The central theoretical contribution is showing that the whole GSW machinery reduces to one operation, the external product $\boxdot$, which multiplies a TGSW ciphertext (integer message) by a TLWE/TRLWE ciphertext (torus message) to yield a ciphertext of the product. The defining identity is Equation 2:

$$\boxdot : \mathrm{TGSW} \times \mathrm{TLWE} \longrightarrow \mathrm{TLWE}, \qquad A \boxdot b = \mathrm{Dec}_{H,\beta,\epsilon}(b) \cdot A.$$

Only one operand is decomposed (unlike the original GSW product, which decomposes both), and the result is a TLWE sample of message $\mu_A \cdot \mu_b$. The crucial property is that the noise grows additively, not multiplicatively. The worst-case amplitude bound is Equation 3:

$$\lVert \mathrm{Err}(A \boxdot b) \rVert_\infty \le (k+1)\,\ell N \beta\, \lVert \mathrm{Err}(A) \rVert_\infty + \lVert \mu_A \rVert_1 (1 + kN)\,\epsilon + \lVert \mu_A \rVert_1\, \lVert \mathrm{Err}(b) \rVert_\infty .$$

If $\lVert \mathrm{Err}(A \boxdot b) \rVert_\infty \le 1/4$ then the output is a valid sample. Under the independence heuristic, the average-case bound replaces amplitudes by variances, giving Equation 4:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(A \boxdot b)) \le (k+1)\,\ell N \beta^2\, \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(A)) + (1 + kN)\,\lVert \mu_A \rVert_2^2\, \epsilon^2 + \lVert \mu_A \rVert_2^2\, \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(b)) .$$

The classical internal TGSW product $\boxtimes$ is just $(k+1)\ell$ independent external products. This retroactively explains why long chains of fresh multiplications and branching algorithms are far cheaper with GSW than balanced binary multiplication trees: only the former map onto the cheap, additive external product.

From the external product comes the CMux gate (controlled multiplexer), the bridge from low-level arithmetic to actual circuits and the workhorse inside bootstrapping. A TGSW control bit $C$ selects between two TRLWE data inputs $d_0, d_1$, as in Equation 5:

$$\mathrm{CMux}(C, d_1, d_0) = C \boxdot (d_1 - d_0) + d_0, \qquad \mathrm{msg}(\mathrm{CMux}) = \mathrm{msg}(C)\,?\,\mathrm{msg}(d_1):\mathrm{msg}(d_0).$$

Its noise (Lemma 3.16) is again additive, as in Equation 6:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(\mathrm{CMux})) \le \max\bigl(\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(d_0)), \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(d_1))\bigr) + \vartheta(C),$$

with $\vartheta(C) = (k+1)\ell N \beta^2\, \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(C)) + (kN+1)\,\epsilon^2$. Figure 1 shows the gate, its inputs (a TGSW control bit plus two TLWE/TRLWE data wires), its output message $\mu = \mu_C \cdot (\mu_{d_1} - \mu_{d_0}) + \mu_{d_0}$, and the worst-case ($\eta$) and average-case ($\vartheta$) noise propagation.

Glue operations: key switching, sample extraction, blind rotation

Three procedures move data between TFHE's ciphertext types as needed.

Key switching, revisited and generalized, is not just re-keying: it homomorphically evaluates arbitrary linear morphisms $f$ from a $\mathbb{Z}$-module to $\mathbb{T}_N[X]$. This is how TFHE moves between the scalar space $\mathbb{T}$ and the polynomial space $\mathbb{T}_N[X]$, i.e. how it packs and unpacks data across slots. There is a public functional key switch (Algorithm 2), where $f$ is a public $\mathbb{R}$-Lipschitz morphism, and a private functional key switch (Algorithm 3), where the morphism $f$ itself is part of the secret key-switching key. The private variant is used inside circuit bootstrapping. Both add only additive noise, governed by the precision $t$ and base of the key-switching keys.

Sample extraction. Given a TRLWE sample $c \in \mathrm{TRLWE}_K(\mu)$ and a position $p \in \{0, \dots, N-1\}$, $\mathrm{SampleExtract}_p(c)$ pulls out the single coefficient $\mu_p$ of the packed polynomial as a scalar TLWE sample under the same key, with no added noise (its error variance is at most that of $c$). The scalar TLWE key $K \in \mathbb{B}^{kN}$ and the TRLWE key $K \in \mathbb{B}_N[X]^k$ are reinterpretations of the same coefficient sequence.

Blind rotation (Algorithm 4) multiplies the polynomial encrypted in a TRLWE ciphertext by an encrypted power of $X$, rotating its coefficients by a secret amount $\rho = b - \sum_{i=1}^{p} s_i a_i \bmod 2N$. It first rotates by the publicly known power $X^{-b}$, then folds in the secret part through a chain of $p$ CMux gates driven by the encrypted bits $s_i$ (each multiplying the accumulator by $X^{a_i}$ when $s_i = 1$). It is the heart of the bootstrapping loop, and its noise (Theorem 4.3) accumulates additively over the $p$ CMux steps, as in Equation 7:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(\mathrm{ACC})) \le \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(c)) + p\bigl[(k+1)\ell N \beta^2\, \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(C_i)) + (kN+1)\,\epsilon^2\bigr].$$

Algorithm 4  BlindRotate
Input:  a TRLWE sample c of v in T_N[X] with key K,
        p+1 integer coefficients a_1, ..., a_p, b in Z/(2N Z),
        p TRGSW samples C_1, ..., C_p of s_1, ..., s_p in B with key K
Output: a TRLWE sample of X^{-rho} . v under key K,
        where rho = b - sum_{i=1..p} s_i . a_i  mod 2N
  1: ACC <- X^{-b} . c
  2: for i = 1 to p do
  3:    ACC <- CMux(C_i, X^{a_i} . ACC, ACC)
  4: end for
  5: return ACC

Gate bootstrapping mode (the FHE workhorse)

By re-expressing FHEW's bootstrapping purely in terms of the external product and combining it with optimized parameters, TFHE bootstraps a binary gate in 13 ms on one core with a 16 MB key. The TLWE-to-TLWE core (Algorithm 9) builds a fixed test vector (a constant-of-sign function encoded in a polynomial), given by Equation 8:

$$v := \left(1 + X + \dots + X^{\bar N - 1}\right) \cdot X^{\bar N / 2} \cdot \mu \;\in\; \mathbb{T}_{\bar N}[X],$$

then blind-rotates it by the rescaled, rounded phase $\tilde\varphi = \tilde b - \sum_{i} \tilde a_i s_i \bmod 2\bar N$ of the input, and finally sample-extracts the constant coefficient. The construction guarantees that the extracted message is $0$ iff $\lvert \varphi_K(c) \rvert < \tfrac14$ and $\mu_1$ otherwise, which is exactly the sign/selection test that refreshes the bit.

Algorithm 9  Bootstrapping TLWE-to-TLWE (calls Algorithm 4)
Input:  a constant mu_1 in T,
        a TLWE sample c = (a, b) in TLWE_{K, eta}(x . 1/2), with x in B,
        a bootstrapping key BK = (BK_i)_{i in [1, n]}
Output: a TLWE sample c_bar in TLWE_{K_bar, eta_bar}(x . mu_1)
  1: let mu = (1/2) . mu_1 in T          (pick one of the two possible values)
  2: let b_tilde = round(2 N_bar . b),  a_i_tilde = round(2 N_bar . a_i) in Z
  3: let v := (1 + X + ... + X^{N_bar - 1}) . X^{N_bar/2} . mu  in T_{N_bar}[X]
  4: ACC <- BlindRotate((0, v),
                         (a_1_tilde, ..., a_n_tilde, b_tilde),
                         (BK_1, ..., BK_n))
  5: return (0, mu) + SampleExtract(ACC)

The full gate bootstrapping (Algorithm 10) chains Algorithm 9 with a public key switch back to the original key. Parameters are chosen so the refreshed noise satisfies $\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(c')) < \tfrac{1}{16}$. A bootstrapped NAND of two samples $c_1, c_2$ over the message space $\{0, \tfrac14\}$ is computed as $c = (0, \tfrac58) - c_1 - c_2$ followed by a bootstrap; the other binary gates use analogous affine pre-combinations.

Algorithm 10  Gate Bootstrapping (calls Algorithms 9 and 2)
Input:  a constant mu_1 in T,
        a TLWE sample c = (a, b) in TLWE_{K, eta}(x . 1/2),
        a bootstrapping key BK, a key-switching key KS
Output: a TLWE sample c' = (a', b') in TLWE_{K, eta}(x . mu_1)
  1: c_bar <- BootstrappingTLWEtoTLWE(mu_1, c, BK)
  2: return c' <- PublicKeySwitchingTLWEtoTLWE(c_bar, Identity, KS)

All standard binary gates run in 13 ms; a single native bootstrapped MUX runs in 26 ms (avoiding the two bootstraps a naive composition would need). Despite the more than $50\times$ speed-up over FHEW, the authors note bootstrapped bit operations are still about $10^9\times$ slower than their plaintext equivalents, which motivates the leveled mode below for arithmetic-heavy workloads.

Leveled mode: packing and look-up tables

For workloads that are not naturally SIMD, Section 5 shows how to keep the $N$ packing slots fully used, demonstrated on homomorphic look-up-table (LUT) evaluation. An arbitrary function $f : \{0,1\}^d \to \{0,1\}^s$ is encoded as a table of values $\sigma_{j,h}$ and evaluated as a CMux binary decision tree of depth $d$ driven by the encrypted input bits $C_0, \dots, C_{d-1}$, where $C_i \in \mathrm{TRGSW}_K(x_i)$ and the integer input is $x = \sum_{i=0}^{d-1} x_i 2^i$.

Two packing directions reuse the slots (Figure 2):

  • Horizontal packing (batching) evaluates all $s$ outputs together by packing up to $N$ LUT values into one TRLWE sample. The number of CMux gates is $\left\lceil \tfrac{s}{N} \right\rceil (2^d - 1)$; this is optimal when $s$ is a multiple of $N$, but wasteful when $s \le N$ since then evaluation is only $s$ times faster than the unpacked approach.
  • Vertical packing packs along the table of a single output instead, combining a blind rotation (Algorithm 4, to bring the right entry to position $0$) with sample extraction. Algorithm 5 evaluates one output $f_j(x)$ with $2^d/N - 1$ CMux gates plus one blind rotation, which dominates the cost for large tables.

The two can be mixed (the dashed square in Figure 2). An arbitrary function $\{0,1\}^{10} \to \{0,1\}$ evaluates in 340 µs in leveled mode. In Figure 2 the green (horizontal) and red (vertical) rectangles mark the two packing directions over the table, and the right-hand part is the CMux decision tree that evaluates the sub-function $f_j$.

Leveled computation models: weighted automata and the TBSR counter

Two new computation models extend the expressivity of the leveled mode while keeping additive noise overhead.

Deterministic weighted finite automata (det-WFA). Generalizing the deterministic-automata logic of prior work, a weighted automaton is evaluated by a chain of CMux gates that propagate weights backwards from the accepting depth $d$ to the initial state $i$ (Algorithm 6). The output is a TRLWE encryption of the word weight $w(\sigma)$, and the noise grows only linearly with the path length, as in Equation 9:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(c_{j,q})) \le (d-j)\,(k+1)\ell N \beta^2\, \vartheta_{\mathrm{TRGSW}} + (d-j)\,(kN+1)\,\epsilon^2 .$$

Algorithm 6  Evaluation of a det-WFA
Input:  a det-WFA A = (Q, i, B, T); T_0(q), T_1(q) are the states reached on
        reading bit 0 / 1 from q, with weights w_0(q), w_1(q),
        d valid TRGSW samples C_0, ..., C_{d-1} of the bits of word sigma
Output: a TRLWE encryption of w(sigma)
  1: for each q in Q accessible at depth d do
  2:    set c_{d,q} := 0
  3: end for
  4: for j = d-1 down to 0 do
  5:    for each q in Q accessible at depth j do
  6:       set c_{j,q} := CMux( C_j,
                                c_{j+1, T_1(q)} + (0, w_1(q)),
                                c_{j+1, T_0(q)} + (0, w_0(q)) )
  7:    end for
  8: end for
  9: return c_{0,i}

TBSR counter. Building on a Block Sequence Representation (BSR) of integers, the new homomorphic TBSR counter supports all the elementary operations that occur inside a multiplication: TBSR multi-addition (Algorithm 7) and TBSR multiplication (Algorithm 8), including the integer division by two used in the schoolbook algorithms. This lets arithmetic functions be evaluated in packed leveled mode cheaply; Algorithm 8 computes all the product bits with $N > 2d$.

Practical guidance from the paper: for leveled multiplication, the weighted-automata technique wins below roughly $128$-bit operands and the TBSR counter wins above. In fully homomorphic (bootstrapped) mode, weighted automata beat gate bootstrapping past $4$-bit inputs, and TBSR overtakes for inputs larger than $64$ bits.

Circuit bootstrapping: making leveled mode composable

The leveled tricks above need their control inputs as low-noise TRGSW ciphertexts, but bootstrapping naturally outputs TLWE ciphertexts. Circuit bootstrapping (Algorithm 11) closes the loop: it converts a high-noise level-0 TLWE sample (amplitude up to $\tfrac14$ over a binary message space) into a low-noise level-1 TRGSW sample (amplitude below $2^{-20}$), in 137 ms at the $110$-bit security level (benchmarked on a single-core Intel Core i7-4910MQ at $2.90$ GHz).

It works across three encryption levels (Figure 3). For each gadget row $w = 1, \dots, \ell$ it runs one TLWE-to-TLWE bootstrapping (Algorithm 9) targeting message $1/B_g^{w}$, then applies $k+1$ private functional key switches (Algorithm 3) with the morphisms $f_u(x) = -K_u \cdot x$ for $u \le k$ (and $f_{k+1}(x) = x$) to assemble the TRGSW matrix entries. The resulting message of row entry $(u,w)$ is given by Equation 10:

$$\mathrm{msg}\bigl(c^{(u,w)}\bigr) = \mu \cdot \frac{K_u}{B_g^{w}}, \qquad \text{with } K_{k+1} := 1 .$$

In total it performs exactly $\ell$ bootstrappings and $\ell(k+1)$ private key switches.

Algorithm 11  Circuit Bootstrapping (calls Algorithms 9 and 3)
Input:  a level-0 TLWE sample c = (a, b) in TLWE_{K, eta}(mu . 1/2), mu in B,
        a bootstrapping key BK = (BK_i in TRGSW_{K_bar, alpha_bar}(K_i))_{i in [1, n]},
        k+1 private key-switch keys KS^{(f_u)} for f_u(x) = -K_u . x (u <= k),
        and f_{k+1}(x) = 1 . x
Output: a level-1 TRGSW sample C in TRGSW_K(mu)
  1: for w = 1 to l do
  2:    c_bar^{(w)} <- Bootstrapping_{BK, 1/Bg^w}(c)
  3:    for u = 1 to k+1 do
  4:       c^{(u,w)} <- PrivKS(KS^{(f_u)}, c_bar^{(w)})
  5:    end for
  6: end for
  7: return C = (c^{(u,w)})_{1 <= u <= k+1, 1 <= w <= l}

Internally it spends about $70\%$ of its time on level-2 operations and $30\%$ on the private key switchings. The payoff: TFHE's fast leveled operations become fully composable, so for arithmetic functions the leveled-plus-circuit-bootstrapping route beats the gate-bootstrapping route. The authors stress this is far cheaper than alternatives that output bootstrapped GSW ciphertexts directly, whose cost would be quasi-quadratic in the security parameter (days of computation versus $0.137$ s). Figure 3 shows the three levels (with the bar / no-bar / under-bar key notation $\bar K$, $K$, $\underline{K}$) and the operations that move ciphertexts between them: Bootstrapping, private key switch (PrivKS), SampleExtract, and pre-PubKS, with the circuit-bootstrapping stages highlighted in blue.

Security analysis and concrete parameters

The paper gives a dedicated, practical security analysis that ties the security level $\lambda$ directly to two intrinsic quantities: the LWE error rate $\alpha$ and the entropy $n$ of the secret key, deliberately minimizing the number of free parameters to avoid circular dependencies. Estimates use the LWE estimator and the parameters recommended in the Homomorphic Encryption Security Standard white paper.

  • Their lattice-reduction model is consistent with FHEW's claims: a root-Hermite factor $\delta \approx 1.0052$ in their analysis versus $1.0064$ in FHEW, i.e. slightly conservative.
  • New gate-bootstrapping parameters (Table 4) achieve more than $128$-bit security classically and more than $80$-bit against quantum attacks: the switching key uses $n = 612$, $\alpha = 2^{-15}$ ($\lambda_{\mathrm{classic}} = 128$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{quantum}} = 90$) and the bootstrapping key uses $N = 1024$, $\alpha = 2^{-26}$ ($\lambda_{\mathrm{classic}} = 129$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{quantum}} = 88$). The earlier Table 3 set (re-evaluating FHEW's parameters) used $n = 500$ at $\alpha \approx 2.43 \cdot 10^{-5}$.
  • The three TRLWE/TRGSW parameter levels used in the circuit-bootstrapping implementation (Table 5) provide at least $152$-bit security: level $0$ uses $n = 500$, $\alpha = 6.10 \cdot 10^{-5}$ ($\lambda = 194$); the level-$1$ and level-$2$ ring parameters use rank $k = 1$ with $N = 1024$, and decomposition base $B_g$ and depth $\ell$ chosen per level.

FFT is used for the polynomial products; its floating-point accuracy was cross-checked against a slower exact Karatsuba implementation, and the average-case noise theorems were validated experimentally against the Gaussian-noise heuristic (the independence assumption that lets variances add). The worst-case, infinity-norm theorems do not use this heuristic at all. The constructions rely on a circular-security assumption (the key-switching key encrypts its own key bits); the authors note this assumption is needed even in the leveled mode, and could only be avoided by supplying the keys as a chain with a fresh key per level.

Empirical comparison: which technique wins when

Figure 4 is the empirical basis for the technique-selection guidance. The $y$ axis is running time in seconds (log scale) and the $x$ axis is the input bit-width (linear for the LUT plots a and b, log scale for c-f). The left column is leveled homomorphic encryption, the right column is fully homomorphic (bootstrapped). The rows cover LUT evaluation, the $\max$ function, and integer multiplication, each comparing the no-packing baseline, gate bootstrapping, horizontal / vertical packing, det-WFA, and TBSR.

Each sub-plot is annotated with its cost formula in the elementary times $t_{XP}$ (external product), $t_{GB}$ (gate bootstrap), $t_{CB}$ (circuit bootstrap), and $t_{KS}$ (key switch). For leveled multiplication, the no-packing cost is $\left(d^4 + o(d^4)\right) t_{XP}$, the det-WFA cost is $\Theta(d^3)\, t_{XP}$, and the TBSR cost is $2d^2\, t_{XP} + (2d-2)\, t_{KS}$; the crossover where TBSR overtakes det-WFA is visible in plots (e) and (f). These curves are what justify the rule of thumb: det-WFA below roughly $128$-bit operands, TBSR above, and circuit bootstrapping making the leveled route competitive against gate bootstrapping for arithmetic.

Conclusion and takeaways

TFHE delivers a single, clean framework - everything over the torus with GSW abstracted down to one additive-noise external product - that improves every quality metric of a homomorphic scheme at once: expressivity, key size, running time, and ciphertext overhead. Its three main contributions are (1) a more than $50\times$ faster gate bootstrapping (13 ms, 16 MB key), (2) richer leveled computation models (weighted automata, the TBSR counter, packed LUTs) with additive noise, and (3) a fast circuit bootstrapping (137 ms) that makes the leveled mode composable. All of it ships in the open-source TFHE library with concrete, more-than-$128$-bit-secure parameters. The work remains a foundation of modern practical FHE; open problems the authors note include further reducing bootstrapping cost and broadening the supported computation models.

Algorithm 2  Public Functional Key Switching  (public R-Lipschitz f)
Input:  p TLWE samples c_i under key K, key-switch key KS_{K->K'},
        public morphism f from a Z-module to T_N[X]
Output: a TRLWE/TLWE sample of f(msg(c_1), ..., msg(c_p)) under key K'
  1: compute the linear combination prescribed by f over the c_i
  2: gadget-decompose the key-dependent coefficients
  3: subtract the matching KS key columns to switch K -> K'
  4: return the assembled sample under K'
Algorithm 3  Private Functional Key Switching  (secret morphism f)
Input:  p TLWE samples c_i under key K, private key-switch key KS^{(f)}
        (which embeds the secret morphism f)
Output: a TRLWE/TLWE sample of f(msg(c_1), ..., msg(c_p)) under key K'
  1: gadget-decompose each input coefficient
  2: accumulate the corresponding columns of the secret KS^{(f)} key
  3: return the assembled sample under K'
Algorithm 4  BlindRotate
Input:  TRLWE sample c of v with key K,
        coefficients a_1, ..., a_p, b in Z/(2N Z),
        TRGSW samples C_1, ..., C_p of bits s_1, ..., s_p under key K
Output: TRLWE sample of X^{-rho} . v,  rho = b - sum_i s_i a_i  mod 2N
  1: ACC <- X^{-b} . c
  2: for i = 1 to p do
  3:    ACC <- CMux(C_i, X^{a_i} . ACC, ACC)
  4: end for
  5: return ACC
Algorithm 5  Vertical-Packing LUT evaluation of one output f_j
Input:  TRGSW samples C_0, ..., C_{d-1} of the input bits,
        the packed table for output j
Output: TLWE sample of f_j(x)
  1: select the right block of N table entries via a CMux tree
     ( 2^d / N - 1 CMux gates )
  2: BlindRotate that block to bring the wanted entry to position 0
  3: return SampleExtract_0( ACC )
Algorithm 6  Evaluation of a det-WFA
Input:  det-WFA A = (Q, i, B, T) with transitions T_0,T_1 and weights w_0,w_1,
        TRGSW samples C_0, ..., C_{d-1} of the input word bits
Output: TRLWE encryption of w(sigma)
  1: for each q accessible at depth d: set c_{d,q} := 0
  2: for j = d-1 down to 0 do
  3:    for each q accessible at depth j do
  4:       c_{j,q} := CMux( C_j,
                            c_{j+1,T_1(q)} + (0, w_1(q)),
                            c_{j+1,T_0(q)} + (0, w_0(q)) )
  5:    end for
  6: end for
  7: return c_{0,i}
Algorithm 7  TBSR Multi-Addition   (sketch)
Input:  several TBSR-encoded integers (Block Sequence Representation)
Output: a TBSR encoding of their sum
  1: add corresponding blocks slot-wise (homomorphic torus addition)
  2: propagate carries via CMux on the encrypted carry bits
  3: re-normalize blocks (including the division-by-two step)
  4: return the resulting TBSR sequence
Algorithm 8  TBSR Multiplication   (requires N > 2d)
Input:  two TBSR-encoded d-bit integers
Output: TBSR encoding holding all product bits
  1: form the partial products (shifts of one operand)
  2: combine them with TBSR multi-addition (Algorithm 7)
  3: normalize and return all product bits
Algorithm 9  Bootstrapping TLWE-to-TLWE
Input:  constant mu_1 in T,
        TLWE sample c = (a, b) in TLWE_{K, eta}(x . 1/2), x in B,
        bootstrapping key BK = (BK_i)_{i in [1, n]}
Output: TLWE sample c_bar in TLWE_{K_bar, eta_bar}(x . mu_1)
  1: mu = (1/2) . mu_1
  2: b_tilde = round(2 N_bar . b);  a_i_tilde = round(2 N_bar . a_i)
  3: v := (1 + X + ... + X^{N_bar-1}) . X^{N_bar/2} . mu
  4: ACC <- BlindRotate((0, v), (a_1_tilde,...,a_n_tilde, b_tilde), BK)
  5: return (0, mu) + SampleExtract(ACC)
Algorithm 10  Gate Bootstrapping
Input:  constant mu_1, TLWE sample c, bootstrapping key BK, key-switch key KS
Output: refreshed TLWE sample c' in TLWE_{K, eta}(x . mu_1)
  1: c_bar <- BootstrappingTLWEtoTLWE(mu_1, c, BK)
  2: return c' <- PublicKeySwitchingTLWEtoTLWE(c_bar, Identity, KS)

  (NAND of c1, c2 over {0, 1/4}:  bootstrap (0, 5/8) - c1 - c2 )
Algorithm 11  Circuit Bootstrapping
Input:  level-0 TLWE sample c of mu in B,
        bootstrapping key BK,
        private key-switch keys KS^{(f_u)}, f_u(x) = -K_u . x (u <= k),
        f_{k+1}(x) = x
Output: level-1 TRGSW sample C in TRGSW_K(mu)
  1: for w = 1 to l do
  2:    c_bar^{(w)} <- Bootstrapping_{BK, 1/Bg^w}(c)
  3:    for u = 1 to k+1 do
  4:       c^{(u,w)} <- PrivKS(KS^{(f_u)}, c_bar^{(w)})
  5:    end for
  6: end for
  7: return C = (c^{(u,w)})_{1 <= u <= k+1, 1 <= w <= l}
