TFHE: Fast Fully Homomorphic Encryption over the Torus

PDF (text-layer, figures rasterized from vector diagrams) — IACR ePrint 2018/421, retrieved via Wayback Machine · full view

At a glance

Authors: Ilaria Chillotti (imec-COSIC, KU Leuven), Nicolas Gama (LMV/UVSQ and Inpher), Mariya Georgieva (EPFL and Inpher), Malika Izabachène (CEA, LIST). This is the complete, unified journal version of the authors' two Asiacrypt papers (2016 and 2017), with full proofs and an experimental validation of the Gaussian-noise heuristic.

One sentence: TFHE rebuilds fully homomorphic encryption directly over the real torus $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$, abstracts the GSW scheme down to a single external product $\boxdot$ whose noise grows additively, and uses that to make both gate bootstrapping and a new circuit bootstrapping fast enough to be practical.

Headline numbers (single core, vs. the prior FHEW scheme [29]):

  • Gate bootstrapping of one binary gate: 13 ms with a 16 MB bootstrapping key, versus FHEW's 690 ms and 1 GB key, at the same ciphertext overhead and the same security parameter (a roughly $53\times$ speed-up and a $64\times$ smaller key).
  • A native bootstrapped MUX gate: 26 ms (otherwise two gates).
  • New circuit bootstrapping (high-noise TLWE to low-noise TRGSW): 137 ms, which makes the leveled mode composable.
  • Evaluating an arbitrary function $\{0,1\}^{10} \to \{0,1\}$ in leveled mode: 340 µs.

Keywords: Fully Homomorphic Encryption, Bootstrapping, Lattices, LWE, GSW, Boolean circuit, deterministic automata.

The problem and the core idea

FHE lets you compute on encrypted data. The catch is noise: every homomorphic operation grows the random error that hides the plaintext, until decryption fails. Gentry's bootstrapping (2009) is the fix: it homomorphically runs the decryption circuit, producing a fresh ciphertext whose noise depends only on that circuit, not on the input. It is the one operation that can actually reduce noise, and the simplest FHE is therefore just a NAND gate followed by a bootstrap, repeated.

TFHE's unifying move is to work entirely over the real torus $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ (real numbers modulo $1$) instead of over integers modulo $q$. The torus is a $\mathbb{Z}$-module: you can add torus elements and multiply them by integers, which is exactly the structure that LWE needs, but you cannot multiply two torus elements together. This scale-invariant view removes the modulus bookkeeping and a recurring $\sqrt{2}$ factor that had caused confusion in earlier implementations. Noise is always quantified by its variance or standard deviation, giving clean additive propagation rules.

A single primitive defines the scheme abstractly: the phase $\varphi_s$, a secret $\kappa$-Lipschitz linear function from the ciphertext space (a $\mathbb{Z}$-module $M$) to the torus. A ciphertext $c$ encrypts message $\mu$ when its phase $\varphi_s(c)$ is concentrated around $\mu$; (approximate) decryption is just evaluating $\varphi_s(c)$, and the error is $\mathrm{Err}(c) = \varphi_s(c) - \mu$. Because the phase is linear, the cryptosystem is additively homomorphic for free. Swapping the structure underlying the phase recovers LWE (set $N = 1$), RingLWE (set $k = 1$), Module-LWE (intermediate), and even scale-invariant approx-GCD or NTRU variants, all from one framework.

Building blocks: TLWE, TRLWE, TGSW and the gadget decomposition

TFHE defines torus-native versions of the standard ciphertext types. In the canonical instantiation, fix $N$ a power of two and $k \ge 1$, and write $\mathbb{T}_N[X] = \mathbb{R}[X]/(X^N+1) \bmod 1$ for the ring of torus polynomials.

  • TLWE (scalar LWE over the torus). A sample is $(\mathbf{a}, b) \in \mathbb{T}^n \times \mathbb{T}$ with phase $\varphi_s(\mathbf{a}, b) = b - \mathbf{s} \cdot \mathbf{a}$ for a binary secret $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{B}^n$; a homogeneous sample is $(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{s}\cdot\mathbf{a} + e)$ with $e$ drawn from a modular Gaussian of standard deviation $\alpha$.
  • TRLWE (the ring variant over $\mathbb{T}_N[X]$). A sample is $(\mathbf{a}, b) \in \mathbb{T}_N[X]^k \times \mathbb{T}_N[X]$ with phase $\varphi_s(\mathbf{a}, b) = b - \mathbf{s} \cdot \mathbf{a}$. A single TRLWE ciphertext packs $N$ torus slots. The canonical TLWE problem of Definition 3.2 is exactly the analogue of General-/Module-LWE over the torus, interpolating between LWE ($N=1$) and RingLWE ($k=1$).
  • TGSW / TRGSW (the GSW-style matrix ciphertexts, recast over the torus). A TGSW sample of message $\mu_A$ has the form $A = Z_A + \mu_A \cdot H$, where $Z_A$ is a stack of TRLWE encryptions of zero and $H \in \mathcal{M}_{(k+1)\ell,\,k+1}(\mathbb{T}_N[X])$ is the fixed gadget matrix. Its message is an integer (often binary) polynomial.

The gadget matrix comes with an efficient approximate gadget decomposition $\mathrm{Dec}_{H,\beta,\epsilon}$ (Algorithm 1) of quality $\beta$ and precision $\epsilon$: given a TLWE/TRLWE sample $v$ it returns a small-coefficient vector $u$ with $\lVert u \rVert_\infty \le \beta$ and $\lVert u \cdot H - v \rVert_\infty \le \epsilon$. This single decomposition is what keeps GSW noise growth small. Algorithm 1 is reproduced below.

Algorithm 1  Gadget Decomposition of a TLWE sample
Input:  a TLWE sample v, gadget base Bg, depth l, with H the gadget matrix
Output: u = [u_{1,1}, ..., u_{k+1,l}] in R^{(k+1)l} such that
        ||u||_inf <= beta and ||u . H - v||_inf <= epsilon
  for each polynomial component a_i of v (i = 1..k+1):
    round a_i to the nearest multiple of 1/Bg^l
    write the rounded value in base Bg as digits u_{i,1}, ..., u_{i,l}
    re-center each digit into (-Bg/2, Bg/2]
  return the concatenated digit vector u

The external product and the CMux gate

The central theoretical contribution is showing that the whole GSW machinery reduces to one operation, the external product $\boxdot$, which multiplies a TGSW ciphertext (integer message) by a TLWE/TRLWE ciphertext (torus message) to yield a ciphertext of the product. Definition 3.12 is simply

$$\boxdot : \mathrm{TGSW} \times \mathrm{TLWE} \longrightarrow \mathrm{TLWE}, \qquad (A, b) \longmapsto A \boxdot b = \mathrm{Dec}_{H,\beta,\epsilon}(b) \cdot A.$$

Only one vector is decomposed (unlike the original GSW product, which decomposes both operands), and the result is a TLWE sample of message $\mu_A \cdot \mu_b$. The crucial property (Theorem 3.13, worst case) is that the noise grows additively, not multiplicatively:

$$\lVert \mathrm{Err}(A \boxdot b) \rVert_\infty \le (k+1)\,\ell N \beta\, \lVert \mathrm{Err}(A) \rVert_\infty + \lVert \mu_A \rVert_1 (1 + kN)\,\epsilon + \lVert \mu_A \rVert_1\, \lVert \mathrm{Err}(b) \rVert_\infty .$$

If $\lVert \mathrm{Err}(A \boxdot b) \rVert_\infty \le 1/4$ the output is a valid sample. Under the independence heuristic 3.11 the average-case bound (Corollary 3.14) replaces amplitudes by variances:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(A \boxdot b)) \le (k+1)\,\ell N \beta^2 \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(A)) + (1 + kN)\,\lVert \mu_A \rVert_2^2\, \epsilon^2 + \lVert \mu_A \rVert_2^2\, \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(b)) .$$

The classical internal TGSW product $\boxtimes$ (Corollary 3.15) is just $(k+1)\ell$ independent external products, which retroactively explains why long chains of fresh multiplications and branching algorithms are far cheaper with GSW than balanced binary trees: only the former map onto the cheap plaintext external product.

From the external product comes the CMux gate (controlled multiplexer), the bridge from low-level arithmetic to actual circuits and the workhorse inside bootstrapping. A TGSW control bit $C$ selects between two TRLWE data inputs $d_0, d_1$:

$$\mathrm{CMux}(C, d_1, d_0) = C \boxdot (d_1 - d_0) + d_0, \qquad \mathrm{msg}(\mathrm{CMux}) = \mathrm{msg}(C)\,?\,\mathrm{msg}(d_1):\mathrm{msg}(d_0).$$

Its noise (Lemma 3.16) is again additive: $\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(\mathrm{CMux})) \le \max(\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(d_0)), \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(d_1))) + \vartheta(C)$, with $\vartheta(C) = (k+1)\ell N \beta^2 \mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(C)) + (kN+1)\epsilon^2$. Figure 1 shows the gate, its inputs (TGSW control plus two TLWE data wires), its output message $\mu = \mu_C \cdot (\mu_{d_1} - \mu_{d_0}) + \mu_{d_0}$, and the worst-case ($\eta$) and average-case ($\vartheta$) noise propagation.

Glue operations: key switching, sample extraction, blind rotation

Three procedures move data between TFHE's ciphertext types as needed.

Key switching, revisited and generalized, is not just re-keying: it homomorphically evaluates arbitrary linear morphisms $f$ from a $\mathbb{Z}$-module to $\mathbb{T}_N[X]$. This is how TFHE moves between the scalar space $\mathbb{T}$ and the polynomial space $\mathbb{T}_N[X]$, i.e. how it packs and unpacks data across slots. There is a public functional key switch (Algorithm 2) and a private functional key switch (Algorithm 3, where the function itself is secret); the private variant is used inside circuit bootstrapping. Both add only additive noise.

Sample extraction. Given a TRLWE sample $c \in \mathrm{TRLWE}_K(\mu)$ and a position $p$, $\mathrm{SampleExtract}_p(c)$ pulls out the single coefficient $\mu_p$ as a scalar TLWE sample under the same key, with no added noise (its error variance is at most that of $c$). The binary TLWE key $K \in \mathbb{B}^n$ and the TRLWE key $K \in \mathbb{B}_N[X]^k$ are reinterpretations of the same coefficient sequence.

Blind rotation (Algorithm 4) multiplies the polynomial encrypted in a TRLWE ciphertext by an encrypted power of $X$, rotating its coefficients by a secret amount $\rho = b - \sum_{i=1}^{p} s_i a_i \bmod 2N$. It first rotates by the known power $X^{-b}$, then by the secret part through a chain of CMux gates driven by the encrypted bits $s_i$. It is the heart of the bootstrapping loop, and its noise (Theorem 4.3) accumulates additively over the $n$ CMux steps.

Algorithm 4  BlindRotate
Input:  A TRLWE sample c of v in T_N[X] with key K.
        p+1 integer coefficients a_1, ..., a_p, b in Z/(2N Z)
        p TRGSW samples C_1, ..., C_p of s_1, ..., s_p in B with key K
Output: A TRLWE sample of X^{-rho} . v
        where rho = b - sum_{i=1..p} s_i . a_i mod 2N, with key K
  1: ACC <- X^{-b} . c
  2: for i = 1 to p
  3:    ACC <- CMux(C_i, X^{a_i} . ACC, ACC)
  4: return ACC

Gate bootstrapping mode (the FHE workhorse)

By re-expressing FHEW's bootstrapping purely in terms of the external product and combining it with optimized parameters, TFHE bootstraps a binary gate in 13 ms on one core with a 16 MB key. The TLWE-to-TLWE core (Algorithm 9) builds a fixed test vector $v := (1 + X + \dots + X^{\bar N - 1}) \cdot X^{\bar N / 2} \cdot \mu$, blind-rotates it by the rescaled, rounded phase $\tilde\varphi = \tilde b - \sum_{i} \tilde a_i s_i \bmod 2N$ of the input, then sample-extracts the constant coefficient. The construction guarantees $\mu = 0$ iff $\lvert \varphi_K(c) \rvert < \tfrac14$ and $\mu = \mu_1$ otherwise, which is the sign/selection test that refreshes the bit.

Algorithm 9  Bootstrapping TLWE-to-TLWE (calling Algorithm 4)
Input:  a constant mu_1 in T, a TLWE sample c = (a,b) in TLWE_{K,eta}(x . 1/2),
        x in B, a bootstrapping key BK = (BK_i)_{i in [1,n]}
Output: a TLWE sample c_bar in TLWE_{K_bar, eta_bar}(x . mu_1)
  1: Let mu = (1/2) mu_1 in T          (pick one of the two possible values)
  2: Let b_tilde = round(2 N_bar . b),  a_i_tilde = round(2 N_bar . a_i) in Z
  3: Let v := (1 + X + ... + X^{N_bar - 1}) . X^{N_bar/2} . mu  in T_{N_bar}[X]
  4: ACC <- BlindRotate((0, v), (a_1_tilde, ..., a_n_tilde, b_tilde), (BK_1, ..., BK_n))
  5: return (0, mu) + SampleExtract(ACC)

The full gate bootstrapping (Algorithm 10) chains Algorithm 9 with a public key switch back to the original key. Parameters are chosen so the refreshed noise satisfies $\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(c')) < \tfrac{1}{16}$. A bootstrapped NAND of two samples $c_1, c_2$ with message space $\{0, \tfrac14\}$ is then computed as $c = (0, \tfrac58) - c_1 - c_2$ followed by a bootstrap.

Algorithm 10  Gate Bootstrapping (calling Algorithms 9 and 2)
Input:  a constant mu_1 in T, a TLWE sample c = (a,b) in TLWE_{K,eta}(x . 1/2),
        a bootstrapping key BK, a key-switching key KS
Output: a TLWE sample c' = (a',b') in TLWE_{K,eta}(x . mu_1)
  1: c_bar <- BootstrappingTLWEtoTLWE(mu_1, c, BK)
  2: return c' <- PublicKeySwitchingTLWEtoTLWE(c_bar, Identity, KS)

All standard binary gates run in 13 ms; a single native bootstrapped MUX runs in 26 ms. Despite the more than $50\times$ speed-up over FHEW, the authors note bootstrapped bit operations are still about $10^9\times$ slower than their plaintext equivalents, which motivates the leveled mode below for arithmetic-heavy workloads.

Leveled mode: packing and look-up tables

For workloads that are not naturally SIMD, Section 5 shows how to keep the $N$ packing slots fully used, demonstrated on homomorphic look-up-table (LUT) evaluation. An arbitrary function $f : \{0,1\}^d \to \{0,1\}^s$ is encoded as a table of values $\sigma_{j,h}$ and evaluated as a CMux binary decision tree of depth $d$ driven by the encrypted input bits $C_0, \dots, C_{d-1}$, where $C_i \in \mathrm{TRGSW}_K(x_i)$ and $x = \sum_{i=0}^{d-1} x_i 2^i$.

Two packing directions reuse the slots (Figure 2):

  • Horizontal packing (batching) evaluates all $s$ outputs together by packing up to $N$ LUT values into one TRLWE sample. The number of CMux gates is $\lceil \tfrac{s}{N} \rceil (2^d - 1)$; optimal when $s$ is a multiple of $N$, but wasteful when $s \le N$ since then evaluation is only $s$ times faster than the unpacked approach.
  • Vertical packing packs along the table of a single output instead, combining a blind rotation (Algorithm 4, to bring the right entry to position $0$) with sample extraction. Algorithm 5 evaluates $f_j(x)$ with $2^d/N - 1$ CMux gates plus one blind rotation, which dominates for large tables.

The two can be mixed (the dashed square in Figure 2). An arbitrary function $\{0,1\}^{10} \to \{0,1\}$ evaluates in 340 µs in leveled mode. In Figure 2 the green (horizontal) and red (vertical) rectangles mark the two packing directions over the table, and the right-hand part is the CMux decision tree that evaluates the sub-function $f_j$.

Leveled computation models: weighted automata and the TBSR counter

Two new computation models extend the expressivity of the leveled mode while keeping additive noise overhead.

Deterministic weighted finite automata (det-WFA). Generalizing the deterministic-automata logic of prior work, a weighted automaton is evaluated by a chain of CMux gates that propagate weights backwards from the accepting depth $d$ to the initial state $i$ (Algorithm 6). The output is a TRLWE encryption of the word weight $w(\sigma)$, and the noise grows only linearly with the path length: $\mathrm{Var}(\mathrm{Err}(c_{j,q})) \le (d-j)(k+1)\ell N \beta^2 \vartheta_{\mathrm{TRGSW}} + (d-j)(kN+1)\epsilon^2$.

Algorithm 6  Evaluation of a det-WFA
Input:  A det-WFA A = (Q, i, B, T). T_0(q), T_1(q) are the states reached on
        reading bit 0 / 1 from q, with weights w_0(q), w_1(q).
        d valid TRGSW samples C_0, ..., C_{d-1} of the bits of word sigma.
Output: A TRLWE encryption of w(sigma)
  1: for each q in Q accessible at depth d do
  2:    set c_{d,q} := 0
  3: end for
  4: for j = d-1 down to 0 do
  5:    for each q in Q accessible at depth j do
  6:       set c_{j,q} := CMux( C_j,
                                c_{j+1, T_1(q)} + (0, w_1(q)),
                                c_{j+1, T_0(q)} + (0, w_0(q)) )
  7:    end for
  8: end for
  9: return c_{0,i}

TBSR counter. Building on a Block Sequence Representation (BSR) of integers, the new homomorphic TBSR counter supports all the elementary operations that occur inside a multiplication: TBSR multi-addition (Algorithm 7) and TBSR multiplication (Algorithm 8), including the integer division by two used in the schoolbook algorithms. This lets arithmetic functions be evaluated in packed leveled mode cheaply; Algorithm 8 computes all the product bits with $N > 2d$.

Practical guidance from the paper. For leveled multiplication, the weighted-automata technique wins below roughly $128$-bit operands and the TBSR counter wins above. In fully homomorphic (bootstrapped) mode, weighted automata beat gate bootstrapping past $4$-bit inputs, and TBSR overtakes for inputs larger than $64$ bits.

Circuit bootstrapping: making leveled mode composable

The leveled tricks above need their control inputs as low-noise TRGSW ciphertexts, but bootstrapping naturally outputs TLWE ciphertexts. Circuit bootstrapping (Algorithm 11) closes the loop: it converts a high-noise level-0 TLWE sample (amplitude up to $\tfrac14$ over a binary message space) into a low-noise level-1 TRGSW sample (amplitude below $2^{-20}$), in 137 ms at the $110$-bit security level (benchmarked on a single-core Intel Core i7-4910MQ at 2.90 GHz).

It works across three encryption levels (Figure 3). For each gadget row $w = 1, \dots, \ell$ it runs one TLWE-to-TLWE bootstrapping (Algorithm 9) targeting message $1/B_g^{w}$, then applies $k+1$ private functional key switches (Algorithm 3) with the morphisms $f_u(x) = -K_u \cdot x$ (and $f_{k+1}(x) = x$) to assemble the TRGSW matrix entries $\mathrm{msg}(\mu \cdot h_i) = \mu K_u / B_g^{w}$. In total it performs exactly $\ell$ bootstrappings and $\ell(k+1)$ private key switches.

Algorithm 11  Circuit Bootstrapping (calling Algorithms 9 and 3)
Input:  A level-0 TLWE sample c = (a,b) in TLWE_{K,eta}(mu . 1/2), mu in B,
        a bootstrapping key BK = (BK_i in TRGSW_{K_bar, alpha_bar}(K_i))_{i in [1,n]},
        k+1 private key-switch keys KS^{(f_u)} for f_u(x) = -K_u . x (u <= k),
        and f_{k+1}(x) = 1 . x.
Output: A level-1 TRGSW sample C in TRGSW_K(mu)
  1: for w = 1 to l
  2:    c_bar^{(w)} <- Bootstrapping_{BK, 1/Bg^w}(c)
  3:    for u = 1 to k+1
  4:       c^{(u,w)} = PrivKS(KS^{(f_u)}, c_bar^{(w)})
  5: return C = (c^{(u,w)})_{1 <= u <= k+1, 1 <= w <= l}

Internally it spends about $70\%$ of its time on level-2 operations and $30\%$ on private key switchings. The payoff: TFHE's fast leveled operations become fully composable, so for arithmetic functions the leveled-plus-circuit-bootstrapping route beats the gate-bootstrapping route. The authors stress this is far cheaper than alternatives that output bootstrapped GSW ciphertexts directly, whose cost would be quasi-quadratic in the security parameter (days of computation versus 0.137 s). Figure 3 shows the three levels (with the bar/no-bar/under-bar key notation $\bar K$, $K$, $\underline{K}$) and the operations that move ciphertexts between them: Bootstrapping, private key switch (PrivKS), SampleExtract, and pre-PubKS, with the circuit-bootstrapping stages highlighted in blue.

Security analysis and concrete parameters

The paper gives a dedicated, practical security analysis that ties the security level $\lambda$ directly to two intrinsic quantities, the LWE error rate $\alpha$ and the entropy $n$ of the secret key, deliberately minimizing the number of free parameters to avoid circular dependencies. Estimates use the LWE estimator and the parameters recommended in the Homomorphic Encryption Security Standard white paper.

  • Their lattice-reduction model is consistent with FHEW's claims: a root-Hermite factor $\delta \approx 1.0052$ in their analysis versus $1.0064$ in FHEW, i.e. slightly conservative.
  • New gate-bootstrapping parameters (Table 4) achieve more than $128$-bit security classically and more than $80$-bit against quantum attacks.
  • The three TRLWE/TRGSW parameter levels used in the implementation (Table 5) provide at least $152$-bit security.

FFT is used for the polynomial products; its accuracy was cross-checked against a slower exact Karatsuba implementation, and the average-case noise theorems were validated experimentally against the Gaussian-noise heuristic. The constructions rely on a circular-security assumption (the key-switching key encrypts its own key bits), which could be avoided in leveled mode at the cost of using many keys.

Empirical comparison: which technique wins when

Figure 4 is the empirical basis for the technique-selection guidance. The $y$ axis is running time in seconds (log scale) and the $x$ axis is the input bit-width (linear for the LUT plots a and b, log scale for c-f). The left column is leveled homomorphic encryption, the right column is fully homomorphic (bootstrapped). The rows cover LUT evaluation, the $\max$ function, and integer multiplication, each comparing the no-packing baseline, gate bootstrapping, horizontal/vertical packing, det-WFA, and TBSR.

Each sub-plot is annotated with its cost formula in the elementary times $t_{XP}$ (external product), $t_{GB}$ (gate bootstrap), $t_{CB}$ (circuit bootstrap), and $t_{KS}$ (key switch). For example, leveled multiplication costs $(d^4 + o(d^4))\,t_{XP}$ with no packing, $\Theta(d^3)\,t_{XP}$ with det-WFA, and $2d^2 t_{XP} + (2d-2)\,t_{KS}$ with TBSR; the crossover where TBSR overtakes det-WFA is visible in plots (e) and (f). These curves are what justify the rule of thumb: det-WFA below roughly $128$-bit operands, TBSR above, and circuit bootstrapping making the leveled route competitive against gate bootstrapping for arithmetic.

Conclusion and takeaways

TFHE delivers a single, clean framework, everything over the torus with GSW abstracted to one additive-noise external product, that improves every quality metric of a homomorphic scheme at once: expressivity, key size, running time, and ciphertext overhead. Its three main contributions are (1) a more than $50\times$ faster gate bootstrapping (13 ms, 16 MB key), (2) richer leveled computation models (weighted automata, the TBSR counter, packed LUTs) with additive noise, and (3) a fast circuit bootstrapping (137 ms) that makes the leveled mode composable. All of it ships in the open-source TFHE library with concrete, more-than-$128$-bit-secure parameters. The work remains a foundation of modern practical FHE; open problems the authors note include further reducing bootstrapping cost and broadening the supported computation models.
