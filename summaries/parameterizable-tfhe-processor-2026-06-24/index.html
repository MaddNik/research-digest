Towards a Functionally Complete and Parameterizable TFHE Processor

PDF (text-layer), arXiv:2510.23483 · full view · arXiv preprint (arXiv:2510.23483v2, 26 Dec 2025)

Overview and Motivation

Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) lets arbitrary functions be evaluated directly on encrypted data, which makes it attractive for securing outsourced and multiparty computation. TFHE is a fast, torus-based FHE scheme built on the learning with errors (LWE) hardness assumption. It supports native linear operations and, through programmable bootstrapping (PBS), the evaluation of arbitrary non-linear lookup tables. TFHE currently offers the fastest bootstrapping of any FHE scheme, yet homomorphic circuit evaluation remains several orders of magnitude slower than the equivalent plaintext computation, which is the main barrier to adoption.

Prior FPGA accelerators mostly offload only the single most expensive step (typically the blind rotation inside PBS) while leaving the rest of the computation on conventional hardware. This forces large ciphertexts and keys to be shuttled across the host-to-FPGA boundary, so overall throughput and scalability become dominated by external memory bandwidth, with prior designs requiring several gigabytes per second. The authors (Reyes Haeusler, Ott, Jayasena, and Peter, from the University of Oldenburg and the University of Tennessee) propose a fully FPGA-based TFHE accelerator that performs PBS entirely on the FPGA and keeps the keys on chip, eliminating that bandwidth bottleneck. Their improved bootstrapping module delivers $240\%$ to $480\%$ more bootstrappings per second than the current state of the art. Code and artifacts are open at the project repository.

Preliminaries and Notation

The torus is written $\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$, and $\mathbb{Z}_q$ denotes the integers modulo $q$. The working ring is the cyclotomic quotient ring $\mathfrak{R}_q^{N} = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^N + 1)$ of polynomials of degree $\lt N$ with coefficients in $\mathbb{Z}_q$, where $N$ is a power of two. The scheme rests on the generalized LWE (GLWE) assumption, which captures both plain LWE (the case $N = 1$) and its ring variant RLWE (the case $N \gt 1$). A GLWE sample under secret key $\mathbf{s}$ with Gaussian noise $\chi_\sigma$ has the form shown in Equation 1, where $\mathbf{a}$ is sampled uniformly and $e \leftarrow \chi_\sigma$.

$$(\mathbf{a}, b) \quad \text{with} \quad b = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{s}\rangle + e$$

A message $m$ is encrypted by scaling it with $\Delta = \lfloor q/(2p) \rfloor$ to push it into the most significant bits, then adding it to the noise of a GLWE sample, giving the ciphertext in Equation 2. Decryption computes $m = \lfloor (b - \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{s}\rangle)/\Delta \rceil$ and is correct as long as $\lVert e \rVert_\infty \lt \Delta/2$.

$$(\mathbf{a},\, b = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{sk}\rangle + \Delta m + e)$$

The relevant parameters are the LWE mask dimension $n$, the RLWE polynomial degree $N$ and mask dimension $k$, the plaintext modulus $p$, the ciphertext modulus $q$, the decomposition base $\beta$, and the decomposition depth $\ell$. TFHE additionally needs GLev and GGSW ciphertext types, a bootstrapping key $\mathrm{BSK}$ (consisting of RGSW ciphertexts encrypting the bits of the LWE key under the RLWE key), and a key-switching key $\mathrm{KSK}$. A decomposition with base $\beta$ and depth $\ell$ splits a value $x$ into $(x_1, \dots, x_\ell)$ that approximately reconstruct $x$ as in Equation 3.

$$\tilde{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{\ell} x_i\, \frac{q}{\beta^i}$$

TFHE Operations: External Product, CMUX, and PBS

Addition is native to GLWE ciphertexts: adding two encryptions of $m_1$ and $m_2$ yields an encryption of $m_1 + m_2$, with noise growing linearly. Multiplication is realized through the external product, which takes a GGSW ciphertext $\hat{\hat{C}}$ encrypting $m_1$ and a GLWE ciphertext $C$ encrypting $m_2$ and returns an encryption of $m_1 \cdot m_2$, as defined in Equation 4.

$$C \boxdot \hat{\hat{C}} = \sum_{i=0}^{k} \sum_{j=1}^{\ell} \mathrm{Decomp}_{\beta,\ell}(C)_{i,j} \cdot \hat{\hat{C}}_{i,j}$$

The controlled multiplexer (CMUX) selects between two GLWE ciphertexts $C_0, C_1$ using a GGSW-encrypted selector bit $\hat{\hat{C}}$, as in Equation 5.

$$\mathrm{CMUX}(\hat{\hat{C}}, C_0, C_1) = C_0 - (C_0 + C_1) \boxdot \hat{\hat{C}}$$

Programmable bootstrapping consists of a blind rotation followed by a sample extraction. Given an RLWE accumulator $acc$ encrypting a redundant lookup-table polynomial $F$, and the LWE ciphertext $(\mathbf{a}, b)$ to bootstrap, the blind rotation first sets $acc = acc \cdot X^{-b}$ and then iterates the CMUX update in Equation 6 over each mask coefficient $a_i \in \mathbf{a}$.

$$acc = \mathrm{CMUX}(\mathrm{BSK}_i,\, acc,\, acc \cdot X^{a_i})$$

This rotates the coefficient holding $\mathrm{LUT}[m]$ into the $0$-th position, so that the table value can be read out by sample extraction into a fresh LWE ciphertext under the RLWE key reinterpreted as an LWE key (mask dimension $kN$). The full PBS thus evaluates an arbitrary lookup table for free during noise refresh. Key switching then returns the bootstrapped ciphertext to the original LWE key $\mathbf{sk}$ via Equation 7.

$$(0, \dots, 0, b) - \sum_{i=0}^{kN-1} \sum_{j=1}^{\ell} \mathrm{Decomp}_{\beta,\ell}(a_i)_j \cdot \mathrm{KSK}_{i,j}$$

Core Arithmetic: Modular Reduction

Modular reduction keeps every intermediate value inside $\mathbb{Z}_q$. The design uses two flavors. A simple reduction handles additions and subtractions, where the result can only fall outside $[0, q)$ by at most $q-1$, so a single conditional add or subtract of $q$ restores it. A full reduction handles products. The authors avoid costly Barrett or Montgomery reductions by adopting the 64-bit NTT-friendly Solinas prime in Equation 8.

$$q = 2^{64} - 2^{32} + 1$$

Because $2^{96} \equiv -1 \pmod q$ and $2^{64} \equiv 2^{32} - 1 \pmod q$, a 128-bit product split into four 32-bit segments $a, b, c, d$ reduces with only three additions and one shift, as derived in Equation 9, followed by one conditional addition to land in $\mathbb{Z}_q$.

$$v = a\cdot 2^{96} + b\cdot 2^{64} + c\cdot 2^{32} + d \equiv (b + c)\cdot 2^{32} + (-a - b + d) \pmod q$$

Scalar multiplication uses the Karatsuba identity in Equation 10 at recursion depth two, cutting the multiplications per level from four to three while balancing logic usage.

$$x \cdot y = x_1 y_1\, 2^{\ell} + (x_1 y_0 + x_0 y_1)\, 2^{\ell/2} + x_2 y_2$$

The Number Theoretic Transform

The dominant cost in TFHE is negacyclic polynomial multiplication; a single PBS requires $n \cdot (k+1) \cdot (k+1) \cdot \ell$ such multiplications. The design accelerates this with a Number Theoretic Transform (NTT) over the Solinas prime field, giving exact finite-field arithmetic and avoiding the rounding errors and elevated decryption-failure risk of fixed-point FFT approaches such as FPT. Working over $\mathfrak{R}_q^N = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^N + 1)$ requires a negacyclic reduction, obtained by twisting the roots of unity: an $N$-point DFT is evaluated at $\omega^i \cdot \psi$ using a $2N$-th primitive root of unity $\psi$ with $\psi^2 = \omega$.

The transform is built from two butterfly types, shown in Figure 1. A Cooley-Tukey (CT) butterfly takes a twiddle factor $\psi^k$ and two values $a_0, a_1$ and computes $a_0' = a_0 + \psi^k a_1$ and $a_1' = a_0 - \psi^k a_1$, producing bit-reversed output from normal-ordered input. A Gentleman-Sande (GS) butterfly computes $\hat{a}_0' = \hat{a}_0 + \hat{a}_1$ and $\hat{a}_1' = (\hat{a}_0 - \hat{a}_1)\,\psi^{-k}$, consuming bit-reversed input and producing normal-ordered output. Using CT butterflies for the forward NTT and GS butterflies (with inverted twiddle factors and a final scaling by $N^{-1}$) for the inverse NTT removes any need for a bit-reversal permutation network.

Butterflies are grouped into per-stage butterfly blocks. For throughput $T$, a block contains $T/2$ butterflies so that $T$ coefficients are processed per cycle, with each butterfly reading its precomputed twiddle factors sequentially from a dedicated memory. The architecture decomposes an $N$-point transform into smaller sub-transforms until reaching a fully parallel $T$-point final stage, as illustrated in Figure 2. Careful scheduling shrinks buffering: the $i$-th stage performs an $N/2^i$-point NTT $(i+1)$ times, so it needs only an output buffer of size $N/2^i$. Inter-stage memory reuse and direct streaming let one-quarter of all values bypass buffering entirely. The total buffering across the $\log(N/T)$ stages is given in Equation 11.

$$\frac{3}{4}N \cdot \sum_{i=0}^{\log(N/T)} 2^{-i}$$

External Product and Blind Rotation

The external product module is built in five parts: decomposition, NTT, element-wise multiplication of transformed polynomials, accumulation, and inverse NTT. The pipelined decomposition module, whose structure for $\ell = 2$ is shown in Figure 3, rounds and removes $\log q - \ell \cdot \log \beta$ bits, splits the remaining $\ell$ blocks of $\log \beta$ bits with carry propagation, and applies a simple reduction to stay in $\mathbb{Z}_q$. The resulting $\ell$ polynomials are transformed by $\ell$ parallel NTT modules and stored in a ping-pong Multi Polynomial Buffer. The design instantiates $(k+1)\times\ell$ element-wise multipliers (each performing $T$ scalar multiplications per cycle, so a polynomial multiply takes $N/T$ iterations) so all polynomial-RLWE products run in parallel, with $\mathrm{BSK}_i$ precomputed in NTT space and pre-multiplied by the inverse-NTT rescaling factor to save resources. Products are summed in a block adder tree over $\log((k+1)\times\ell)$ steps before the final inverse NTT.

The blind rotation iterates over the bootstrapping-key elements and the LWE mask coefficients. Each iteration computes the update in Equation 12, which is split into an external product $acc' = acc \boxdot \mathrm{BSK}_i$ followed by the finalization $acc = acc' \cdot X^{a_i} + acc - acc'$.

$$acc = (acc \boxdot \mathrm{BSK}_i)\cdot(X^{a_i} - 1) + acc$$

The block diagram of one blind-rotation iteration is shown in Figure 4. Because $acc'$ is an RLWE ciphertext, multiplication by $X^{a_i}$ is a negacyclic polynomial rotation: a rotation module rearranges coefficients $g_0, \dots, g_{N-1}$ into $-g_{N-a_i}, \dots, -g_{N-1}, g_0, \dots, g_{N-a_i-1}$, where each wrapped coefficient is negated as $q - g_i$. This time-domain rotation is chosen over an NTT-domain rotation, which would need large run-time lookup tables because the $a_i$ values are only known at run time. The result $acc'$ is held in a ping-pong buffer while $acc - acc'$ is computed concurrently, and the output feeds back into itself for $n$ iterations. The final result on inputs $\mathrm{BSK}$, $acc$, and $(\mathbf{a}, b)$ encrypting $\Delta m$ with error $e$ is an RLWE encryption of $acc \cdot X^{\Delta m + e}$.

Programmable Bootstrapping Module

The programmable bootstrapping module wraps an initialization step, the blind rotation, and a sample extraction, and is pipelined to process multiple ciphertexts at once. Its block diagram is shown in Figure 5. The PBS pipeline is fed an input ciphertext address, the bootstrapping-key address, the sample-extract index, and the output address; addresses for pipelined operations are propagated through buffer chains. Crucially, the bootstrapping-key address is not fixed (supporting different keys) and the sample-extract index is variable rather than fixed to $0$. The high-level PBS flow is reproduced below.

PBS(LWE ciphertext (a, b), acc encoding LUT, BSK, extract index h):
    # Initialization
    acc = FetchLUT(LUT address)            # RLWE encryption of redundant F
    acc = RotatePolynomialRight(acc, b)    # multiply all polynomials by X^(-b)

    # Blind rotation: n iterations over mask a = (a_0, ..., a_{n-1})
    for i = 0 to n - 1:
        acc_prime = ExternalProduct(acc, BSK_i)            # acc &boxdot; BSK_i
        acc = RotatePolynomialLeft(acc_prime, a_i) + acc - acc_prime

    # Sample extraction reuses the polynomial-rotation logic
    (a_prime, b_prime) = SampleExtract(acc, h)
    return (a_prime, b_prime)             # LWE ct under Sk', mask dim kN

The initialization module takes the body $b$ and the lookup-table address, negacyclically rotates all polynomials of $acc$ by $X^{-b}$ using the rotation module adapted from the blind rotation, and feeds the blind rotation. Sample extraction reads coefficient $h$ out of the rotated RLWE ciphertext, following the rearrangement in Equation 13 for the extracted mask elements $a'_{iN+j}$, and reuses the same polynomial-rotation logic.

$$a'_{iN+j} = \begin{cases} d_{i,\,h-j} & \text{if } 0 \le j \le h, \\ -d_{i,\,h-j} & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

Key Switching, MulAdd, and Programmability

A single dual-purpose module provides both key switching and a fused multiply-add (MulAdd), shown in Figure 6. The key-switch path decomposes the incoming mask coefficients in a pipeline, multiplies them by the corresponding key-switch-key entries, and accumulates in an addition tree to compute Equation 7. The $\mathrm{KSK}_{i,j}$ entries are kept negated so that the whole subtraction from $(0, b)$ becomes one large sum without sign switching. Because key switching is faster than bootstrapping, its throughput is matched to the PBS module so that pipelining is eased; for PBS throughput $T_{BS}$ the key-switch throughput is set as in Equation 14, padding the input so the value is not fractional.

$$T_{KS} \ge \frac{T_{BS}}{n}$$

The spare cycles are repurposed for MulAdd: an is_ks flag deactivates the accumulation and returns additions and multiplications directly, and subtraction uses the modular representation of a negative scaling factor. The step that turns the accelerator into a processor is programmability. Each module is given data addresses to fetch its operands from memory rather than receiving all data from outside, which is what lets a custom instruction set be interpreted and executed on the FPGA instead of the device exposing a single fixed function. A minimal instruction set of PBS, AddMul, and KS needs only two instruction bits plus three 64-bit data addresses and $\log N$ bits for the sample-extract index. In the current prototype the PBS module is fully implemented and the dual-purpose key-switch/MulAdd module is designed and shown feasible, but the key-switch module itself, the instruction module, and a compiler are left as future work. Ciphertext-ciphertext multiplication is not a dedicated operation; as in the tfhe-rs library, homomorphic multiplication is done by concatenating messages and performing a PBS with an appropriately constructed lookup table.

On-Chip Design and Bandwidth

Keeping the whole computation and the keys on chip collapses the external bandwidth requirement. A processor instruction needs $3 \cdot 64$ bits for data addresses, $\log N$ bits for the sample-extract index, and two bits for the instruction itself. Because the keys stay on the FPGA, for the largest tested parameter sets at the highest achieved PBS rate the design needs only about $3$ megabits per second of external bandwidth, versus the several gigabytes per second required by prior offloading designs.

The dominant internal bandwidth cost is loading the large RGSW bootstrapping-key elements; this is amortized by batched bootstrapping (as in FPT), which keeps each key element in use longer so that more clock cycles are available to load the next element. For the highest-performing configuration ($T = 32$ at $325$ MHz, with $k = 1$, $\ell = 2$, $n = 500$) a minimum batch size of nine yields about $93$ GB/s internal bandwidth for the PBS module plus $73$ GB/s for the key-switch module, totaling roughly $166$ GB/s, which the on-chip high-bandwidth memory can supply.

Experimental Setup

The design is implemented and evaluated on an AMD Virtex UltraScale+ HBM VCU128 FPGA, which has two HBM stacks providing up to $460$ GB/s aggregate bandwidth, manufactured on a $20$ nm process. The host communicates with the FPGA over a PCIe Gen2 x8 link ($16$ GT/s) using the XDMA IP core and memory-mapped I/O; a modified tfhe-rs library on the host drives the XDMA driver. On the FPGA, the PCIe endpoint interfaces with the XDMA core, a custom controller coordinates command and data transactions, and an on-chip crossbar gives the TFHE processor concurrent access to both HBM stacks. Results: NTT and Bootstrapping Throughput

To compare fairly across platforms and parameter choices, the authors baseline on the blind rotation (the heaviest, most widely implemented operation), scale prior 32-bit designs by a factor of two to account for 64-bit arithmetic, and normalize decryption failure rates (DFR) to a common $2^{-64}$ by squaring the DFR per repeated computation, scaling by the factor in Equation 15.

$$z = \frac{\log_2(\mathrm{DFR}_x)}{-64}$$

NTT (1024-point). Running at $600$ MHz with $T = 2$, the 64-bit negacyclic NTT achieves about $1172$ NTTs per millisecond at $1.17\ \mu s$ latency, the best frequency and throughput among the compared designs. It is only narrowly beaten on latency by YYKKP, which uses over $7\times$ more DSPs, $5\times$ more flip-flops, and $7\times$ more LUTs at half the bit width. Against HLW (same 64-bit modulus) it delivers roughly $9\times$ the throughput and $6\times$ lower latency, and it outperforms KL, Proteus, and CM by around $4\times$.

PBS on the standard parameter set ($n = 500$, $N = 1024$, $k = 1$, $\ell = 2$, $\log\beta = 10$, DFR $2^{-64}$). The $T = 32$ configuration runs at $325$ MHz and reaches $10156$ PBS per second at 64-bit ($20312$ normalized 32-bit equivalent), over $3.4\times$ the throughput of the next fastest FPGA design (FPT) and more than $5.8\times$ other FPGA implementations, with comparable total resource use. At the best performance-per-resource point it matches ALT, KL, and YKP on PBS rate while using far fewer resources, with about $18\%$ higher throughput. Reported PBS/s scale across configurations from $1123$ at $T = 2$ to $10156$ at $T = 32$ (64-bit), with frequency falling from $575$ MHz to $325$ MHz as $T$ grows because of longer interconnect and routing congestion (a standalone $T = 8$ NTT nearly hits $600$ MHz, indicating inter-block routing rather than the NTT limits the clock). Energy efficiency is second-best ($140$ to $171$ PBS per watt), behind only ALT; latency trails ALT and FPT, partly because ALT uses smaller composite-NTT multiplications and key unrolling that this design does not yet adopt.

Results: Scalability and Comparison to CPU and GPU

On the demanding large parameter set from Ohba et al. ($n = 800$, $N = 16384$, $k = 1$, $\ell = 5$, $\log\beta = 6$, DFR $2^{-64}$), the $T = 8$ configuration runs at $400$ MHz and reaches $122$ PBS per second, achieving over $30\times$ higher throughput and $10\times$ lower latency than the Ohba FPGA baseline, demonstrating the design scales to large parameters. In the architecture, $k$ affects only buffer sizes while $N$ mainly affects on-chip BRAM and URAM blocks already partitioned into $T$ sub-buffers, so as long as these fit within the FPGA RAM primitives, scaling the parameters proportionally scales performance with negligible per-operation penalty.

Against general-purpose hardware (a 16-core AMD 7950X3D and an Nvidia L40 GPU running tfhe-rs), the FPGA delivers a $2.89\times$ (CPU) and $1.16\times$ (GPU) speedup on the standard parameter set and $4.35\times$ (CPU) and $1.12\times$ (GPU) on the large set, with improving efficiency at scale relative to the CPU and similar scalability to the L40. This is notable because the FPGA is built on a $20$ nm node while the CPU and GPU use $5$ to $6$ nm FinFET processes; despite that disadvantage the $T = 32$ design reaches over $5\times$ ($28\times$) lower latency and $2.8\times$ ($1.16\times$) higher throughput than the 16-core CPU (GPU) at similar or lower power.

Conclusion and Future Work

The paper presents a fully FPGA-integrated TFHE accelerator that runs programmable bootstrapping entirely on chip, keeps keys on the device, and thereby removes the off-chip bandwidth bottleneck that limited prior work, reducing external bandwidth from gigabytes per second to about $3$ megabits per second. The compact, parameterizable design outperforms the state of the art by $240\%$ to $480\%$ in bootstrappings per second, scales across standard and large parameter sets, and beats CPU and GPU baselines despite an older process node. The prototype implements PBS and supporting programmable infrastructure, and adds a designed-and-validated dual-purpose key-switch and MulAdd module. Remaining work is to complete the key-switch and instruction modules, define a dedicated instruction set, and build a compiler that translates homomorphic circuits into hardware instructions, with further gains expected from key unrolling and composite NTTs. The authors frame the result as a foundation for future ASIC-based TFHE processors.
