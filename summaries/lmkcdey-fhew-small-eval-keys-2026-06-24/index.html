Efficient FHEW Bootstrapping with Small Evaluation Keys, and Applications to Threshold Homomorphic Encryption

PDF (text-layer, figures rasterized from vector diagrams) — IACR ePrint 2022/198, retrieved via Wayback Machine · full view

At a glance

Authors: Yongwoo Lee, Andrey Kim, Rakyong Choi, Maxim Deryabin, Jieun Eom, Donghoon Yoo (Samsung Advanced Institute of Technology) and Daniele Micciancio (UC San Diego). Published at EUROCRYPT 2023; this is the ePrint 2022/198 version (revised June 2023). The scheme is widely referred to by the authors' initials as LMKCDEY and ships in OpenFHE.

One sentence: The paper introduces a new blind-rotation algorithm for FHEW/TFHE-style gate bootstrapping, built on ring automorphisms instead of the usual AP or GINX accumulator updates, that simultaneously gives small evaluation keys (the GINX advantage) and support for arbitrary secret-key distributions at no runtime penalty (the AP advantage), while also lowering bootstrapping noise.

Headline numbers (single NAND gate, 128-bit security, PALISADE/OpenFHE on a Xeon Gold 6240):

  • Blind-rotation key shrinks from 20.91 MB (binary GINX) to 12.67 MB — a ~39% reduction (the abstract's conservative claim is “more than 30%” / 20→14 MB), while runtime drops slightly to 80.1 ms vs. 84.1 ms (GINX) and 89.7 ms (GINX*).
  • vs. AP at the same Gaussian parameter set: 776 MB → 12.67 MB key and 127.8→80.1 ms.
  • Asymptotic saving of up to an O(log n) factor in either runtime or key size, depending on secret-key size / number of threshold parties.
  • Lowest bootstrapping failure probability of any FHEW method at matched parameters (e.g. 2−85.7 vs. AP's 2−77.7 on its Gaussian set).

Keywords: Automorphism, Blind Rotation, Bootstrapping, FHE, Threshold Homomorphic Encryption.

The problem: AP vs. GINX, and why you can't have both

FHEW (Ducas–Micciancio, EUROCRYPT 2015) and its torus variant TFHE (Chillotti et al.) are the standard way to do bit-level homomorphic computation: each Boolean gate is followed by a bootstrap that resets the noise. The expensive core of that bootstrap is a blind rotation, and there have historically been two competing ways to do it, each with a fatal trade-off:

  • AP / FHEW (Alperin-Sheriff–Peikert): stores, for every secret coordinate si and every possible value, an encryption E(α·si) and uses αi as a selector. Works for any secret-key distribution at constant runtime — but the evaluation key is huge (hundreds of MB).
  • GINX / TFHE: stores a single encryption E(si) per coordinate and uses a homomorphic MUX. The keys are small — but it is directly applicable only to binary secrets. Larger secrets force a key-size blow-up (linear in the bit-width k in [MP21], or exponential 2k−1 in [JP22]) plus higher noise. An optimized variant GINX* halves the work for ternary keys only.

This matters because non-binary secret keys are not exotic. Threshold and multi-key FHE build the global secret as a sum s = s1 + … + sk of per-party shares; even if each share is binary, the sum has coefficients as large as k (the number of parties — up to ~1000 in some applications). With AP this is fine but memory-prohibitive; with GINX it is slow. The paper's question: can one blind rotation give fast evaluation with arbitrary keys and small keys?

Background: blind rotation, RGSW, and FHEW gate bootstrapping

Work over the power-of-two cyclotomic ring R = Z[X]/(XN+1). A blind rotation takes a ring element f, an LWE ciphertext (α⃗, β) of a message u = β + ⟨α⃗, s⃗⟩ (mod q, with q | 2N), and rotation keys, and outputs an RLWE encryption of f·Yu — i.e. it negacyclically rotates f by the encrypted amount u. The two ingredients are RLWE′ (a gadget-decomposed RLWE vector) and RGSW ciphertexts, with an external product ⊛ (RLWE × RGSW → RLWE) and a scalar product ⊙ (R × RLWE′ → RLWE). The paper measures all costs in number of ⊙ products (the dominant operation; each ⊛ is two ⊙, each key-switch is one ⊙, and each ⊙ is dg+1 NTTs).

A FHEW NAND gate adds two small-modulus LWE ciphertexts, blind-rotates to evaluate the gate into the exponent, extracts an LWE sample, then returns to the original key/modulus via key- and modulus-switching. The paper adopts the improved ordering of [MP21] (start at the larger modulus Q, modulus-switch down to q just before blind rotation), shown below.

Core idea: blind rotation by automorphisms

Earlier methods implement multiplication-in-the-exponent (Xαisi) by a sequence of additions/MUXes. LMKCDEY instead uses ring automorphisms ψt: a(X) ↦ a(Xt), followed by key-switching back to the original key. The naive version is: given RLWE(fi−1), apply ψ1/αi, homomorphically multiply by Xsi (one ⊛ with brki = RGSW(Xsi)), then apply ψαi — landing at RLWE(fi−1·Xαisi). Crucially each coordinate needs only one RGSW key (like GINX), yet the cost is independent of the size of si (like AP). Using automorphisms for FHEW key-size reduction was suggested earlier ([BDF18], following a remark of Micciancio), but only in prime-order rings where automorphisms exist for every α; the power-of-two cyclotomic case is harder and is what this paper solves practically.

The key algorithmic insight (the reordering / generator trick): rather than apply a different automorphism ψαi/αi+1 at every step, iterate a single fixed automorphism ψg where g generates the large cyclic subgroup of Z*2N (e.g. g = 5). Sweeping g, g2, … visits every rotation amount; whenever the running exponent gℓ equals some αj, you intersperse the ⊛ multiplication by brkj. The whole blind rotation then needs essentially one automorphism key (for g, plus one for −g), n external products, and a number of automorphism applications that the analysis bounds tightly.

Three technicalities the algorithms must handle: (1) the αi can be arbitrary in Zq but automorphisms exist only for invertible (odd) t; (2) Z*2N is not cyclic — it factors as ZN/2 ⊗ Z2 with generators {g, −1}; (3) naively running over all of Z*2N takes O(q) steps, but we want O(n).

The algorithms: core, even-coefficient handling, and round-to-odd

The paper presents a family of algorithms (Alg. 3–7):

  • Core (Alg. 3, odd αi, q = 2N): partitions indices into sets I±ℓ = {i : αi = ±gℓ}, sweeps ℓ from N/2−1 down to 0 applying ψg between non-empty sets and ψ−g at the sign flip, multiplying in brkj as each set is reached. Because consecutive empty sets let automorphisms be composed, the algorithm stores keys {akgu} only for a small window size w (powers g1..w) rather than all N.
  • Even αi: automorphisms only exist for odd t. Two fixes — a memory-efficient variant (Alg. 4) that does ~n/2 extra ⊛ products, and a computation-efficient variant (Alg. 5) using auxiliary keys brk*i = RGSW(Xsi+si+1) to re-express the sum with all-odd coefficients at no extra products. An optional key brknsum = RGSW(X−∑si) shifts all αi by 1 to balance parity.
  • Case q = N (Alg. 6): the common practical case; multiply the input by 2 to raise the modulus to 2N, making all coefficients even, then one brknsum multiplication makes them odd. This is the variant used for the headline benchmarks.
  • Round-to-odd (Alg. 7): a neat trick that folds the parity fix into the final modulus-switch. Instead of rounding to the nearest integer, round to the nearest odd integer (with values nearest 0 going to 0). This yields an all-odd LWE ciphertext mod 2N for free, eliminating brknsum entirely — saving runtime, key size, and noise. The round-to-odd error equals ordinary modulus-switching to N.

Analysis: complexity, key size, and noise

The number of automorphism applications is what the analysis controls. Each set I±ℓ is empty with probability (1−1/N)n ≈ e−n/N (assuming uniform αi), so the expected number of non-empty sets is k ≈ N(1−e−n/N). With a window of w stored automorphism keys, the number of automorphism applications drops to roughly (1−1/w)k + N/w — bounded by (1−1/w)n + N/w in the worst case, and N(1−(1−1/w)e−n/N) on average. Experimentally, w ≥ 10 already saturates the speed-up.

So the new method also has the least noise growth, which in turn permits smaller (faster) parameters.

Application: threshold (and multi-key) FHE

The driving application is threshold FHE, where a secret s = ∑j sj is split across parties and the public key p = ∑j pj is the sum of local public keys (linearity of LWE). The challenge is generating the evaluation key — encryptions of the (now non-binary) shared secret — without leaking shares. The paper gives a non-interactive-ish distributed protocol that leverages the simple structure of its keys:

  • Automorphism keys: each party encrypts ψt(zj) under the shared public key (using a smudging error distribution χsmenc), and the server adds them: ∑j RLWE′(zj(Xt)) = RLWE′(z*(Xt)).
  • Blind-rotation keys: because shares combine in the exponent, parties multiply RGSW ciphertexts: ∏j RGSW(Xsj,i) = RGSW(Xs*,i) (error stays additive).

The payoff: the blind-rotation cost and key size are identical to the single-party Algorithm 3 — the number of participants does not affect asymptotic runtime or key size (only the noise grows, requiring modestly larger parameters). With GINX this would be impossible, since the summed secret is wide. The same machinery applies to compact-ciphertext multi-key FHE (e.g. [ZZC+21]) where keys are combined by summation. The threshold noise analysis gives σ2fresh ≤ (2N/3)|J|σ2 + (∥z*∥2+1)σ2smenc, and since the dominant key noise σbrk ≫ σak, the new method's noise advantage over an AP-style threshold scheme grows even larger in this setting.

Relation to the TFHE/FHEW line, and significance

This work sits squarely in the FHEW (DM15) → TFHE (CGGI20) gate-bootstrapping lineage, alongside the AP and GINX blind-rotation families systematized in Micciancio–Polyakov [MP21]. Its contribution is a third blind-rotation paradigm — automorphism-based — that dominates both predecessors on runtime, key size, and noise at matched security. The experiments are done in RLWE for fair comparison, but the authors note (Remark 1) the method ports directly to the Torus/TFHE setting with FFTs over the torus in place of NTTs over ZQ, just as GINX was. Practically, the scheme became the default FHEW bootstrapping method ("LMKCDEY") in OpenFHE.

Why it matters: it removes the long-standing tension between key size and key-distribution generality. Binary-secret restrictions had theoretical security caveats (sparse/binary LWE is weaker) and blocked clean threshold/multi-key constructions; AP avoided them but at ~60× the key size. LMKCDEY lets implementers use Gaussian (or any) secret keys — better-grounded in lattice hardness reductions — with small keys and the best runtime, and makes FHEW-based threshold HE practical with a participant-count-independent key size. Open directions the authors flag: applying the automorphism technique to NTRU-based bootstrapping (e.g. FINAL) and to full Torus-LWE TFHE implementations.
