Efficient FHEW Bootstrapping with Small Evaluation Keys, and Applications to Threshold Homomorphic Encryption

EUROCRYPT 2023 (IACR ePrint 2022/198, revised 10 June 2023) · full view ↗

At a glance

Authors: Yongwoo Lee, Daniele Micciancio, Andrey Kim, Rakyong Choi, Maxim Deryabin, Jieun Eom, Donghoon Yoo (Samsung Advanced Institute of Technology and UC San Diego). Published at EUROCRYPT 2023; this is the ePrint 2022/198 version (revised June 2023). The scheme is widely referred to by the authors' initials as LMKCDEY and ships in OpenFHE.

One sentence: The paper introduces a new blind-rotation algorithm for FHEW/TFHE-style gate bootstrapping, built on ring automorphisms instead of the usual AP or GINX accumulator updates, that simultaneously gives small evaluation keys (the GINX advantage) and support for arbitrary secret-key distributions at no runtime penalty (the AP advantage), while also lowering bootstrapping noise.

Headline numbers (single NAND gate, 128-bit security, PALISADE/OpenFHE on a Xeon Gold 6240):

  • Blind-rotation key shrinks from 20.91 MB (binary GINX) to 12.67 MB, a ~39% reduction (the abstract's conservative claim is "more than 30%"), while runtime drops slightly to 80.1 ms vs. 84.1 ms (GINX) and 89.7 ms (GINX*).
  • vs. AP at the same Gaussian parameter set: 776 MB to 12.67 MB key and 127.8 to 80.1 ms.
  • Asymptotic saving of up to an $O(\log n)$ factor in either runtime or key size, depending on secret-key size / number of threshold parties.
  • Lowest bootstrapping failure probability of any FHEW method at matched parameters (e.g. $2^{-85.7}$ vs. AP's $2^{-77.7}$ on its Gaussian set).

Keywords: Automorphism, Blind Rotation, Bootstrapping, FHE, Threshold Homomorphic Encryption.

The problem: AP vs. GINX, and why you cannot have both

FHEW (Ducas-Micciancio, EUROCRYPT 2015) and its torus variant TFHE (Chillotti et al.) are the standard way to do bit-level homomorphic computation: each Boolean gate is followed by a bootstrap that resets the noise. The expensive core of that bootstrap is a blind rotation, and there have historically been two competing ways to do it, each with a fatal trade-off:

  • AP / FHEW (Alperin-Sheriff-Peikert): stores, for every secret coordinate $s_i$ and every possible value, an encryption $\mathrm{RGSW}_z(Y^{v B_r^j s_i})$ and uses the base-$B_r$ digits of $\alpha_i$ as selectors. Works for any secret-key distribution at constant runtime, but the evaluation key is huge (hundreds of MB).
  • GINX / TFHE: stores a single encryption $\mathrm{RGSW}_z(s_i)$ per coordinate and uses a homomorphic MUX. The keys are small, but it is directly applicable only to binary secrets. Larger secrets force a key-size blow-up (linear in the bit-width $k$ in [MP21], or $2^k - 1$ in [JP22]) plus higher noise. An optimized variant GINX* halves the work for ternary keys only.

This matters because non-binary secret keys are not exotic. Threshold and multi-key FHE build the global secret as a sum $s = s_1 + \dots + s_k$ of per-party shares; even if each share is binary, the sum has coefficients as large as $k$ (the number of parties, up to ~1000 in some applications). With AP this is fine but memory-prohibitive; with GINX it is slow. The paper's question: can one blind rotation give fast evaluation with arbitrary keys and small keys?

Background: blind rotation, RGSW, and FHEW gate bootstrapping

Work over the power-of-two cyclotomic ring $R = \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^N+1)$. A blind rotation takes a ring element $f \in R_Q$, an LWE ciphertext $(\vec{\alpha}, \beta) \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{n+1}$ of a message $u = \beta + \langle \vec{\alpha}, \vec{s} \rangle$ (mod $q$, with $q \mid 2N$), and rotation keys, and outputs an RLWE encryption $\mathrm{RLWE}_{Q,z}\!\left(f \cdot Y^{\beta + \langle \vec{\alpha}, \vec{s} \rangle}\right)$ where $Y = X^{2N/q}$. That is, it negacyclically rotates $f$ by the encrypted amount $u$. The two ingredients are RLWE' (a gadget-decomposed RLWE vector) and RGSW ciphertexts, with an external product $\circledast$ (RLWE $\times$ RGSW $\to$ RLWE) and a scalar product $\odot$ ($R \times \mathrm{RLWE}' \to$ RLWE). The paper measures all costs in number of $\odot$ products (the dominant operation; each $\circledast$ is two $\odot$, each key-switch is one $\odot$, and each $\odot$ is $d_g+1$ NTTs).

Key switching converts $\mathrm{RLWE}_{z_1}(m)$ to $\mathrm{RLWE}_{z_2}(m)$ using $\mathrm{ksk} = \mathrm{RLWE}'_{z_2}(z_1)$. Automorphisms $\psi_t: a(X) \mapsto a(X^t)$ for $t \in \mathbb{Z}^*_{2N}$ permute coefficients (norm-preserving, so no added error) and are realized via switching keys $\mathrm{ak}_t = \mathrm{RLWE}'_{z(X)}(z(X^t))$ followed by a key switch back to $z(X)$.

A FHEW NAND gate adds two small-modulus LWE ciphertexts, blind-rotates to evaluate the gate into the exponent, extracts an LWE sample, then returns to the original key/modulus via key- and modulus-switching. Figure 1 shows the original FHEW ordering [DM15, MP21].

Prior algorithms: AP and GINX blind rotation

For reference, the two prior blind-rotation procedures are reproduced verbatim. AP decomposes each $\alpha_i$ in base $B_r$ and applies one external product per digit; it supports any key distribution but needs $2 d_r (B_r - 1) n$ keys.

Algorithm 1  Blind Rotation: AP [DM15, AP14]
1: procedure BlindRotateAP(f, (alpha, beta), {brk_{i,j,v} = RGSW_z(Y^{v B_r^j s_i})}_{i,j,v})
2:     acc <- (0, f * Y^beta)
3:     for (i = 0; i < n; i = i + 1) do
4:         for (j = 0; j < log_{B_r}(q); j = j + 1) do
5:             alpha_{i,j} = floor(alpha_i / B_r^j) (mod B_r)
6:             acc <- acc (X) brk_{i, j, alpha_{i,j}}
7:     return acc = RLWE_z(f * Y^u)

GINX writes each $s_i = \sum_j u_j s_{i,j}$ as a subset-sum with $s_{i,j} \in \{0,1\}$ and applies a homomorphic MUX; small keys for binary/ternary secrets but cost and key size grow with the secret distribution.

Algorithm 2  Blind Rotation: GINX [CGGI20, GINX16, MP21]
1: procedure BlindRotateGINX(f, (alpha, beta), {brk_{i,j} = RGSW_z(s_{i,j}) | s_i = sum_j s_{i,j} u_j})
2:     acc <- (0, f * Y^beta)
3:     for (i = 0; i < n; i = i + 1) do
4:         for (j = 0; j < |U|; j = j + 1) do
5:             acc <- acc + (Y^{alpha_i u_j} - 1) * (acc (X) brk_{i,j})
6:     return acc = RLWE_z(f * Y^u)

Core idea: blind rotation by automorphisms

Earlier methods implement multiplication-in-the-exponent ($X^{\alpha_i s_i}$) by a sequence of additions/MUXes. LMKCDEY instead uses ring automorphisms $\psi_t: a(X) \mapsto a(X^t)$, followed by key-switching back to the original key. The naive version is: given $\mathrm{RLWE}(f_{i-1})$, apply $\psi_{1/\alpha_i}$, homomorphically multiply by $X^{s_i}$ (one $\circledast$ with $\mathrm{brk}_i = \mathrm{RGSW}(X^{s_i})$), then apply $\psi_{\alpha_i}$, landing at $\mathrm{RLWE}(f_{i-1} \cdot X^{\alpha_i s_i})$. Crucially each coordinate needs only one RGSW key (like GINX), yet the cost is independent of the size of $s_i$ (like AP). Using automorphisms for FHEW key-size reduction was suggested earlier ([BDF18], following a remark of Micciancio), but only in prime-order rings where automorphisms exist for every $\alpha$; the power-of-two cyclotomic case is harder and is what this paper solves practically.

The key algorithmic insight (the reordering / generator trick): for $N \ge 8$, $\mathbb{Z}^*_{2N} \cong \mathbb{Z}_{N/2} \otimes \mathbb{Z}_2$ with generators $\{g, -1\}$ (e.g. $g = 5$), so every $t \in \mathbb{Z}^*_{2N}$ is $\pm g^k$. Rather than apply a different automorphism per step, iterate the single fixed automorphism $\psi_g$. Grouping indices by $I^+_\ell = \{i : \alpha_i = g^\ell\}$ and $I^-_\ell = \{i : \alpha_i = -g^\ell\}$, and using $g^{N/2} = 1 \pmod{2N}$, the exponent decomposes as

$$\sum_i \alpha_i s_i = \Big( \textstyle\sum_{j \in I^+_0} s_j + \cdots + g\,\textstyle\sum_{j \in I^+_{N/2-1}} s_j \;-\; g\,\textstyle\sum_{j \in I^-_0} s_j + \cdots + g\,\textstyle\sum_{j \in I^-_{N/2-1}} s_j \Big) \pmod{2N}.$$

Sweeping $g, g^2, \dots$ visits every rotation amount; whenever the running exponent $g^\ell$ matches some $\alpha_j$, you intersperse the $\circledast$ multiplication by $\mathrm{brk}_j$. The whole blind rotation then needs essentially one automorphism key (for $g$, plus one for $-g$), $n$ external products, and a number of automorphism applications the analysis bounds tightly.

Three technicalities the algorithms must handle: (1) the $\alpha_i$ can be arbitrary in $\mathbb{Z}_q$ but automorphisms exist only for invertible (odd) $t$; (2) $\mathbb{Z}^*_{2N}$ is not cyclic, it factors as $\mathbb{Z}_{N/2} \otimes \mathbb{Z}_2$; (3) naively running over all of $\mathbb{Z}^*_{2N}$ takes $O(q)$ steps, but we want $O(n)$.

The core algorithm and the window-size optimization

The core procedure (for odd $\alpha_i$ and $q = 2N$) sweeps $\ell$ from $N/2 - 1$ down to $0$, applying $\psi_g$ between non-empty sets and $\psi_{-g}$ at the sign flip, multiplying in $\mathrm{brk}_j$ as each set is reached. When consecutive sets $I^\pm_\ell$ are empty, the automorphisms between them can be composed; rather than store keys for all $N$ powers, only a small number $\{\mathrm{ak}_{g^u}\}_{u \in [1,w]}$ for a window size $w$ are kept. The full procedure uses $n$ keys $\mathrm{brk}_i$ and two automorphism keys $\mathrm{ak}_g$ and $\mathrm{ak}_{-g}$; it performs $n$ external products and $N-1$ automorphisms before the window optimization.

Algorithm 3  Core Blind Rotation Sub Algorithm for odd alpha_i
1: procedure BlindRotateCore(acc, alpha, {brk_i}_{i in [0,n-1]}, {ak_{g^u}}_{u in [1,w]}, ak_{-g})
2:     v <- 0
3:     for (l = N/2 - 1; l > 0; l = l - 1) do
4:         for j in I^-_l do
5:             acc <- acc (X) brk_j
6:         v <- v + 1
7:         if (I^-_{l-1} != empty or v = w or l = 1) then
8:             acc <- Auto_{g^v}(acc, ak_{g^v})
9:             v <- 0
10:    for j in I^-_0 do
11:        acc <- acc (X) brk_j
12:    acc <- Auto_{-g}(acc, ak_{-g})
13:    for (l = N/2 - 1; l > 0; l = l - 1) do
14:        for j in I^+_l do
15:            acc <- acc (X) brk_j
16:        v <- v + 1
17:        if (I^+_{l-1} != empty or v = w or l = 1) then
18:            acc <- Auto_{g^v}(acc, ak_{g^v})
19:            v <- 0
20:    for j in I^+_0 do
21:        acc <- acc (X) brk_j
22:    return acc

Here $(X)$ denotes the external product $\circledast$. Setting the initial accumulator $f'(X) = f(X^{-g}) \cdot X^{-g\beta}$ makes the output equal $\mathrm{RLWE}_z\!\left(f(X) \cdot X^{\beta + \langle \vec{\alpha}, \vec{s} \rangle}\right)$.

Handling even coefficients: three variants

Automorphisms $\psi_t$ exist only for odd $t$, so the $\alpha_i$ must be made odd. The paper gives three fixes.

(1) Memory-efficient (Algorithm 4). Set $\omega_i = \alpha_i - 1$ for even $\alpha_i$ (else $\omega_i = \alpha_i$), run the core on $\vec{\omega}$, then multiply back in $\mathrm{brk}_i$ for each even $\alpha_i$. This costs about $n/2$ extra external products on average. An optional key $\mathrm{brk}_{\mathrm{nsum}} = \mathrm{RGSW}(X^{-\sum_i s_i})$ shifts all $\alpha_i$ by 1 to keep the majority odd in the worst case.

Algorithm 4  Memory Efficient Blind Rotation Algorithm, q = 2N
1: procedure BlindRotateME(f, alpha, beta, {brk_i}_{i in [0,n-1]}, brk_nsum, {ak_{g^u}}_{u in [1,w]}, ak_{-g})
2:     acc <- (0, f(X^{-g}) * X^{-g beta})
3:     if number of even alpha_i is > n/2 then
4:         acc <- acc (X) brk_nsum
5:         alpha <- alpha + 1_n (mod 2N)
6:     for (i = 0; i < n; i = i + 1) do
7:         if alpha_i is even then
8:             omega_i <- alpha_i - 1 (mod 2N)
9:         else
10:            omega_i <- alpha_i
11:    acc <- BlindRotateCore(acc, omega, {brk_i}, {ak_{g^u}}, ak_{-g})
12:    for (i = 0; i < n; i = i + 1) do
13:        if alpha_i is even then
14:            acc <- acc (X) brk_i
15:    return acc = RLWE_z(f(X) * X^{beta + <alpha, s>})

(2) Computation-efficient (Algorithm 5). Auxiliary keys $\mathrm{brk}^*_i = \mathrm{RGSW}(X^{s_i + s_{i+1}})$ let one re-express $\sum_i \alpha_i s_i = \sum_i \alpha'_i s'_i$ with all-odd $\alpha'_i$ and $s'_i \in \{s_i, s_i + s_{i+1}\}$, at no extra external products. If $\alpha_{i+1}$ is odd, set $s'_i = s_i, \alpha'_{i+1} = \alpha_{i+1}$; otherwise $s'_i = s_i + s_{i+1}, \alpha'_{i+1} = \alpha_{i+1} - \alpha_i$ (always odd).

Algorithm 5  Computation Efficient Blind Rotation Algorithm, q = 2N
1: procedure BlindRotateCE(f, (alpha, beta), {brk_i}_{i in [0,n-1]}, {brk*_i}_{i in [0,n-2]}, brk_nsum, {ak_{g^u}}_{u in [1,w]}, ak_{-g})
2:     acc <- (0, f(X^{-g}) * X^{-g beta})
3:     if alpha_0 is even then
4:         acc <- acc (X) brk_nsum
5:         alpha <- alpha + 1_n (mod 2N)
6:     Find odd alpha'_i : sum_i alpha_i s_i = sum_i alpha'_i s'_i
7:     for (i = 0; i < n; i = i + 1) do
8:         if s'_i = s_i then
9:             brk'_i <- brk_i
10:        else
11:            brk'_i <- brk*_i
12:    acc <- BlindRotateCore(acc, alpha', {brk'_i}, {ak_{g^u}}, ak_{-g})
13:    return acc = RLWE_z(f(X) * X^{beta + <alpha, s>})

(3) Case $q = N$ (Algorithm 6). The common practical case: multiply the input by 2 to raise the modulus to $2N$ (all coefficients now even), then one $\mathrm{brk}_{\mathrm{nsum}}$ multiplication makes them odd via $\vec{\alpha}' = 2\vec{\alpha} + \vec{1}_n$. This is the variant behind the headline benchmarks.

Algorithm 6  Blind Rotation Algorithm, q = N
1: procedure BlindRotateOptim(f, alpha, beta, {brk_i}_{i in [0,n-1]}, brk_nsum, {ak_{g^u}}_{u in [1,w]}, ak_{-g})
2:     acc <- (0, f(X^{-g}) * X^{-2 g beta})
3:     acc <- acc (X) brk_nsum
4:     alpha' <- 2 alpha + 1_n (mod 2N)
5:     acc <- BlindRotateCore(acc, alpha', {brk_i}, {ak_{g^u}}, ak_{-g})
6:     return acc = RLWE_z(f(X) * X^{2(beta + <alpha, s>)})

Round-to-odd: folding the parity fix into modulus switching

As in [DM15], swapping the operation order (start at the higher modulus $Q$ and modulus-switch down to $q$ just before blind rotation, Figure 2) reduces noise and key switches. On top of that, the paper proposes the round-to-odd trick, which eliminates $\mathrm{brk}_{\mathrm{nsum}}$ entirely. Instead of ordinary rounding, the modulus reduction rounds each coordinate to the nearest odd integer $\lfloor x \rceil_{\mathrm{odd}}$ (values closer to 0 than to $\pm 1$ go to 0):

$$\left( \vec{\alpha} = \Big\lfloor \tfrac{2N}{Q_{ks}} \cdot \vec{\alpha}' \Big\rceil_{\mathrm{odd}},\; \beta = \Big\lfloor \tfrac{2N}{Q_{ks}} \cdot \beta' \Big\rceil_{\mathrm{odd}} \right) = \mathrm{LWE}_{2N}(q/4 \cdot m).$$

This yields an all-odd LWE ciphertext mod $2N$ for free, so no $\mathrm{brk}_{\mathrm{nsum}}$ multiplication is needed, saving runtime, key size, and noise. The modulus-reduction error of round-to-odd is equivalent to modulus switching to $N$. Algorithm 7 is then just the core called directly on the all-odd input.

Algorithm 7  Blind Rotation Algorithm with Round-to-odd Input, q = 2N
1: procedure BlindRotateRoundToOdd(f, alpha, beta, {brk_i}_{i in [0,n-1]}, {ak_{g^u}}_{u in [1,w]}, ak_{-g})   // alpha, beta are all odd
2:     acc <- (0, f(X^{-g}) * X^{-g beta})
3:     acc <- BlindRotateCore(acc, alpha, {brk_i}, {ak_{g^u}}, ak_{-g})
4:     return acc = RLWE_z(f(X) * X^{beta + <alpha, s>})

Analysis: complexity, key size, and noise

The number of automorphism applications is what the analysis controls. Each set $I^\pm_\ell$ is empty with probability $(1 - 1/N)^n \approx e^{-n/N}$ (assuming uniform $\alpha_i$), so the expected number of non-empty sets is $k \approx N(1 - e^{-n/N})$. With a window of $w$ stored automorphism keys, each block of $v_i$ automorphisms is realized as one $g^{v'_i}$ ($v'_i = v_i \bmod w$) plus $v''_i = \lfloor v_i / w \rfloor$ applications of $g^w$. Using $\sum_i v_i \le N$, the number of automorphism applications drops to

$$\kappa + \tfrac{N - \kappa}{w} = (1 - 1/w)\kappa + (1/w)N \;\le\; (1 - 1/w)n + (1/w)N,$$

and to $N\!\left(1 - (1 - 1/w)\,e^{-n/N}\right)$ on average. Experimentally, $w \ge 10$ already saturates the speed-up. Each $\odot$ operation contributes error variance $\frac{d_g N B_g^2}{12}\sigma^2$; each $\circledast$ (RGSW encrypting a monomial) adds $2\sigma^2_\odot$ and each automorphism adds $\sigma^2_\odot$, versus $4\sigma^2_\odot$ (GINX) or $8\sigma^2_\odot$ (GINX*) per $\circledast$ because of their pre-processing. So the new method also has the least noise growth, which in turn permits smaller (faster) parameters.

Application: threshold (and multi-key) FHE

The driving application is threshold FHE, where a secret $s = \sum_j s_j$ is split across parties and the public key $p = \sum_j p_j$ is the sum of local public keys (linearity of LWE). The challenge is generating the evaluation key, encryptions of the (now non-binary) shared secret, without leaking shares. The paper gives a distributed protocol that leverages the simple structure of its keys:

  • Automorphism keys: each party encrypts $\psi_t(z_j)$ under the shared public key (with a smudging error distribution $\chi_{\mathrm{smenc}}$), and the server adds them: $\sum_j \mathrm{RLWE}'(z_j(X^t)) = \mathrm{RLWE}'(z^*(X^t))$.
  • Blind-rotation keys: because shares combine in the exponent, parties multiply RGSW ciphertexts: $\prod_j \mathrm{RGSW}(X^{s_{j,i}}) = \mathrm{RGSW}(X^{s^*_i})$ (error stays additive).

The payoff: blind-rotation cost and key size are identical to the single-party Algorithm 3, the number of participants does not affect asymptotic runtime or key size (only the noise grows, requiring modestly larger parameters). With GINX this would be impossible, since the summed secret is wide. The same machinery applies to compact-ciphertext multi-key FHE (e.g. [ZZC+21]) where keys are combined by summation. The threshold fresh-key noise satisfies $\sigma^2_{\mathrm{fresh}} \le \tfrac{2N}{3}|J|\sigma^2 + (\|z^*\|^2 + 1)\sigma^2_{\mathrm{smenc}}$, and since the dominant key noise $\sigma_{\mathrm{brk}} \gg \sigma_{\mathrm{ak}}$, the new method's noise advantage over an AP-style threshold scheme grows even larger in this setting.

Relation to the TFHE/FHEW line, and significance

This work sits in the FHEW (DM15) to TFHE (CGGI20) gate-bootstrapping lineage, alongside the AP and GINX blind-rotation families systematized in Micciancio-Polyakov [MP21]. Its contribution is a third blind-rotation paradigm, automorphism-based, that dominates both predecessors on runtime, key size, and noise at matched security. The experiments are done in RLWE for fair comparison, but the authors note (Remark 1) the method ports directly to the Torus/TFHE setting with FFTs over the torus in place of NTTs over $\mathbb{Z}_Q$, just as GINX was. Practically, the scheme became the default FHEW bootstrapping method ("LMKCDEY") in OpenFHE.

Why it matters: it removes the long-standing tension between key size and key-distribution generality. Binary-secret restrictions had theoretical security caveats (sparse/binary LWE is weaker) and blocked clean threshold/multi-key constructions; AP avoided them but at ~60x the key size. LMKCDEY lets implementers use Gaussian (or any) secret keys, better-grounded in lattice hardness reductions, with small keys and the best runtime, and makes FHEW-based threshold HE practical with a participant-count-independent key size. Open directions the authors flag: applying the automorphism technique to NTRU-based bootstrapping (e.g. FINAL) and to full Torus-LWE TFHE implementations.
