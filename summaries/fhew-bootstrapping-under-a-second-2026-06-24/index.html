FHEW: Bootstrapping Homomorphic Encryption in Less Than a Second

PDF, IACR ePrint 2014/816 · full view

Motivation: bootstrapping is the FHE bottleneck

Every fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) scheme since Gentry's 2009 construction relies on noisy lattice ciphertexts. Each homomorphic operation grows the noise, and once it crosses a threshold the ciphertext stops decrypting. Gentry's bootstrapping fixes this by homomorphically evaluating the decryption circuit on an encrypted secret key, refreshing the ciphertext back to a low noise level. It is what makes unbounded computation possible, and it is the dominant cost in every practical implementation. At the time of writing, the fastest reference implementation (HElib, Halevi and Shoup, Crypto 2014) needed about six minutes per bootstrap.

Ducas and Micciancio study bootstrapping in the simplest useful setting. Given two LWE-encrypted bits, ciphertexts $c_0, c_1 \in \mathrm{LWE}_s^{2/q}(m_i)$, compute an encryption of their logical NAND $m = 1 - m_0 m_1 = m_0 \,\bar{\wedge}\, m_1$ and return it refreshed to the same low-noise form as the inputs. NAND is complete for boolean circuits, so a fast bootstrapped NAND yields a full FHE. Their prototype performs one homomorphic NAND plus a full refresh in about $0.69$ seconds on a single $3$ GHz core, roughly $500\times$ faster than HElib's per-bootstrap time, in about $600$ lines of C++ (released as FHEW on GitHub). Security rests on worst-case hardness of standard lattice problems (LWE / Ring-LWE) plus the usual circular-security assumption needed to publish a compact evaluation key.

LWE encryption, modulus switching, key switching

The base scheme is plain symmetric LWE. With dimension $n$, message modulus $t \ge 2$, ciphertext modulus $q = n^{O(1)}$ and a randomized rounding function $\chi$, a message $m \in \mathbb{Z}_t$ is encrypted under secret key $s \in \mathbb{Z}_q^n$ as

$$\mathrm{LWE}_s^{t/q}(m) = (a,\ \chi(a \cdot s + m q / t) \bmod q) \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{n+1}, \qquad a \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_q^n \text{ uniform}.$$

When $t \mid q$ this is $(a,\ a \cdot s + e + m q / t \bmod q)$. Decryption computes $m' = \lfloor t(b - a \cdot s)/q \rceil \bmod t$. A ciphertext decrypts correctly whenever its error satisfies $|\mathrm{err}| < q/(2t)$, since then $|(t/q) e| < 1/2$. The notation $\mathrm{LWE}_s^{t/q}(m, E)$ denotes the encryptions of $m$ whose error is bounded by $E$.

Two helper conversions are central to the pipeline. Modulus switching rescales a ciphertext from modulus $Q$ to $q$ with the scaled randomized rounding $[x]_{Q:q} = \lfloor q x / Q \rfloor + B$, where $B$ is a Bernoulli bit with $\Pr\{B=1\} = (qx/Q) - \lfloor qx/Q \rfloor$; the resulting rounding error is subgaussian of parameter $\sqrt{2\pi}$. Lemma 5 gives the output error parameter $\sqrt{(q\sigma/Q)^2 + 2\pi(\lVert s \rVert^2 + 1)}$. Key switching converts a ciphertext under key $z$ into one under a different key $s$ using auxiliary encryptions $k_{i,j,v} \in \mathrm{LWE}_s^{q/q}(v z_i B_{ks}^j)$; it expands each coordinate $a_i = \sum_j a_{i,j} B_{ks}^j$ in base $B_{ks}$ and computes

$$\mathrm{KeySwitch}((a,b), K) = (0, b) - \sum_{i,j} k_{i,j,a_{i,j}}.$$

Lemma 6: this yields an encryption of the same message with output error parameter $\sqrt{\alpha^2 + N d_{ks} \sigma^2}$. To keep key-switching noise small the LWE secret $s$ is taken binary, which is proven as hard as standard LWE (Brakerski et al. 2013).

A new low-noise homomorphic NAND gate

LWE is approximately additively homomorphic: adding $E(m_1)$ and $E(m_2)$ gives a noisier $E(m_1 + m_2)$. Working mod $2$ this only gives XOR. The key trick is to move to arithmetic mod 4 during the cycle. Assume the two input bits arrive in a shifted form: ciphertexts $c_i \in \mathrm{LWE}_s^{4/q}(m_i, q/16)$, with message modulus $t = 4$ and error bound $q/16$ (versus the standard $t = 2$, $E = q/4$). Then NAND is a key-free affine combination.

Lemma 7 gives the algorithm $\mathrm{HomNAND}$ mapping two such ciphertexts to $\mathrm{LWE}_s^{2/q}(m_0 \,\bar{\wedge}\, m_1,\ q/4)$:

$$\mathrm{HomNAND}((a_0,b_0),(a_1,b_1)) = \Big(-a_0 - a_1,\ \tfrac{5q}{8} - b_0 - b_1\Big).$$

Assuming $2t = 8$ divides $q$ so that $5q/8$ is an integer, the result $(a,b)$ satisfies

$$b - a \cdot s - (1 - m_0 m_1)\tfrac{q}{2} = \tfrac{q}{4}\Big(\tfrac{1}{2} - (m_0 - m_1)^2\Big) - (e_0 + e_1) = \pm \tfrac{q}{8} - (e_0 + e_1),$$

so $(a,b)$ is a genuine $\mathrm{LWE}_s^{2/q}$ encryption of $1 - m_0 m_1 = m_0 \,\bar{\wedge}\, m_1$ with error at most $\lvert \pm q/8 - (e_0 + e_1) \rvert < q/8 + q/16 + q/16 = q/4$.

HomNAND uses no key material and only a handful of modular additions. The whole homomorphic-circuit problem therefore reduces to a single refreshing function

$$\mathrm{Refresh} : \mathrm{LWE}_s^{2/q}(m, q/4) \ \longrightarrow\ \mathrm{LWE}_s^{4/q}(m, q/16),$$

which restores the shifted, low-noise input form. Figure 1 shows the full per-gate cycle: HomNAND maps the two refreshed mod-4 inputs to a noisy mod-2 ciphertext, the accumulator (ACC) operations lift it to the ring, and KeySwitch then ModSwitch bring it back to a refreshed mod-4 LWE ciphertext under the original key. A circuit with $g$ gates needs $n + g$ refreshes (one per input wire and per gate output); supplying the inputs pre-refreshed leaves essentially one Refresh per gate. Crucially, previous homomorphic AND methods required input error $O(\sqrt{q})$; this construction tolerates $O(q)$, a far weaker demand on the refresh step.

The homomorphic accumulator and the Refresh / bootstrapping procedure

Refresh is bootstrapping: homomorphically evaluate LWE decryption $\lfloor 2(b - a \cdot E(s))/q \rceil \bmod 2$ on an encrypted key $E(s)$. Decryption is a scalar product mod $q$ followed by a most-significant-bit (rounding) extraction. Following Alperin-Sheriff and Peikert, an intermediate encryption scheme $E$ with message space $\mathbb{Z}_q$ is used: it can encrypt $s$ componentwise as $E(s) = (E(s_1), \dots, E(s_n))$ and supports affine maps $b - a \cdot E(s) = E(b - a \cdot s)$. The needed structure is abstracted as a Homomorphic Accumulator.

Definition 1: a quadruple $(E, \mathrm{Init}, \mathrm{Incr}, \mathrm{msbExtract})$ with moduli $t, q$. Write $\mathrm{ACC} \leftarrow v$ for $\mathrm{Init}(v)$ and $\mathrm{ACC} \mathrel{+}\leftarrow E(v)$ for $\mathrm{Incr}(\mathrm{ACC}, E(v))$. After $\mathrm{ACC} \leftarrow v_0$ then a sequence of increments by $E(v_1), \dots, E(v_\ell)$, ACC is an $\ell$-encryption of $v = \sum_i v_i \bmod q$. It is $E$-correct if $\mathrm{msbExtract}(\mathrm{ACC})$ yields a ciphertext $c \in \mathrm{LWE}_s^{t/q}(v, E(\ell))$ with overwhelming probability. For this application $t = 4$ and we need $E(\ell) \le q/16$.

The refresh procedure is parametrized by base $B_r$ with $d_r = \lceil \log_{B_r} q \rceil$. The refreshing key holds $K_{i,c,j} = E(c\, s_i B_r^j \bmod q)$ for $c \in \{0, \dots, B_r - 1\}$, $j = 0, \dots, d_r - 1$, $i = 1, \dots, n$ (about $n B_r d_r$ ciphertexts). The algorithm initializes the accumulator to $b + q/4$, folds in the scalar product $-a \cdot s$ coordinate by coordinate via base-$B_r$ digits, then extracts the msb:

Algorithm 1.  Refresh_K(a, b),  for K = {K_{i,c,j}}_{i<=n, c<=B_r, j<=d_r}

    ACC <- b + (q/4)
    for i = 1, ..., n do
        Compute the base-B_r representation of  -a_i = sum_j B_r^j * a_{i,j}  (mod q)
        for j = 0, ..., d_r - 1 do  ACC +<- K_{i, a_{i,j}, j}
    end for
    Output msbExtract(ACC).

Theorem 8 (correctness): with a correct accumulator, on input $c \in \mathrm{LWE}_s^{2/q}(m, q/4)$ and a valid key, Refresh outputs a ciphertext in $\mathrm{LWE}_s^{t/q}(m, E(nd))$. The implicit accumulator value at the end of the loop is

$$v - \tfrac{q}{4} = b + \sum_{i,j} a_{i,j} s_i B_r^j = b + \sum_i s_i \Big(\sum_j B_r^j a_{i,j}\Big) = b - \sum_i a_i s_i = \tfrac{q}{2} m + e.$$

Since $|e| < q/4$, we get $0 < e + q/4 < q/2$, hence $0 < v < q/2$ when $m = 0$ and $q/2 < v < q$ when $m = 1$. So $\mathrm{msb}(v) = m$, and msbExtract returns the refreshed encryption. The number of accumulator increments per refresh is $n d_r$.

Realizing the accumulator with Ring-GSW external products

The accumulator is instantiated with a private-key, ring variant of the GSW cryptosystem. Work in the power-of-two cyclotomic ring $R = \mathbb{Z}[X]/(X^N + 1)$, $R_Q = R/QR$, with $N = 2^K$, $q \mid 2N$, gadget base $B_g$ (assumed a power of $3$, $Q = B_g^{d_g}$). A parameter $u$, invertible mod $Q$ and close to $Q/(2t)$, is fixed with $\delta = u - Q/(2t)$, $|\delta| < 1$. The decisive idea, generalizing Alperin-Sheriff and Peikert, is to encode the additive group $\mathbb{Z}_q$ directly into the multiplicative group of roots of unity of $R$: a message $m$ is carried in the exponent as $Y^m$, where $Y = X^{2N/q}$. The roots of unity $\langle X \rangle = \{1, X, \dots, X^{N-1}, -1, \dots, -X^{N-1}\}$ form a cyclic group of order $2N$, and $\mathbb{Z}_q \simeq \langle Y \rangle$ embeds into it. Each $\mathbb{Z}_q$ element is thus one ciphertext, not a vector of $|C|$ binary ciphertexts as in prior work.

The gadget matrix is $G = (I, B_g I, \dots, B_g^{d_g - 1} I) \in R_Q^{2 d_g \times 2}$. The scheme operations:

E_z(m):  pick a <- R_Q^{2 d_g} uniform,  e <- subgaussian (param zeta) over R^{2 d_g}
         output  E_z(m) = [ a ,  a.z + e ]  +  u * Y^m * G    in  R_Q^{2 d_g x 2}

Init (ACC <- v):   ACC := u * Y^v * G    in R_Q^{2 d_g x 2}   (noiseless encryption of v)

Incr (ACC +<- C):  decompose  u^{-1} ACC = sum_{i=1}^{d_g} B_g^{i-1} * D_i
                   (each D_i has coefficients in {(1-B_g)/2, ..., (B_g-1)/2})
                   update  ACC := [D_1, ..., D_{d_g}] . C

Incr is exactly the GSW external product: gadget-decompose the accumulator, then multiply by the fresh ciphertext $C$. Because messages live in the exponent, the multiplicative homomorphism of GSW, $Y^m \cdot Y^{m'} = Y^{m + m'}$, becomes the additive accumulation the refresh loop needs. Fact 9: if ACC encrypts $m$ and $C$ encrypts $m'$, then $\mathrm{ACC} \mathrel{+}\leftarrow C$ encrypts $m + m'$ with new error $e'' = e + [C_1, \dots, C_{d_g}] \cdot e'$. The asymmetric noise growth (the small, bounded decomposition multiplies the fresh error) is what keeps GSW noise nearly additive.

msb extraction and the testing vector

Extracting the top bit converts the ring accumulator back into a single LWE ciphertext. It uses a testing vector $t = -\sum_{i=0}^{q/2 - 1} \overrightarrow{Y^i}$ (the $Y^i$ written as coefficient vectors). Its defining property is $t \cdot \overrightarrow{Y^v} = -1$ for $0 \le v < N$ and $+1$ for $N \le v < 2N$, i.e. $t \cdot \overrightarrow{Y^v} = -(-1)^{\mathrm{msb}(v)}$. (In the simple case $q = 2N$ the testing vector is just $-(1, 1, \dots, 1)$.) msbExtract takes a switching key $K$ from the ring key $z$ to the LWE key $s$ and runs:

Algorithm 2.  msbExtract_K(ACC),  K = {k_{i,j,w}}_{i<=N, j<=B_ks, w<=d_ks}
Require: switching key  k_{i,j,w} <- LWE_s^{q/q}( w * z_i * d_ks^j )  from z to s;
         ACC an l-encryption of v.

  1: [a^t, b^t] <- ( [ -0^t, t^t, -0^t, ..., -0^t ] . anticirc(ACC) )  in Z_Q^{2N}
  2: c  <- (a, b_0 + u)                  in LWE_{vec z}^{t/Q}( msb(v) )
  3: c' <- KeySwitch(c, K)               in LWE_s^{t/Q}( msb(v) )
  4: c'' <- ModSwitch(c')                in LWE_s^{t/q}( msb(v) )
  5: Return c'.

Line 1 contracts the second row $[a, b']$ of the accumulator against the testing vector. Since $b' = \mathrm{anticirc}(a) \cdot z + u \cdot \overrightarrow{Y^v} + e$, and the coordinates of the embedded $Y^v$ sum to $(-1)^{\mathrm{msb}(v)}$, using $1 - (-1)^x = 2x$ for a bit $x$ gives $c = (a,\ a \cdot \overrightarrow{z} + t \cdot e + 2u\, \mathrm{msb}(v))$, an $\mathrm{LWE}_{\overrightarrow{z}}^{t/Q}$ encryption of $\mathrm{msb}(v)$ because $u \approx Q/(2t)$. Key switching moves it to key $s$, modulus switching brings it down to modulus $q$, producing the refreshed LWE ciphertext.

Efficient Incr (Algorithm 3) keeps both the accumulator and the bootstrapping-key ciphertexts in FFT/NTT form:

Algorithm 3.  Incr( ACC in R^{2 d_g x 2},  C in R^{2 d_g x 2} )

    Compute ACC <- FFT^{-1}(ACC)
    Decompose u^{-1} ACC = sum_{i=1}^{d_g} B_g^{i-1} D_i,  set D = [D_1 ... D_{d_g}] in R^{2 d_g x 2 d_g}
    Compute D <- FFT(D)
    Return D (.) C       // pointwise product in FFT domain

Each increment costs $4 d_g$ backward FFTs and $4 d_g^2$ forward FFTs; with the NTT, $4 d_g$ forward transforms can be traded for a few additions mod $Q$ by reconstructing $D_1 = u^{-1}\mathrm{ACC} - \sum_{i=2}^{d_g} B_g^{i-1} D_i \bmod Q$ from the others.

Noise analysis

Invertibility in $R$ is handled via the factorization $X^N + 1 = (X^{N/2} + X^{N/4} - 1)(X^{N/2} - X^{N/4} - 1) \bmod 3$ into two irreducibles (Fact 2), giving fields of order $3^{N/2}$; with Hensel lifting (Lemma 3) random matrices over $R_Q$ ($Q$ a power of $3$) are invertible with overwhelming probability (Lemma 4). This underlies Fact 12, which bounds the spectral norm of the stacked decomposition matrices: $s_1([D^{(0)}, \dots, D^{(\ell-1)}]) = O(B_g \sqrt{N d_g \ell})$.

Lemma 11 (intermediate error): assuming Ring-LWE hardness, for an $\ell$-encryption with $\ell \ge \omega(\sqrt{\log N})$, the ciphertext $c$ of line 2 of Algorithm 2 has subgaussian error of parameter

$$\beta = O\big(\varsigma\, B_g \sqrt{q\, N d_g\, \ell}\big) \qquad \text{with mean } 2\delta,$$

derived because the final error is $e = t \cdot \mathrm{anticirc}([D^{(0)}, \dots, D^{(\ell-1)}]) \cdot (e^{(1)}, \dots, e^{(\ell)})$, where $\lVert t \rVert = \sqrt{q/2}$, the decomposition block has spectral norm $O(B_g \sqrt{N d_g \ell})$, and the concatenated fresh errors are subgaussian of parameter $\varsigma$. Combining Lemma 11 with the key-switch and modulus-switch noise (Lemmata 6 and 5) gives the full accumulator correctness:

Theorem 10. Assuming $\text{Ring-LWE}_{R,Q,\chi}$, the accumulator is $E$-correct with

$$E(\ell) = \sqrt{\frac{q^2}{Q^2}\big(\varsigma^2 B_g^2\, \ell\, q\, N d_g + \sigma^2 N d_{ks}\big)} + \lVert s \rVert^2 \cdot \omega(\sqrt{\log n}).$$

Asymptotic parameters: binary secret $s$; $B_g, B_r, B_{ks} = \Theta(1)$ so $d_g, d_r, d_{ks} = O(\log n)$; $\sigma, \varsigma = \omega(\sqrt{\log n})$; $2N = q = \Theta(n)$; $Q = n^2 \log n \cdot \omega(\log n)$. This yields refresh error $E(nd) = O(n) \le q/16$, satisfying the NAND requirement. Refresh costs $dn$ homomorphic operations, versus $dn \cdot O(\log^3 q / \log\log q)$ in Alperin-Sheriff and Peikert; the pre-switch refresh error is parameter $\alpha = O(n^2 \log n)$ here versus $\Theta(n^{5/2} \log^3 n / \log\log n)$ there, with memory cut by $O(\log^2 q / \log\log q)$.

Concrete parameters, security and the sub-second timing

For the implementation the proof-only constraints ($B_g$ a power of $3$, $Q$ a power of $B_g$) are dropped, requiring only $B_g^{d_g} \ge Q$. Under the central-limit heuristic, refreshed error behaves as a Gaussian of standard deviation

$$\beta = \sqrt{\frac{q^2}{Q^2}\Big(\frac{\varsigma^2 B_r^2}{12} \cdot n d_r \cdot \frac{q}{2} \cdot 2 N d' + \sigma^2 N d_{ks}\Big) + \frac{\lVert s \rVert^2 + 1}{12}},$$

with $d' = B_g - 1 + Q/B_g^{d_g}$ (here $d' = 2.5$ instead of $d_g = 3$). Chosen parameters: LWE $n = 500$, $Q = 2^{32}$, $\sigma = 2^{17}$, $q = 2^9$; Ring-GSW $N = 2^{10}$, $\varsigma = 1.4$; gadget $B_g = 2^{11}$, $d_g = 3$, $u = Q/8 + 1$; bootstrapping key $B_r = 2^3$, $d_r = 2$; key-switching key $B_{ks} = 2^4$, $d_{ks} = 7$. Resulting sizes: bootstrapping key $1032$ MB, key-switching key $314$ MB, and $48{,}000$ FFTs per NAND gate. Final error parameter $\beta = 6.94$, giving per-NAND error probability $p = 1 - \mathrm{erf}(r/\sqrt{2}) \le 2^{-31}$ with $r = (q/8)/(\sqrt{2}\,\beta)$; applying HomNAND before the switches lowers this to $2^{-45}$.

Security: $\delta\text{-binLWE}(n, Q, \sigma, 2^{-64}) = 1.0064$ and $\delta\text{-LWE}(N, Q, \varsigma, 2^{-64}) = 1.0064$, corresponding (per BKZ 2.0 estimates) to block size $> 190$ and $> 2^{100}$ enumeration nodes per call. Binary-secret LWE security accounts for the modulus-switching attack via $\delta\text{-binLWE}(n,q,\sigma,\epsilon) = \min_{q' \le q} \delta\text{-LWE}\big(n, q', \sqrt{(q'/q)^2 \sigma^2 + n/24},\ \epsilon\big)$.

Implementation uses double-precision complex FFT (FFTW, primitive $2N$-th root $\omega = \exp(2\pi \imath / 2N)$) rather than NTT, with $Q$ a power of two so reduction mod $Q$ is free; a negacyclic FFT of rank $N$ is read off the odd indices of a rank-$2N$ real-data FFT, hitting about $6$ Gflops and $10$ microseconds per FFT at $2N = 2048$. The product magnitude $S = B_g Q \sqrt{N}/4 = 2^{46}$ sits just within double precision; small floating-point errors fold into the accumulator noise. Result: one HomNAND plus one Refresh runs in $0.69$ seconds on a single $3$ GHz $64$-bit core using $2.2$ GB of memory, within an order of magnitude of HElib's amortized per-bit bootstrapping cost, in roughly $600$ lines of C++ (FHEW, released on GitHub).

Extensions and conclusions

The technique is not limited to NAND. Working mod $4$, a majority gate on $x_1, x_2, x_3$ is the msb test on the shifted sum $(x_1 + x_2 + x_3) - 0.5$. Larger moduli $t$ handle arbitrary weighted threshold gates $\sum_i w_i x_i > h$ via arithmetic modulo $t > 2\sum_i |w_i|$. Replacing msbExtract with a membership test for any anti-symmetric set $S \subset \mathbb{Z}_t$ (i.e. $x \in S \iff x + t/2 \notin S$) generalizes further: with $t = 6$, an arbitrary-fan-in XOR $x_1 \oplus \dots \oplus x_k$ is one Refresh via the test $(x_1 + \dots + x_k \bmod 6) \in \{1, 3, 5\}$, giving xor-for-almost-free. Since the membership test is cheap relative to the rest of Refresh, multi-output gates (e.g. a $3$-input, $2$-output add-with-carry at $t = 6$ in a single shot, instead of $5$ binary gates) cost little extra. Taking weighted combinations $\sum_i 2^i x_i$ and testing subsets of $\mathbb{Z}_{2^{k+2}}$ can in principle realize any boolean gate, though cost grows exponentially in fan-in $k$.

Open problems left by the authors: combining this fast sub-second bootstrapping with techniques that do more complex computation between bootstraps, fully exploiting the ring-LWE message space inside the accumulator, and merging with the CRT techniques of Alperin-Sheriff and Peikert. The scheme still needs a circular-security assumption to publish a compact evaluation key.
