Optical rotatory dispersion in chiral media provides a mechanism for spectral filtering, but classical monochromators face fundamental bandwidth limitations. This work bridges macroscopic optical filtering with quantum electrodynamic limits by using computational chemistry methods (TD-DFT and Boltzmann averaging) to calculate ORD curves for saccharides, identifying theoretical minimum bandwidths tied to the Cotton effect near molecular absorption bands. Classical experiments demonstrated bandwidth limitations of approximately 20 nanometers, while first-principles calculations predicted potentially achievable sub-nanometer performance using visible-absorbing chiral molecules. The authors propose a single-photon QED architecture operating at ultra-low temperatures (approximately 10 millikelvin) where spectral purity would be constrained solely by molecular excited state natural lifetime, establishing ultimate theoretical criteria for chiral optical filters. This work pioneers quantum monochromator concepts that could enable ultrasensitive applications requiring extreme spectral selectivity.
